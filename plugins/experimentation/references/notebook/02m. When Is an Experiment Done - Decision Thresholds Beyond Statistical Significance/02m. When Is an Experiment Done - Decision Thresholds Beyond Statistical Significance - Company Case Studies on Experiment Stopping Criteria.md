# Company Case Studies - Experiment Stopping Criteria

## Netflix: Meta-Analysis and Optimal Stopping (Jan 2019)
Authors: Gang Su & Ian Yohai

### Business Context
- 139+ million global members
- Customer acquisition focus: signup process optimization
- Millions of daily visitors with state-of-the-art A/B testing infrastructure
- Still desire larger samples to test more innovative ideas

### Benefits of Early Stopping
1. Run more tests in same timeframe → higher chance of finding better experiences
2. Rapidly test waters to identify best investment areas
3. Bring improvements to customers sooner when sizable effects detected

### Risks of Short Experiments
1. **Seasonal fluctuations**: Time of day, day of week, week over week variations
2. **Novelty effects**: Need time to identify if effects diminish
3. **Delayed manifestation**: Some treatment effects take longer to appear
4. **Non-representative audiences**: Holidays/special events (e.g., new title launches)
5. **Improper early calling**: HARKing or p-hacking substantially inflates false positives

### Two-Part Framework

**Part 1: Meta-Analysis for Seasonality Detection**

Purpose: Identify which experiments have time-invariant treatment effects

**Method:**
- Analyze experiments across time dimensions (time of day, day of week, week over week, pre/post-event)
- Fixed-effect model: Consistent outcomes across time → can pool data for more confident estimate
- Random-effect model: Inconsistent outcomes → acknowledges heterogeneity with wider confidence intervals

**Example application:**
- "Continue" button aesthetics: Monday vs Friday visitors unlikely to differ → time-invariant
- Background image for new TV series: More compelling during launch when awareness high → time-variant

**Retrospective study findings:**
- Most signup flow tests do NOT demonstrate strong heterogeneous treatment effects over time
- Could have ended many tests early and innovated faster

**Part 2: Group Sequential Testing (GST) for Optimal Stopping**

**Why GST chosen:**
- Compared SPRT, Sequential Triangular Testing, and GST
- GST demonstrated best performance and practical value
- Perfect fit: samples accumulated over time in batches (like clinical trials)

**GST Implementation:**
1. Before test starts: Decide minimum running time and number of interim analyses
2. Allocate total Type I error (e.g., 0.05) across all interim analyses
3. Each interim test becomes more conservative than regular peeking
4. Stop immediately when statistically significant

**When GST works best:**
- Quick test of ideas where exact magnitude less critical
- Need to end test prematurely due to severe negative impact
- Observed effect size substantially larger than expected

**Trade-offs:**
- Slightly wider confidence intervals
- Small inflation of treatment effects
- Very accurate point estimates obtained much sooner

### Platform Integration
Actively developing into Netflix Experimentation and Causal Inference platform:
- Meta-analysis
- Optimal stopping
- Heterogeneous treatment effects detection
- Goal: Accelerate experimentation workflow and expedite product innovation

## Airbnb: Complex Marketplace Experimentation (May 2014)
Author: Jan Overgoor

### Business Complexity
**Two-sided marketplace challenges:**
- Users browse when not logged in/signed up
- Device switching (web ↔ mobile) mid-booking
- Bookings take days to confirm (need to wait for results)
- Success depends on inventory availability and host responsiveness (out of control)

**Complex booking flow (4 steps):**
1. Visitor makes search
2. Searcher contacts host about listing
3. Host accepts inquiry
4. Guest books the place

**Alternative flows:**
- Instant book (skip contact step)
- Booking request (straight to booking)

**Main metric:** Overall conversion rate from searching to booking

### The Peeking Problem: Real Example

**Experiment:** Changed max price filter from $300 to $1000

**What happened:**
- Day 7: p-value hit 0.05, effect size 4% → appeared significant
- If stopped at day 7: Would have concluded strong positive effect
- Kept running: Experiment ended neutral, effect size practically null
- Final result: p-value indicated remaining effect was noise

**Why early significance misleading:**
1. **Early converters bias**: Users who book quickly have disproportionate influence early
2. **Sample size paradox**: Even small samples massive by classical statistics standards
3. **Natural variation**: Large early effect size through chance → p<0.05 early
4. **Multiple testing**: Computing p-value repeatedly increases likelihood of finding false effect

**Actual outcome:** Launched increased max price filter despite neutral result because certain users wanted high-end search capability and no metric dip observed

### Airbnb's Decision Framework

**Best practice for minimum duration:**
1. Determine minimum effect size you care about (before starting)
2. Compute required duration based on:
   - Sample size (daily new samples)
   - Desired certainty level
3. Set time in advance to minimize false positives

**Challenge:** Often don't know effect size or even direction in advance
- Could be hugely successful → losing profits by not launching sooner
- Could introduce bug → better to stop early before alienating more users

**Heuristic for stability:**
Inspect metric development over time rather than single p-value result. When "significance" first reached, does graph look converged?

### Dynamic P-Value Threshold

**Principle:** Be more skeptical of early results

**Implementation:**
- Ran simulations of ecosystem with various parameters
- Simulated varying: real effect size, variance, certainty levels
- Derived dynamic (time-based) p-value threshold
- Very low threshold at beginning
- Threshold increases as more data accumulates
- Likelihood of false positive much lower later

**Outputs from simulation:**
- False positive/negative rates
- Estimated effect size accuracy for true positives
- Custom decision boundary curve for their system

**Important note:** Curve very particular to Airbnb's system and parameters. Not generalizable without own analysis.

### Context-Based Evaluation

**Single metric focus:** Good practice to prevent cherry-picking significant results

**But also need context:** Looking only at single metric loses understanding

**Airbnb approach:** Evaluate primary metric but examine full context including:
- Breakdown by booking flow steps
- Multiple related metrics
- Coherency across funnel stages

## Comparative Analysis

### Organizational Maturity Levels

**Netflix (High Maturity):**
- Formal two-part framework (meta-analysis + GST)
- Retrospective validation studies
- Platform-integrated decision support
- Science-centric approach
- Pre-specified interim analysis plans

**Airbnb (Pragmatic Maturity):**
- Custom simulation-based thresholds
- Dynamic decision boundaries
- Context-aware evaluation
- Acknowledges business complexity
- Balances rigor with practical needs

### Decision Criteria Comparison

| Aspect | Netflix | Airbnb |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Primary method | Group Sequential Testing | Dynamic p-value thresholds |
| Seasonality check | Meta-analysis (formal) | Implicit in longer durations |
| Early stopping | When GST significant | When dynamic threshold crossed |
| Validation | Retrospective studies | Simulation-based |
| Platform integration | Full automation planned | Custom framework |

### Common Themes

**Both organizations:**
1. Recognize naive peeking inflates false positives
2. Use simulation to validate stopping rules
3. Balance speed of innovation with statistical rigor
4. Consider business context beyond pure statistics
5. Invest in custom frameworks for their specific needs
6. Document and share learnings with broader community

**Key difference:**
- Netflix: More formal statistical framework (GST + meta-analysis)
- Airbnb: More empirical/simulation-driven approach (custom thresholds)

### Practical Takeaways

**When to invest in early stopping:**
1. High experiment volume (want to test more ideas)
2. Opportunity cost of delays (missing improvements)
3. Risk of severe negative impacts (need fast detection)
4. Mature experimentation program (can validate approaches)

**Prerequisites:**
1. Ability to run retrospective analyses
2. Simulation capability for your ecosystem
3. Understanding of your metric behavior over time
4. Clear definition of acceptable error rates

**Warning signs to watch:**
1. Early significance that doesn't look converged
2. Effect size changes substantially over time
3. Incoherent results across related metrics
4. Patterns inconsistent with prior experiments
