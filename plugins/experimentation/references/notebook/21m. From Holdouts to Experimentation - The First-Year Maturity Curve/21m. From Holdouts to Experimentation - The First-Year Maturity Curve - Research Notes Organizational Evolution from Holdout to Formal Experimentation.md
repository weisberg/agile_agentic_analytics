# Research Notes: Organizational Evolution from Holdout to Formal Experimentation

## Source 1: Conversion Maturity Model

**URL:** https://conversion.com/blog/conversion-maturity-model/

### Key Findings:

**Five Stages of Maturity:**
1. **Reactive** - Ad hoc, sporadic testing by individuals, no strategic direction, no leadership support, no clear goals/KPIs, no ROI tracking, minimal research, no documentation/sharing, "spaghetti testing"
2. **Emerging** - More regular experiments, gaining traction in specific teams, champions present but no official buy-in, backlog forming, KPIs being assessed, ROI showing for some experiments, research sporadic
3. **Strategic** - Senior leadership buy-in, dedicated team, established frameworks, defined success metrics, experiments align with business objectives, regular research, integrated tools, standardized processes
4. **Integrated** - Company-wide experimentation, shared roadmap, cross-functional collaboration, KPIs tracked and shared business-wide, constant research loop, rigorous documentation, automated prioritization
5. **Optimized** - Experimentation fundamental to business (Amazon, Netflix, Booking.com), test-and-learn culture at all levels, AI/automation, custom tools, continuous refinement

**Four Maturity Areas (dimensions):**
1. Experiment Goals - What you're trying to achieve
2. Delivery and Process - How efficiently you run experiments
3. Strategy and Culture - Leadership buy-in, adoption, test-and-learn mindset
4. Data and Tools - Research loops and technical infrastructure

**Key Insight:** Organizations rarely mature evenly across all dimensions - might be Strategic in goals but Emerging in tools.

**Context:** Developed by Conversion agency after working with clients ranging from complete newbies to most mature organizations. Built on previous PACET model (Process, Accountability, Culture, Expertise, Technology).


## Source 2: Koalatative - 3 Most Common Experimentation Maturity Buckets

**URL:** https://koalatative.com/blog/the-3-most-common-experimentation-maturity-buckets/
**Author:** Gerda Vogt-Thomas (experienced practitioner)

### Three Maturity Buckets:

**1. Beginners:**
- Learning to set up first A/B test, reveals tech debt and lack of systems/processes
- No clear owner or vision for the web, different marketers competing over site real estate
- "Internal CRO Champion" (usually mid-level marketer) believes in cause but lacks authority to push forward
- Best case: CRO champion is one of the founders
- Extremely long fulfillment process for implementing anything
- Lack of buy-in from upper management - experimentation seen as cost/inconvenience
- Need to build all systems for customer research from scratch
- Often marketing/web reports to sales
- Analytics is 95% tracking errors, 5% user behavior

**2. Intermediate Group:**
- Testing tool installed, running test or two occasionally
- Tests fueled by internal heuristics and personal opinions rather than customer data
- Tests run on "bad stats" - lack of understanding of experiment statistics
  - Don't know when to stop, MDE calculations, sample sizes off, significance vs p-values misunderstood, tracking not set up correctly
- Session-based analysis instead of user-based
- Using visual editors of testing tools to "build" tests
- Relying on testing tool numbers to analyze tests
- Analytics is 70% tracking errors, 30% user behavior

**3. Advanced Pros:**
- Dedicated team with autonomy running experiments consistently every month
- Most ideas backed by clear customer research findings
- Clear test approval and prioritization framework
- Test data sent to data warehouse, segment analysis performed consistently
- Test reports shared across organization, leadership buy-in, shared ideas across departments
- User-based analysis instead of session-based
- Experiments coded by dedicated A/B test developers
- Clear process for getting tests through design, development, QA
- Good understanding of basic stats but needs help with deeper analysis (e.g., "flat" tests)
- Struggles: getting test velocity up, keeping internal team trained
- Main struggle: internal politics and interdepartmental gatekeeping of information
- Most analytics setup reliable but needs maintenance

### Key Insights:
- **Hardest bucket for external consultants:** Beginners (need internal process/growing pain before outside help works)
- **Most to gain quickly:** Intermediates (have building blocks, traffic, just need direction)
- **Intermediate phase focus:** Infrastructure, team, skill for consistent research and maintaining testing program - not yet strict on statistics
- **When to focus on velocity:** After running 2-5 tests/month for several months with strong processes
- Culture starts from top down - if leadership sees experimentation as nuisance, consultants can't help much


## Source 3: Statsig - Holdout Testing for Validating Product Changes

**URL:** https://www.statsig.com/perspectives/holdout-testing-the-key-to-validating-product-changes

### Key Concepts:

**What is Holdout Testing:**
- Technique where you deliberately keep a small group of users from seeing latest updates
- Compare their behavior/metrics against those who got new changes
- Focuses on long-term effects (days, weeks, months) vs A/B testing's immediate reactions
- Key for spotting negative side effects or user fatigue that appear later
- Tracks engagement, retention, revenue over longer period

**How to Set Up:**
- Create experiment with three groups: control, test, and holdout
- Control group: no changes
- Test group: gets new updates
- Holdout group: kept completely away from changes
- Common split: 45% control, 45% test, 10% holdout
- Use feature flags to isolate holdout group from changes
- Keep user segmentation consistent to preserve test integrity
- Use stratified sampling to ensure holdout group represents overall user base

**Analysis:**
- Monitor key metrics over time (engagement, retention, revenue)
- Compare behaviors between holdout and exposed groups
- Effects might be gradual or delayed
- Use Monte Carlo simulations to estimate impacts when results aren't statistically significant
- Requires cross-team collaboration (data scientists, product managers, engineers)

**Integration into Development:**
- Run alongside other experiments in agile methodologies
- Use results as inputs for sprint planning
- Rotate users in holdout groups regularly to prevent user frustration
- Incorporate into continuous delivery pipeline
- Regular communication of results to stakeholders builds trust


## Source 4: Eppo - Holdouts: Measuring Experiment Impact Accurately

**URL:** https://www.geteppo.com/blog/holdouts-measuring-experiment-impact-accurately
**Author:** Giorgio Martini (PhD Economics, former ML & Economics Manager at LinkedIn)

### Critical Insight on Maturity:

"Holdouts help measure the cumulative, long-term impact of an experimentation program. But getting value out of holdouts requires scale and maturity that not all organizations are ready for."

### Why Cumulative Impact is Lower Than Sum of Individual Experiments:

**Winner's Curse** - Most powerful force:
- A/B tests give unbiased estimates, but when restricted to winning variants (those actually shipped), this property no longer holds
- Some variants were actually good, some just got lucky - we don't know which
- Underpowered experiments especially susceptible - positive outcomes disproportionately due to luck
- The more underpowered, the more inflated the estimate

**Other Forces:**
- Novelty effects - short-run results overestimate long-run impact
- Interactions between experiments (rare) - tend to partially cancel out effects
- P-hacking - finding subpopulations, running longer, changing winsorization thresholds

**Key Finding:** "Cumulative impact is lower – sometimes far lower – than the bottom-up approach of adding individual impacts"

### Use Cases for Holdouts:

1. **Measure aggregate impact** of entire teams/organizations over time
2. **Understand long-term effects** when short-term experiments show novelty effects
3. **Account for interactions** (though interactions are usually rare and tiny - Microsoft research shows this)

### Hard Requirements for Holdouts:

**Sample Size:**
- If struggling to get power for regular A/B tests with 50%/50% split, not ready for holdouts
- Don't want to hold back too many users from improvements
- Need power analysis to determine if 5% holdout gives enough sample size
- 1% holdouts unlikely to be powered unless you have Facebook's scale - better to skip entirely

**Stable Product Experience:**
- Must keep feature flags that would otherwise be fully rolled out and cleaned up
- Creates tech debt, developer friction, dependencies, incompatibilities
- Overhead from maintaining old features, separate documentation, training support staff

### When NOT to Use Holdouts:

- Non-persistent identifiers (guests, logged-out users with session IDs/cookies)
- ML models where training data becomes stale
- Individual experiments with strong novelty concerns (hold individual experiment longer instead)
- Interference effects like marketplace cannibalization or network effects

### Interpreting Results:

- When holdout impact is 80% of sum of individual impacts = cause for celebration (don't expect 100%)
- When holdout impact is 20% of sum = something amiss in experimentation process
- Check: Are tests run without power analysis? Short time despite novelty effects? High false discovery rate? Interventions restricted to segments where they performed well?

### Long-term Value:

"The true value of holdouts comes out in the long run. They help build trust that experimentation best practices are being followed."


## Source 5: Alex Birkett - 10 Ways to Wreck an Experimentation Program

**URL:** https://www.alexbirkett.com/experimentation-program-mistakes/

### 10 Common Mistakes That Kill Programs:

**1. "Quick Wins" Forever**
- Quick wins don't last forever - run out fast unless website is "total shit"
- Opportunity cost for more complex, higher impact projects
- Set poor expectations for value of experimentation
- Win rate goes from 90% to 20% after obvious stuff is fixed
- Must start with strong understanding of value and eye for long term
- Experimentation is portfolio allocation - some quick wins, some bigger projects

**2. Rely on Industry Conversion Rate Benchmarks**
- Conversion rate is contextual - competitor rates are completely meaningless
- Only true answer: "good conversion rate is better than what you had last month"
- You're running your own race, you are your own benchmark
- Caring about industry rates is opportunity cost and diversion

**3. Staff Crucial Roles with Mercenaries and Interns**
- Don't hire experimentation person too early - need foundations first:
  - Traffic adequate for running experiments
  - Data infrastructure for tracking
  - Tech stack capable of running experiments
  - Design and development to run worthwhile tests
- Without these, expect disappointment and burnout

**4. Document Nothing**
- Without documentation, repeat tests from years ago with no memory
- Writing experiment doc in advance helps plan test
- Storing results helps cement and communicate learnings
- If not learning from tests (wins, losses, inconclusives), not really testing
- Critical for scaling - new hires need to know what was tested

**5. Goal (Only) On CVR Increases and Winning Tests**
- CVR increases: non-stationary data, fluctuates by season, affected by external factors (press coverage brings low-converting traffic)
- Winning tests: incentive problem - teams test "safer" items or cherry pick data
- After adequate optimization, need riskier tests that might lose big
- Better metrics: test velocity, test quality, number of tests run, win rate, win per test

**6. Ignore Experiment Design and Statistics**
- "A bad test is worse than no test"
- Bad test has none of certainty of proper statistics but all confidence of "data-driven decision"
- Invest in data literacy: hire data scientists, pay for training, create processes/guardrails

**7. Don't Question or Invest in Data Infrastructure**
- Never seen perfect Google Analytics setup
- Can spend up to 50% of time on data infrastructure
- If can't measure it, can't experiment on it
- If data not trustworthy, experiments won't be trustworthy
- "Trust, but verify"

**8. Change Strategy Frequently**
- Experimentation is scaffolding to support cogent business strategy
- If strategy constantly changing, team never has runway for longer projects or to accumulate incremental wins
- Experimentation doesn't replace need for strategy and vision

**9. Cherry Pick Results (HARKing)**
- "HARKing" = Hypothesizing After Results are Known
- "Texas sharpshooter fallacy" - painting bullseye after shots landed
- Two reasons: ignorance and incentives
- Culture of "success theater" promotes only winning campaigns
- Create culture where failure is expected but you learn from it

**10. Keep Tight Grip on Experimentation**
- Worst thing: dictate entirely from top down
- Takes away autonomy and mastery, demotivates team, bottlenecks ideas
- Path programs follow: Decentralized > Centralized > Center of Excellence
  - Decentralized: individuals run experiments with no oversight
  - Centralized: specialized team owns all efforts, eliminates errors but caps value
  - Center of Excellence: specialist team owns tools/processes/culture, enables others to run experiments autonomously
- Best programs (Microsoft, Booking.com, Netflix, Shopify) operate as Center of Excellence
- "CRO is an operating system" - methodology by which any team can make better decisions

### To Build and Maintain Program Need:
- Highly motivated individuals
- Executive buy-in and understanding
- Sufficient traffic
- Sufficient budget

## Source 7: Convert - How to Get Buy-In for Experimentation

**URL:** https://www.convert.com/blog/a-b-testing/how-to-build-buy-in-for-experimentation/
**Author:** Alex Birkett (HubSpot, Workato experience)

### 13 Tips for Building Buy-In:

**1. Have an executive sponsor**
- Reality: not going to change minds from bottom up in company of sufficient size
- Need influential executive support
- Pick senior manager who: takes data-driven approach, loves to innovate/experiment, has budget
- Low maturity organizations lack senior sponsorship
- Leaders must: understand value, align on KPIs/goals, create psychological safety for failure

**2. Make strong business case**
- Build simple growth model: input metrics, output metrics, baselines, historical trends
- Calculate business improvement with minimum/reasonable/exciting assumptions
- Show quantitative forecasting - learning doesn't pay bills
- Come prepared with case studies, hard data, gaps/opportunities, potential ROI

**3. Crawl, walk, run**
- Don't over promise early
- Don't try to democratize experimentation before knowing how to run basic A/B test
- Speed phase can create bad habits - high-velocity, low-quality mode
- Start slow, solve biggest current problem, then find next one

**4. Show your work and celebrate wins**
- Weekly experimentation recap email to all stakeholders
- Celebrate every win, especially collaborative efforts
- Slack channel with automated notifications for launches/conclusions
- Weekly experimentation meeting for results/current experiments/backlog
- Constantly evangelize the work

**5. Skip the nerd speak**
- Leaders care about innovation, moving needle, growth - not Bayesian vs Frequentist
- Always have executive summary, lots of visuals, clear "so what?"
- Save technical details for 1:1s with analysts

**6. Educate stakeholders**
- Mixture of passive (Wiki with documentation, recommended courses) and active (1:1s with allies) education
- Only 9% of businesses have organization-wide education program
- Much more likely that mature organizations have one

**7. Make sure data is trustworthy**
- If data is bad, experiments will be bad
- Never seen perfect Google Analytics setup
- Can spend up to 50% of time on data infrastructure

**8. Involve stakeholders in process**
- HiPPO is reality - keep percentage of portfolio open for their ideas
- Ikea Effect: people value things they help build
- Invite to workshops, brainstorming, meetings

**9. Incorporate other teams**
- Mature organizations have mixture of centralized, decentralized, center of excellence models
- Brings more diverse/creative experiment ideas
- Allows experimentation to scale

**10. Have a process**
- Documentation, workflows, rituals show you take experimentation seriously
- Easier to onboard people

**11. Trigger FOMO**
- Share wins from other teams/departments
- People want to be part of success

**12. Avoid success theater**
- Don't cherry pick metrics or only report wins
- Create culture where failure is expected but you learn from it

**13. Make experimentation cheap and easy**
- Remove friction
- Invest in tools and infrastructure

## Source 9: Martijn Scheijbeler - 20 Reasons Why Most Experiment Programs Fail

**URL:** https://martijnscheijbeler.com/20-reasons-why-most-experiment-programs-are-failing/
**Published:** November 7, 2017
**Experience:** 200+ experiments

### 20 Common Failures in Experimentation Programs:

**Ideas Stage:**
1. **They just launch, they just test** - Not experimenting but putting features live without testing impact
2. **Believe they're wasting money** - Think losing experiments kill bottom line (but gut decisions would cost more)
3. **Expect big wins** - Early stage gets low-hanging fruit, but won't achieve big results all the time
4. **"My competitor is doing X"** - Competitor probably has no clue either, focus on your own customers
5. **Running tests without enough traffic** - If can't run ~20 experiments yearly, might be better to focus elsewhere

**Hypothesis Stage:**
6. **Don't create hypothesis** - Can't verify success without documented hypothesis
7. **Testing multiple variables at once** - Hard to figure out what led to change
8. **Use gut feeling not numbers** - Use data as basis for experiments, not gut feeling

**Design & Engineering:**
9. **Before/After not A/B test** - Just launching and seeing impact is dangerous, can't isolate impact
10. **71616 design revisions** - Wasting time trying to find perfect design, that's why you're testing

**Quality Assurance:**
11. **Don't QA tests** - Every broken test wastes testing days

**Run & Analysis:**
12. **Not running long enough** - Calling results too early, need to account for business cycles
13. **Running multiple overlapping tests** - Creates unintentional multivariate test
14. **Data not sent to main analytics tool** - Discrepancies between A/B tool and analytics
15. **Going with results without significance** - 75% significance means 25% of time you can't trust results
16. **Running tests too long** - More than 4 weeks problematic due to cookie deletion, changing context

**Repeat:**
17. **Not deploying winners fast enough** - Takes 2 months to launch
18. **Not keeping track of tests** - No documentation means can't reference past learnings
19. **Not sharing learnings** - Other teams might implement clear losers
20. **Not iterating on losers** - Can learn from failures too

### Key Insights:
- Early stage programs pick low-hanging fruit easily, gets harder over time (local maximum)
- Experimentation is about reducing uncertainty, not guaranteeing wins
- Documentation critical for organizational learning and scaling


## Source 10: Statsig - Experimentation Maturity: Ad Hoc to Embedded

**URL:** https://www.statsig.com/perspectives/experiment-maturity-adhoc-embedded
**Published:** June 23, 2025

### The Problem with Ad Hoc Experimentation:
"Most companies run experiments like they're planning a surprise party - lots of excitement, minimal coordination, and everyone hopes it works out."

Pattern: Someone has bright idea, throws together test, then crickets when it comes to using results.

### What Embedded Experimentation Looks Like:
- Every feature ships with test plan
- Teams have clear metrics they're optimizing for
- Results feed directly into next sprint
- Nobody asks "should we test this?" because answer is always yes

### Getting to Embedded Requires:
"Fundamentally rewire how organization thinks about building products"
- Accept brilliant ideas might be wrong
- Data beats opinions
- Small iterations often beat big bets

### Strategies to Embed Experimentation:

**Start Small:**
- Pick one team already curious about testing
- Make them experimentation champions
- Give them best tools and most support
- Let them show everyone else what's possible

**Infrastructure Investment (Non-Negotiable):**
- Need platform that plays nice with existing stack
- Can pull from data warehouse, push to analytics tools
- Doesn't require PhD to operate

**Cultural Playbook:**
1. Make experiment results public - share wins AND losses
2. Build peer review into process (nothing ships without test plan review)
3. Create regular experiment review meeting where teams share learnings
4. Reward velocity of learning, not just positive results

**Embed Everywhere:**
- Product reviews start with "what did we learn from last tests?"
- Sprint planning includes experiment design
- Roadmaps have built-in time for iteration based on results

**Create Experimentation Toolkit:**
- Document best practices
- Create templates for common test types
- Build library of past experiments
- Make it easier to run good experiment than bad one

### Key Insight:
"You're not trying to turn everyone into statisticians. You're trying to create environment where testing is default, where 'I think' gets replaced with 'the data shows,' and where failures are just inputs for next experiment."

### The Gap is Widening:
"Gap between organizations that excel at experimentation and those that dabble is only getting wider. In world where user preferences change weekly and competition is always one feature away, ability to test, learn, and iterate quickly isn't just nice to have - it's survival."

## Source 12: Amanda Natividad - How to Scale Small Win Into Big Bet

**URL:** https://amandanat.substack.com/p/how-to-scale-a-small-win-into-a-big
**Published:** May 2024

### Core Philosophy:
"When small experiment works, obvious question is: 'Should I go bigger now?' Answer: Maybe. But not blindly."

Scaling small win should still be controlled, measured move—not emotional "it worked once, so let's 10x it!" reaction.

### Before Scaling, Pressure-Test Results:
- Was win consistent across different channels, audiences, or times?
- Could result be fluke (seasonality, lucky viral hit, timing)?
- Did learning match question you set out to answer?

If yes—green light to expand. If no—consider another small round of testing first.

### Scale One Step at a Time:
Scaling doesn't mean going from $100 → $10,000 overnight. Could mean:
- Increasing ad spend by 2x, not 10x
- Extending content format to second channel
- Expanding winning CTA across multiple landing pages

Small expansions let you keep learning without risking too much too fast.

### Find Highest Upside:
Ask: "If I double down here, where's highest upside?"

Examples:
- If email format drives higher clickthroughs, expand style to next product launch
- If LinkedIn post format drives more engagement, repurpose into carousels, threads, short videos
- If micro-influencer partnership converts, build small program with similar creators

### Not Everything Needs Scaling:
Scale what connects to real goals (more leads, better brand affinity, higher conversion rates).

Scaling should feel **deliberate**, not **reactive**.

Goal isn't just "growth"—it's **compounded learning and bigger wins**.
