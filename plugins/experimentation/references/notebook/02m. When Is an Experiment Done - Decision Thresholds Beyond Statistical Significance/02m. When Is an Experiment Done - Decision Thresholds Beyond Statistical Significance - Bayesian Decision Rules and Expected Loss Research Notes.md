# Bayesian Decision Rules and Expected Loss Research Notes

## Source 1: Statsig - Bayesian A/B Testing (Oct 2025)

### Core Value Proposition
Bayesian methods allow making decisions as data comes in, with explicit risk quantification. Product teams cannot always wait for fixed sample sizes.

### Key Decision Metrics

**1. Probability of Improvement: P(B > A)**
- Direct probability statement (e.g., "92% chance variant B beats A")
- More intuitive for stakeholders than p-values
- Enables mid-flight decision making

**2. Expected Loss**
- Quantifies downside of wrong decision
- Defines cost of shipping a loser OR holding back a winner
- Used to optimize decisions when multiple outcomes matter

### Practical Decision Framework

**Setup Requirements:**
1. **Empirical priors from history**: If conversion rates cluster 4-6%, encode that belief
2. **Express risk as expected loss**: Define downside scenarios
3. **Precommit stop rules**: Set check cadence, probability thresholds, minimum exposure
4. **Validate via simulation**: Confirm false positive rates match intentions

**Decision Thresholds:**
- Typical threshold: ~90% probability to be best
- Must validate false positive/negative rates through simulation
- Thresholds should align with business risk tolerance

### Where Bayesian Pays Off
1. **Low traffic/niche funnels**: Shrink variance, avoid month-long waits
2. **Real-time product ramps**: Sequential evidence with guardrails
3. **Complex multi-metric tradeoffs**: Optimize expected loss across outcomes

### Guardrails to Prevent Bias

**Two workable paths:**
1. Frequentist-style alpha spending or group sequential design to bound peeks
2. Bayesian thresholds on expected loss/probability of uplift with pre-declared check times

**Bias protection measures:**
- Run posterior predictive checks for model misspecification
- Simulate error rates across traffic bands and seasonality
- Track peek behavior and decision loss in logs
- Calibrate priors with holdouts or cross-validation

**Prior selection cautions:**
- Overconfident priors can drown the data
- Start with weakly-informative priors
- Use empirical Bayes where history exists
- Document prior sources transparently

### Communication Best Practices
- Prefer "probability of uplift" and "expected loss" over p-values
- Set clear risk caps and share in dashboard
- Show how prior and likelihood combine
- Include side-by-side posterior vs naive estimates
- Call out peeks and early stops explicitly
- Clarify that credible intervals ≠ p-values

## Source 2: Towards Data Science - Expected Loss Analysis (Apr 2021)
Author: Iqbal Ali (former Head of Optimisation at Trainline)

### The Dual View: Risk + Reward

**Traditional view (incomplete):** Only shows reward (conversion rates over time)
**Complete view:** Shows both reward AND risk (expected loss)

### Expected Loss Definition
Developed by Chris Stucchio for VWO. Presents the risk of choosing one variant over another.

**Interpretation:**
- Lower expected loss = better choice
- Expressed as percentage cost
- Financial impact depends on traffic volume and segment

### Practical Example

**Scenario:** De-risking important feature release
- Win: desirable but not essential
- Flat: acceptable
- Loss: want to avoid

**Results:**
- Bayesian probability: 89.9% (just below 90% threshold)
- Control slightly higher conversion rate (stable for 3 weeks)
- Expected loss of choosing variant over control: 0.75%

**Decision:** Business must weigh urgency of feature rollout against 0.75% risk

### Calculation Method

**Process:**
1. Generate random samples from beta distribution for each variant
2. Compare samples pairwise (variation vs control)
3. Calculate differences: `variation_sample - control_sample`
4. Baseline negative results to 0 (expected loss never negative)
5. Return mean of differences as percentage

**For Control Group Expected Loss:**
- Take max(variation - control, 0) for each sample pair
- Average across all samples
- Express as percentage

**For Variation Group Expected Loss:**
- Take max(control - variation, 0) for each sample pair
- Average across all samples
- Express as percentage

**Sample size:** Typically 10,000-100,000 random samples for accuracy

### Decision Rules Used in Practice

**Three validation criteria:**
1. **Seven days without expected loss lines crossing**
2. **Consistency of expected loss lines** (stable trends)
3. **Probability of best ≥ 90%**

**Key insight:** Can make decision earlier than traditional methods if risk view is stable (e.g., week 3 instead of week 4+)

### Benefits of Expected Loss View

**Reduces experiment runtime:**
- Especially for de-risking experiments
- Makes risk explicit for stakeholders
- Enables earlier decision-making with confidence

**Improves communication:**
- Stakeholders understand risk + reward together
- Makes decision-making easier
- Quantifies business impact in percentage terms

**Example interpretation:**
- "0.75% expected loss might be deal-breaker for large companies with high traffic"
- Financial impact varies by company size and traffic volume

### Technical Implementation

**Libraries used:**
- numpy (random sampling)
- scipy.stats.beta (beta distribution)
- functools.reduce (aggregation)

**Key statistical concepts:**
- Beta distribution for conversion rate sampling
- Central limit theorem (normal distribution assumption)
- Standard deviations define probability ranges
- 68.26% of samples fall within ±1 standard deviation

**Data inputs:**
- Visits/users per variant
- Conversions per variant
- Calculate conversion rates
- Generate successes and failures for beta distribution

## Key Practical Insights

### Decision Latency vs Statistical Certainty

**Bayesian advantage:** Can make decisions earlier while maintaining explicit risk control
- Traditional: Wait for fixed sample size
- Bayesian: Monitor continuously with risk quantification

**Trade-off management:**
- Business urgency vs statistical certainty
- Expected loss provides the bridge
- Stakeholders can make informed risk-based decisions

### Organizational Implementation

**Start small approach:**
1. Pick one primary metric
2. Define guardrails up front
3. Document minimum worthwhile effect
4. Set weakly-informative prior with source
5. Treat interim checks as sequential tests
6. Validate with simulations

**Transparency requirements:**
- Document all assumptions
- Show prior selection rationale
- Report posterior vs naive estimates
- Expose all peeks and early stops
- Validate error rates match intentions

### Risk-Adjusted Thresholds

**Context-dependent:**
- Feature de-risking: May accept higher expected loss if feature critical
- Revenue optimization: Lower expected loss threshold
- User experience: Consider multiple metrics with expected loss

**Threshold calibration:**
- Simulate under null and uplift scenarios
- Verify error rates under various peek schedules
- Tune based on business risk appetite
- Document threshold rationale

**Financial impact consideration:**
- 0.75% loss may be acceptable for small tests
- Same 0.75% may be unacceptable at scale
- Must translate percentage to actual business impact
