# Research Notes: Null and Negative Results in Experimentation

## Key Statistics on Experiment Failure Rates

### Major Tech Companies (Source: Ronny Kohavi, VP at Microsoft and Airbnb)

- **Airbnb**: 92% failure rate (highest observed)
- **Bing**: 85% failure rate
- **Microsoft**: ~66% average failure rate (about two-thirds)
- **Booking.com and Google**: 80-90% failure rate range

### Key Insights from Ronny Kohavi:

1. **Failure rates increase with optimization maturity**: Bing's 85% failure rate was higher than Microsoft's overall 66% because "it's harder to improve something you've been optimizing for a while"
2. **Platform advantage for measurement**: "When you have a platform it's easy to get this number - you look at how many experiments were run and how many of them fail"
3. **Implementation vs. idea failure**: "Usually it's not that the idea is bad but that there is an implementation issue or something we haven't thought about that forces to pivot again"
4. **Multiple iterations**: Teams may need "two or three or four pivots or bug fixes" to ultimately get to a successful launch
5. **Universal humbling experience**: "Every group that starts to run experiments they always start off by thinking that somehow they're different and their success rate is going to be much much higher and they're all humbled"

## Implications for Teams

1. **User behavior is unpredictable**: Even the best teams struggle to predict outcomes
2. **High-stakes betting is risky**: Teams will only be right about 20% of the time
3. **Failure is the norm**: Expect to spend >80% of time on experiments that fail
4. **Speed is crucial**: If >80% will fail, the teams that test fastest will win

## Important Caveat

Using benchmarks from mature companies can be misleading for startups. Mature products are already highly optimized, making small wins harder to achieve compared to early-stage products.

## Execution Components for Handling Failure

Three key components needed:

1. **Goals**: OKRs, WIGs (Wildly Important Goals), Leading Measures
2. **Rituals**: Daily check-ins, weekly syncs, retrospectives
3. **Tools, Frameworks & Data**: Dashboards, progress trackers, hypothesis documents, CRMs

## Hidden Value of Negative Results (Source: Eppo Blog - Bennett Tulloch)

### Why Teams Fear Negative Results

1. **Human nature**: Tendency to equate success with wins and write off losses as failures
2. **Organizational pressure**: All eyes on important experiments creates fear of failure
3. **Risk aversion**: Teams avoid testing risky ideas, focus on "safe bets"
4. **Misconception**: Belief that negative results = wasted effort

### Four Key Values of Negative Results

#### 1. Learning What Doesn't Work

- Provides indisputable proof that a feature/design/strategy wasn't effective
- Creates clarity and helps teams move forward smarter
- Rules out dead ends and narrows focus on better options
- Without red results, teams operate on assumptions rather than evidence

#### 2. Containing Fallout (Risk Mitigation)

- **Example**: Experiment shows 15% drop in AOV, but only exposed to 10% of users
- If launched without testing, would have caused 15% drop across entire user base
- Experiments function as a safety net
- Prevents costly damage that would be harder to reverse
- Avoids launching features that hurt customer experience

#### 3. Building a Culture of Learning

- Removes stigma around negative results
- Shifts focus from winning to learning
- Teams become more comfortable taking big swings and testing bold ideas
- Encourages creativity, innovation, and long-term competitive advantage
- **Key insight**: Negative results show metrics are responsive to changes in that area
- Reveals levers that influence customer behavior (even if pulling wrong direction)
- With adjustments, negative levers can potentially be reversed to positive

#### 4. Accelerating Experimentation Velocity

- Fear of negative results causes teams to pre-filter ideas
- Only launching "confident wins" slows everything down
- Faster testing = faster answers
- Red and neutral results guide better experiments
- Higher velocity = more chances to find breakthroughs

### The 1/3 Distribution Rule

Mature experimentation programs typically see:

- **1/3 positive (green)**: Measurable improvements
- **1/3 neutral (gray)**: No significant impact, but refines approach
- **1/3 negative (red)**: Fail to meet expectations but illuminate blind spots

**Important**: If results skew heavily green, the team is probably not taking enough risks or experimenting ambitiously enough

This aligns with patterns from Google and Netflix: most tests do not yield measurable improvements, but each outcome provides valuable insights.

### Mindset Shift Principles

1. **Red results are not failures** - they're critical steps in learning process
2. **Celebrate learning wins** - every result pushes team closer to understanding
3. **Run more experiments** - more tests = more valuable insights
4. **Highlight cost of not experimenting** - untested negative features are far worse

### Competitive Advantages of Embracing Negative Results

1. Faster at identifying and acting on opportunities
2. More effective innovation rooted in evidence, not assumptions
3. Avoid costly missteps while competitors fly blind

### Key Quote

"The faster your organization becomes comfortable with the reality that experiments fail two-thirds of the time, the quicker you'll unlock the true potential of an experimentation-led culture."

## Learning Repositories and Documentation (Source: Convert.com - Multiple CRO Experts)

### Definition and Purpose

A **learning repository** is a singular location to store all information about past tests:

- Target page
- Hypothesis and implementation details
- Test elements and variants
- Results and impact on metrics

### Key Benefits

#### 1. Building Testing Culture

- Wider business and stakeholders can see experiments and learnings
- More people involved = faster adoption of experimentation mindset
- Sparks new ideas across departments (e.g., paid ads using successful page language)
- Cross-pollination of insights

#### 2. Time Efficiency

- Reduces time spent finding important information
- Eliminates need to repeatedly answer stakeholder queries
- Faster onboarding for new team members

#### 3. Strategic Value

"Learnings are the core principle of building meaningful and effective products. The key to running experience-informed experiments is to have a central repository that is accessible, structured, searchable and properly updated/managed. Doing this in a successful way can have an **exponential effect on the productivity and quality of the overall experimentation program output**." - Matthias Mandiau, CTO of Vanar Blockchain

### Common Mistakes Avoided by Learning Repositories

#### 1. Failing to Respect "Expensive" Company Data

- Tests cost valuable time and money
- Data is company property that should be preserved
- Knowledge shouldn't depend on people (who come and go)
- Enables better knowledge sharing, onboarding, business cases, and research

#### 2. Missing Out on Data-Informed "Gut Instinct"

"When you don't have data (logged learning/artifacts from testing), you have to make decisions based off of either your gut, or the gut instinct of stakeholders. A database of learnings creates a gut for all of us that we can use as a gut check, but one that isn't just our own or biased." - Natalia Contreras-Brown, VP of Product Management at The Bouqs

#### 3. Repeating Tests That Have Been Run Before

- Without tracking, easy to re-run tests already tried
- Or miss test ideas thinking they've been run
- Wastes 30+ days getting data that should have been saved

#### 4. Poor Communication or Lack of Access

- Mature CRO teams excel at quick, easy information access
- Repository cuts communication barriers
- Prevents sidelining workflows while searching for information
- Acts as "data layer" in testing workflow

#### 5. Leaving Learning (and Money) on the Table

- Majority of tests fail - only through iteration do teams improve
- Hard to see progress without tracking
- **40-50% of experiments at H&M and Brainlabs were iterations of previous experiments**

### Real-World Success Stories

#### GoodUI Pattern Analysis

- Ran 51 tests using previous winning design layouts
- Achieved **71% success rate** when using previous winners to design new tests on other sites
- Demonstrates predictive potential of documented learnings

#### Drip Agency Cross-Client Success

- Took winning page layouts from one client
- Tested on another client → immediate lift in conversions

#### GoodUI Iteration Success

- Took winning test ideas from one client
- Used as basis to test other pages on same site
- Result: **42% lift in conversions**

### Repository Structure Models

#### 1. Centre of Excellence (CoE) - RECOMMENDED

- Experimentation team acts as CoE for entire organization
- Functions as concierge for other departments wanting to run experiments
- Oversees integrity and alignment with broad goals
- Easy to coordinate and monitor
- Challenge: Less accessible than decentralized

#### 2. Decentralized Units

- Units sit across departments
- Everyone can run tests regardless of department/experience
- Teams have most know-how of their own growth loops
- Challenge: Tougher to coordinate repository

#### 3. Hybrid Model (Most Popular)

- Repository accessible in cloud
- Setup and management with 1 team/manager (centralized)
- Each product team has stakeholder responsible for uploading learnings (decentralized)
- One person takes overall ownership, but test "owners" add learnings post-completion

### What to Document: Metadata Categories

**Standard Categories:**

1. **Test types**: A/B, MVT, A/B/n
2. **Pages**: Homepage, product page, pricing page, etc.
3. **Touchpoints**: Ads, catalog, blog, emails, app
4. **Audiences**: Segments, behavioral data, goals, visitor source, geolocation
5. **Growth surface areas**: Free trial offers, demos, hooks

**Additional Metadata:**

- Dates
- URL/Regex
- Iteration on previous test (link to prior test)
- Screenshots of variants
- Isolated or not (# of changes)
- Impact % with confidence intervals
- Metric type

**Key Principle**: "Themable and memo'd so it's easily filtered. Succinct for scannable learnings." - David Mannheim

### Communication Best Practices

#### 1. Make It a Habit

- Part of weekly activities
- At least once a week meeting to review hypotheses and results
- Every sprint should contain 1-2 experiments
- Share in learning newsletters

#### 2. Be Humble & Transparent

- Don't present insights with 100% certainty
- Show it's one of best ways to make decisions vs. gut feeling
- **Don't hide tests that lost** - use as opportunity to show double-sided benefit
- Present tests that lost as much as those that won

#### 3. Don't Gate Learning, Involve Everyone

- Not just for marketing/optimization teams
- Get everyone on board
- Have 2-3 experienced people per team to spread influence

#### 4. Make Learning Shareable

- Use tools like Notion, Google Slides, Miro
- Easy to access from any device, anywhere
- Great for remote teams

#### 5. Use Insights to Plan Tests ("Upcycling")

- Old tests can be enriched by new insights
- Design better iterations based on compounded learning
- Person who ran old test "communicates" through repository entry

#### 6. Take Bets

- Add excitement to testing
- Exercise team judgment on how prior tests predict future outcomes
- Creates vested interest in experimentation

### Tools Recommendations

**Specialized Tools:**

- Effective Experiments: Workflows, quality data, program insights, collaboration
- GrowthHackers Tool: Dashboard, hypothesis builder, progress reports, status tracker

**Expert Recommendation for Starting:**\
"Airtable. Quick and easy and free. Otherwise, I like Effective Experiments for bigger programs if they want a tool that bridges the different layers." - Ben Labay, CEO @ Speero

**Key Advice**: Don't need 'perfect' repository from day 1. Just need something to store tests in and use ASAP. Start simple, prove value, then invest in more expensive options.

## Booking.com Experimentation Culture Case Study

### Business Results

- **Conversion rate**: 2-3 times the industry average
- **Growth**: 26.02% revenue YoY
- **Scale**: Over 557 million web visits in one month, most downloaded travel app globally
- **Volume**: Running 1000+ concurrent experiments at any given time

### Core Cultural Principles

#### 1. Tests Aligned to Strategy

All experiments must connect to strategic goals

#### 2. Testing Underpins Culture

**Failure Rate**: 9/10 tests deemed as "failures" (never make it into features)

- **VC Portfolio Approach**: Majority will fail, but 10% that wins will win big enough to pay back failures
- This approach reduces financial and emotional costs of failures
- "When you conduct a large volume of experiments, a low success rate still translates into a significant number of successes" - Stefan Thomke

**Everything Should Be Tested**

- "If it can be a test, it should be a test. If it can't be a test: why do it?"
- Avoid "we test some things but other things we just know" culture
- Everything is an assumption until proven otherwise

**No Preferential Treatment of HiPPOs**\
"We have evidence that having a C in your title doesn't make you more successful in deciding what improves your product" - Stuart Frisby, ex Director of Design

- HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion) must submit to same testing process as everyone else

**Guidelines, Not Rules**

- Anyone can propose a test; management permission not required
- No restrictions on what can be tested or how
- Avoids restrictions on creativity

**Data-Driven Culture with 100% Access to Data**

- Everyone gets access to as much data as possible
- Decisions must be informed by test-gathered data

**Customers Shape the Product**

- Product is result of customer feedback through tests over time
- Not any one person's conception of ideal product
- Can wind up looking like something few people would design

#### 3. Volume of Testing

**Lots of Tests**

- Volume is output of successful testing culture
- Constraining volume increases risk and frequency of failure
- Makes test proposers more risk averse
- "If a company does only a handful of experiments a year, it may have only one success or, if it's unlucky, none. Then failure is a big deal." - Stefan Thomke

**At the Same Time**

- Hundreds, sometimes thousands, of atomic tests running simultaneously
- More variants of Booking.com site exist than humans that have ever lived

**At Scale**

- High-risk tests exposed to many people as fast as possible
- Get read fast on whether positive or detrimental
- "We're going to find out immediately if it's really bad, rather than letting it be really bad for a few people for a really long time" - Stuart Frisby

**Every Part of the Product**

- Track changes throughout user journey
- Test every part of product being maintained
- Cross-functional squads tackle end-to-end user experience

#### 4. Rigor

**Hypotheses > Ideas**

- Ideas are easy, hypotheses create rigor
- Hypotheses require framing concept and proving/disproving it

#### 5. Trust Driven by Enablement

**In-House Testing Platform (since 2002)**

- Strategic choice to develop own experimentation platform
- Control over experiment creation
- Implement right data and metrics
- Much of data output is automated

**Experimentation Department**

- Balance science of experimentation with risk appetite of business
- Add more metrics, guardrails, ability to process more data
- Learn how teams use platform
- Educate and train teams on best practice

**Training and Support**

- Onboarding training on experiments
- On-the-job support from data science
- Senior data scientists provide analytics training

### Prevents Costly Mistakes

**Safety Net Function**

- All shipped code wrapped in experiment
- Easy to turn feature on/off if broken
- Can see immediately in data if something went wrong
- "I can count at least 10 times where we launched an experiment and needed to turn it off the same day as we had broken something else"

### Key Quote

"Booking.com, Expedia, and their ilk are the exception. Instead of running hundreds or thousands of online tests a year, many firms run no more than a few dozen that have little impact. If testing is so valuable, why don't companies do it more? After examining this question for several years, I can tell you that the central reason is **culture**." - Stefan Thomke, Harvard Business Review

## Amy Edmondson's Three Types of Failure Framework

**Source**: Amy Edmondson, Harvard Business School Professor, "Right Kind of Wrong" (Financial Times Schroeders Business Book of the Year)

### The Three Types

#### 1. Intelligent Failures (The Right Kind of Wrong)

**Definition**: Failures that help gather data for new products or projects\
**Example**: A prototype that doesn't work

**Criteria for Intelligent Failure**:

- **In new territory**: Not enough knowledge to produce a success
- **In pursuit of a goal**: Requires learning and discovery
- **Hypothesis driven**: You've done your homework
- **Small enough**: Just big enough to learn from

**Alternative name**: "Beeping forward" - moving forward despite uncertainty to gather information

#### 2. Basic Failures (Preventable)

**Definition**: Everyday occurrences that are often preventable\
**Examples**: Email sent to wrong person, missed meeting\
**Cause**: "Coulda, shoulda, woulda" - not using knowledge that was available due to:

- Inattention
- Neglect
- Overconfidence

**Prevention Strategy**: Keep people on high alert by reminding them of possibility/probability of failure

- Healthcare practitioner: Remind team they work in complex, error-prone environment
- Pilot: Admit they've never flown perfect flight yet, ask crew for help spotting errors

#### 3. Complex Failures (Usually Preventable)

**Definition**: String of little mistakes that add up\
**Example**: Ship crashing into rocks\
**Characteristic**: Can turn into "real monsters"\
**Prevention**: Psychological safety enables blameless reporting to prevent escalation from basic to complex

### Psychological Safety Connection

**Warning Signs**: Many mistakes happen because warning signs were missed, ignored, or downplayed

**Leaders Should**:

- Pay attention to **ambiguous threats**
- Ensure employees at all levels willing to speak up **without certainty**
- Change attitude to **false alarms**

**Example**: Toyota's Andon cable - empowers factory floor staff to pause production when they spot threat to quality

### Mindset Shift: Learning Over Knowing

**Key Principle**: Favor learning over knowing (Adam Grant's "Think Again" approach)

- Essential for navigating uncertain, changing world
- Reframe automatic thoughts that cause pain/embarrassment
- Give yourself permission to learn

### The Electric Maze Experiment

**Setup**: Teams cross rug with grid of colored squares; some beep when stepped on\
**Goal**: Cross without making single beep\
**Common behavior**: Students hesitate before stepping (fear of beeping)\
**Insight**: This is "curious waste of time" - ALL squares (beeping or not) provide information\
**Lesson**: Information is unknowable beforehand, so every step is learning opportunity\
**Question for leaders**: "Is your team being encouraged, empowered, and enthused to 'beep forward'?"

## Amazon's Two-Way Door Decision Framework (Jeff Bezos 2016 Shareholder Letter)

### Day 1 vs Day 2 Culture

- **Day 1**: Vitality, customer obsession, experimentation
- **Day 2**: "Stasis. Followed by irrelevance. Followed by excruciating, painful decline. Followed by death."
- Core principle: "It is always Day 1"

### Four Essentials for Day 1 Defense

1. Customer obsession
2. Skeptical view of proxies
3. Eager adoption of external trends
4. High-velocity decision making

### High-Velocity Decision Making Principles

#### 1. Two-Way Door Decisions

"Many decisions are reversible, two-way doors. Those decisions can use a light-weight process. For those, so what if you're wrong?"

- Reversible decisions can use lightweight process
- Enables experimentation without heavy bureaucracy
- Example: A/B testing a feature on site detail page or mobile app

#### 2. 70% Information Rule

"Most decisions should probably be made with somewhere around 70% of the information you wish you had. If you wait for 90%, in most cases, you're probably being slow."

- Need to be good at quickly recognizing and correcting bad decisions
- Being wrong may be less costly than being slow
- Being slow is expensive for sure

#### 3. "Disagree and Commit"

"If you have conviction on a particular direction even though there's no consensus, it's helpful to say, 'Look, I know we disagree on this but will you gamble with me on it? Disagree and commit?'"

**Real Example**: Amazon Studios original

- Bezos view: "Debatable whether it would be interesting enough, complicated to produce, the business terms aren't that good, and we have lots of other opportunities"
- Team had completely different opinion
- Bezos response: "I disagree and commit and hope it becomes the most watched thing we've ever made"
- Result: Decision made quickly without need to convince
- Team had already brought home 11 Emmys, 6 Golden Globes, and 3 Oscars

**Key distinction**: Not passive acceptance, but:

- Genuine disagreement of opinion
- Candid expression of view
- Chance for team to weigh view
- Quick, sincere commitment to go their way

#### 4. Escalate True Misalignment Early

- Some teams have fundamentally different objectives and views
- No amount of discussion will resolve deep misalignment
- Default dispute resolution without escalation: exhaustion (whoever has more stamina wins)
- "You've worn me down" is awful decision-making process
- Go for quick escalation instead

### Customer Obsession and Experimentation

"Staying in Day 1 requires you to experiment patiently, accept failures, plant seeds, protect saplings, and double down when you see customer delight."

Key insight: "No customer ever asked Amazon to create the Prime membership program, but it sure turns out they wanted it"

## Airbnb Experimentation Culture Case Study

### Scale of Experimentation

- **Starting point**: Less than 100 experiments per week
- **Current state**: 700+ experiments per week
- **Failure rate**: 92% (as noted by Ronny Kohavi)

### Three Core Elements for Building Experimentation Culture

#### 1. Building Platforms and Tools to Support Experimentation

- Dedicated experimentation platform
- Automated tools to reduce friction
- Infrastructure that scales with demand

#### 2. Embedding Experimentation into Key Business Functions

- Data scientists embedded in product teams
- Not just "consulted" on product - data critical to decision-making
- Data scientists sit next to PMs, alongside engineers and designers
- Critical voice in roadmap planning

**Operating Model Shift**: Centralized → Decentralized

- Data scientists allocated to customer journeys
- Solve specific business problems
- Partner closely with Product Managers and Engineers
- Cross-functional teams

#### 3. Actively Shaping Culture of Experimentation

### Six Key Elements

#### 1. Invest in Data-Minded People Early

- **Riley Newman hired as data scientist at employee #10**
- Made before "big data craze"
- Strong signal that data would be central
- "They were eager to learn and evolve as fast as possible"

Early advantage: "We knew so little about the business that any insight was groundbreaking. The company was so small that everyone was in the loop about every decision"

#### 2. Data Isn't Numbers, It's People

"Data science is an act of interpretation – we translate the customer's voice into a language more suitable for decision-making." - Riley Newman

**Key Insight**: Data becomes most powerful when translated from cold, hard facts into "voice of the customer"

- Identify purchasing preferences and behaviors
- Inform business decision-making, prioritization, resource allocation
- Product development guidance

#### 3. Embedding Data Scientists into Teams

**Problem with centralized model**: Viewed as transactional interaction

- "How many nights booked in New York?"
- "How many listings in Paris?"
- Answer question, done

**Solution**: Decentralized model

- Data scientists embedded in product teams
- Sit next to PMs
- Critical voice in roadmap planning
- Best product ideas came from data scientists

**Bi-Weekly Experiment Reviews**:

- Product team members present experiments
- Focus on lessons learned, mistakes, next steps
- "Knowledge sharing was off the charts" - Lindsay Pettingill

#### 4. Hypothesis-Led, Not Feature Driven

"Experimentation is founded on the scientific method. This approach is an iterative learning model that is hypothesis-led."

**Good hypothesis**:

- Grounded in reality, existing facts, observations, or information
- Not a guess - a theory about what may work in future
- Based on existing quantitative or qualitative information

**Key principle**: "Just because we can build a new feature or product, it doesn't mean that we should"

#### 5. Aligning Around Measurable North Star Metric

- Singular focus on key metrics
- Everyone aligned on what success looks like
- Enables autonomous decision-making

#### 6. Building Platforms and Tools to Support Experimentation

- Reduce friction in running experiments
- Automate analysis where possible
- Make it easy for teams to experiment

### Cultural Quote

"Experimentation needs to be embraced and evangelised throughout the org. One thing that made this quite easy at Airbnb was the fact that data scientists were embedded in product teams." - Lindsay Pettingill

"For experimentation to work in your business it must come from a place of natural curiosity, with a really strong desire to learn from your customers."

"We used controlled experiments to learn and make decisions at every step of product development, from design to algorithms. They are equally important in shaping the user experience." - Jan Overgoor

## Netflix: A Culture of Learning Case Study

### Scale and Context

- Running experiments for more than 20 years (first investments in A/B testing tooling in 2001)
- Hundreds of product variants tested each year
- Multiple tests running simultaneously on product at any given time
- Members may be allocated to several tests simultaneously
- "There is not one Netflix product" - large number of product variants always being tested

### Failure Rate and Mindset

**Low Win Rate**: "Only a small percentage of our ideas turn out to be winners in A/B tests"

- Despite broad expertise across all disciplines
- Hundreds of product variants built and tested each year
- Only small percentage rolled out to 200+ million members

**Key Quote**: "At Netflix, we do not view tests that do not produce winning experience as 'failures.'"

**What They Learn from Non-Winning Tests**:

- Member preferences
- What works (and doesn't work) for different member cohorts
- Where there may or may not be opportunities for innovation
- Combining learnings from tests in given innovation area (e.g., Mobile UI) helps paint more complete picture

**Revisiting Failed Ideas**:

- As member base grows globally
- As consumer preferences and expectations evolve
- Sometimes signals from original analysis suggest better timing
- May provide value to newer member cohorts

### Cultural Principles

#### 1. Humbling and Empowering

"The low win rate in our experimentation program is both humbling and empowering."

**Humbling**: "It's hard to maintain a big ego when anyone at the company can look at the data and see all the big ideas and investments that have ultimately not panned out."

**Empowering**: "Nothing proves the value of decision making through experimentation like seeing ideas that all the experts were bullish on voted down by member actions in A/B tests — and seeing a minor tweak to a sign up flow turn out to be a massive revenue generator."

#### 2. Democratizes Ideation

"Because Netflix tests all ideas, and because most ideas are not winners, our culture of experimentation democratizes ideation."

**Key Insight**: Product managers hungry for ideas, open to innovative suggestions from anyone in company, regardless of seniority or expertise

**Reasoning**: "After all, we'll test anything before rolling it out to the member base, and even the experts have low success rates!"

**Result**: Product suggestions from engineers, data scientists, even executives can result in unexpected wins

#### 3. More Voices in Decision Making

"A culture of experimentation allows more voices to contribute to ideation, and far, far more voices to help inform decision making."

**Outcome**: "A better product for our members and an internal culture that is humble and values ideas and evidence: experimentation is a win-win proposition for Netflix."

### Organizational Structure

#### Data and Insights Organization

- Teams partner with all corners of company
- Own data pipelines from executive dashboards to personalization systems
- Data-driven mindset ubiquitous at all levels
- Represented at highest echelon of Netflix Leadership

#### Experimentation-Focused Data Scientists

- Collaborate with product innovation teams across Netflix
- Design and execute tests to support learning agendas
- Contribute to decision making
- Build domain expertise about aspects of Netflix experience
- Become valued partners to product managers and engineering leaders
- Help shape evolution of Netflix product through opportunity sizing
- Identify areas ripe for innovation
- Frequently propose hypotheses that are subsequently tested

### Decision-Making Process

#### "Informed Captain" Model

- Netflix does not make decisions by committee or consensus
- Single "Informed Captain" responsible for each significant decision
- Makes judgment call after digesting relevant data and input from colleagues (including dissenting perspectives)
- A/B test results or causal inference studies are expected input

#### Debate and Socialization

**Expectations**:

- Decisions on investment areas for innovation and testing documented in memos
- Test plans for major innovations documented
- Results of major tests documented
- All summarized, socialized broadly, and actively debated

**Forums**:

- Broadly accessible
- Diverse set of viewpoints provide feedback on test designs and results
- Open to anyone interested
- Price of admission: reading the memo
- Strong executive attendance but notable lack of hierarchy
- "We all seek to be led by the data"

**Data Scientists' Role**:

- Active and valued participants in forums
- Expected to speak for the data
- What can and cannot be concluded from experimental results
- Pros and cons of different experimental designs
- Not informed captains on product decisions
- Active contributors as interpreters of data

### Testing Philosophy

#### Bold Testing

"At Netflix, we are not afraid to test boldly, and to challenge fundamental or long-held assumptions."

**Example**: Top 10 list

## Experiment Documentation and Knowledge Bases (Statsig)

### The Problem

"Most teams are sitting on a goldmine of experimental learnings that nobody can actually find."

- Scattered across Slack threads
- Buried in spreadsheets
- Locked away in heads of people who might not work there anymore

**Key insight**: "Without proper documentation, you're basically throwing away all those insights you worked so hard to uncover"

### Role of Knowledge Bases

#### Collective Memory

Instead of asking "didn't we test something like this before?" in every planning meeting, teams can pull up past experiments and see exactly what worked and what didn't

#### Onboarding

Rather than spending weeks downloading years of context from different people's brains, new team members can self-serve and get up to speed fast

#### Searchable Repository

"Teams that nail this create a searchable repository of wins, failures, and 'huh, that's weird' moments that spark new test ideas"

- Like having conversation with past self who took good notes

#### Transparency and Accountability

- Everyone can see what's been tested
- Avoid two teams running same experiment three months apart
- Keeps people accountable
- If test bombed, that's valuable data - but only if someone can find it later

### Best Practices for Creating Knowledge Bases

#### 1. Define Clear Purpose

**Question**: What are you actually trying to accomplish?

- If goal is just to check compliance box → graveyard of half-filled templates
- If building something that helps teams ship better features faster → changes everything

#### 2. Organization is Make-or-Break

"Nobody's going to dig through 500 untagged experiments to find that one test about checkout flow from 2021"

**Need**:

- Consistent naming conventions
- Smart categorization that matches how team thinks
- Keywords people will actually search for
- Structure that works for both technical and non-technical users

#### 3. Add Visual Content

"A picture really is worth a thousand words when you're trying to explain why that button color test had such weird results"

- Charts, screenshots, visuals wherever possible
- Data team loves statistical analysis
- Design team just wants to see what variants looked like

#### 4. Maintenance Process

"The maintenance part is where most knowledge bases go to die"

**Solutions**:

- Simple review process (e.g., quarterly check-ins)
- Make contributing part of experiment wrap-up process
- Stale documentation is worse than no documentation

### Building and Maintaining Effective Knowledge Base

#### Tool Selection

"The best knowledge base is the one your team will actually use"

- Fancy features don't matter if everyone ignores them
- Look at existing workflow
- If team lives in Slack, tool that integrates there will get more traction
- Less copying and pasting required, the better

#### Guidelines (Not 50-Page Manual)

Clear basics:

- What counts as experiment worth documenting?
- Who's responsible for adding documentation?
- What's minimum viable documentation? (hypothesis, what you did, what happened, what you learned)
- How quickly after experiment should docs be added?

#### Stakeholder Involvement

Get stakeholders involved early:

- Product managers want business impact
- Engineers want implementation details
- Designers want visual examples
- Build something that serves everyone

#### Regular Updates

- Assign ownership (rotate quarterly)
- Use templates for consistency and speed
- Invest in search that actually works
- "If people can't find experiments in under 30 seconds, they'll stop looking"

### Leveraging Knowledge Bases to Foster Experimentation Culture

#### Shifts Team Thinking

"A good knowledge base doesn't just store information - it changes how your team thinks about experimentation"

**Examples**:

- Random idea in brainstorming? Quickly check if tested something similar
- Heated debate about user preferences? Pull up actual data from previous tests
- Companies like Airbnb and Booking.com credit experimentation success partly to making past learnings accessible

#### Meta-Analysis Opportunities

Once dozens or hundreds of experiments documented, patterns emerge:

- Tests on mobile consistently show different results than desktop
- Certain types of copy changes never move the needle
- "These insights only surface when you can look across experiments, not just at individual tests"

#### Breaking Down Silos

Experiment learnings searchable across teams:

- Growth team can learn from platform team's infrastructure tests
- Breaks down silos that keep valuable insights trapped in team bubbles

### Moves to Level Up Experimentation Culture

1. **Make checking knowledge base first step** in any experiment planning
2. **Share "experiment of the month" highlights** in all-hands meetings
3. **Create learning sessions** where teams walk through most surprising results
4. **Reward people** who surface insights from old experiments that inform new ones

### The Virtuous Cycle

"The goal isn't just to document for documentation's sake. It's to create this virtuous cycle where every experiment makes the next one better. Where failures become as valuable as successes because someone else can learn from them. Where your tenth experiment on a feature is informed by the previous nine, not starting from scratch."
