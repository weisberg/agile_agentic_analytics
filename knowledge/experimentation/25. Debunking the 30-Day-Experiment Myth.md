# Debunking the 30-Day-Experiment Myth

## Core idea

An experiment should not run for 30 days because the calendar says so.

It should run until the evidence is sufficient for the decision being made, given:

- the size of the effect that matters
- the speed at which the outcome appears
- the risk of being wrong
- the cost of waiting
- the risk of prolonged exposure

The right duration is an information problem, not a habit.

## Why the 30-day rule became popular

The default persists for understandable reasons:

- monthly reporting cycles
- a desire to capture weekly seasonality
- fear of peeking and false positives
- sprint planning convenience
- a general preference for one simple rule

The deeper historical roots trace back to Ronald Fisher's agricultural experiments at Rothamsted in the 1920s, where experiment duration was dictated by harvest seasons. That fixed-horizon mindset migrated to clinical trials and then to digital experimentation, where the constraints are entirely different. In direct mail testing, the average mail piece's response lifespan was 17-20 days, making 20-30 days a natural collection window. Some early A/B testing platforms hardcoded 30 days as a default duration, and users never overrode it.

In SaaS, Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) creates a structural gravitational pull on 30-day testing: the billing cycle is monthly, so teams assume they need a month to see financial impact. But "churn lag" means the definitive signal may be delayed far beyond 30 days, especially in B2B with annual contracts. And in agile development, 2-week sprint cycles create operational symmetry: launch at sprint start, test for two sprints (~30 days), decide in the fourth sprint.

Those reasons explain the habit, but they do not justify it for every test.

## The real question: long enough for what?

Experiment duration should always be tied to the estimand.

That means asking:

- What outcome are we actually trying to measure?
- When does that outcome occur?
- How much evidence do we need before acting?

If the answer is "first-session click-through rate," a month may be excessive.

If the answer is "60-day funding quality" or "90-day retention," a month may be far too short.

### The hidden estimand shift

A common failure mode: teams declare grand objectives (improve retention, increase LTV) but execute on short-term proxies (clicks, opens), creating a sleight-of-hand where everyone assumes the long-term outcome follows. This is the "estimand problem" -- the mismatch between what teams say they care about and what they actually measure. Making the estimand explicit forces honest conversations about whether the chosen duration is adequate.

Some causal questions simply cannot be answered via experiment in any reasonable duration. Teams should have the discipline to acknowledge this and use modeling, cohort analysis, or judgment when the experimental answer would take longer than the decision timeline allows.

### Goodhart's Law and surrogate metrics

If a proxy metric is pushed too aggressively, the correlation with the true outcome can break. Goodhart's Law ("when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure") applies directly: optimizing for a 7-day proxy of retention can produce features that game the proxy without improving actual retention.

## What should determine experiment duration

### 1. Statistical power and minimum detectable effect

If the team wants to detect a tiny change, the test will need more sample and usually more time.

Important implication:

- lower MDE means longer duration
- smaller effect of interest means slower decisions

The duration should follow from the sample size requirement, not from comfort.

### 2. Outcome observability

You cannot stop early and claim to measure an outcome that has not had time to happen.

Examples:

- immediate checkout clicks are observable quickly
- funded accounts may take days or weeks
- churn or retention may take months

If the outcome is delayed, either:

- run longer
- keep outcome observation running after exposure ends
- or explicitly switch to a validated proxy

### 3. Temporal dynamics of the treatment effect

Effects evolve.

Important patterns include:

- novelty spikes
- learning curves
- fatigue
- delayed conversions
- day-of-week or pay-cycle effects

A test should span the effect arc, not just the launch moment.

### 4. Decision risk

High-stakes changes need stronger evidence.

Examples:

- pricing
- fee structures
- critical product flows
- regulated experiences
- major algorithm or policy shifts

Low-risk and reversible changes can justify faster, lighter-weight decisions.

### 5. Cost of waiting

Waiting is not free.

Every extra day may mean:

- delayed rollout of a winner
- prolonged exposure to a loser
- blocked experimentation backlog
- slower learning across the organization

Duration should balance certainty against opportunity cost.

## When 30 days is too long

Thirty days is often unnecessarily long when:

- traffic is high
- the effect is large
- the metric moves quickly
- the change is easy to reverse
- the business needs speed more than maximal certainty

Examples:

- CTA tests on high-traffic pages
- obvious performance regressions
- simple UX fixes with immediate signals
- canary-style tests meant to catch severe harm quickly

In these cases, 30 days can be a tax on learning.

## When 30 days is too short

Thirty days is often too short when:

- the decision metric matures slowly
- user behavior changes over weeks, not days
- learning or fatigue effects are likely
- traffic is low
- the experiment affects high-trust or regulated experiences

Examples:

- lifecycle or frequency tests
- onboarding changes whose value appears later
- long purchase cycles
- financial-services flows with delayed value realization

In these cases, 30 days creates false confidence.

## When running too long creates its own problems

The cost of waiting is not just opportunity cost. Running an experiment too long introduces distinct validity threats:

- **Accumulated contamination and attrition.** Cookie expiration, user churn, and shifting population composition over 6-8+ weeks mean the users at the end of the test are not the same population as at the start.
- **External event contamination.** Competitor actions, news events, and seasonal shifts accumulate and dilute the treatment signal.
- **Effect decay and non-stationarity.** The treatment effect may be positive for the first month and negative in the second, but the aggregate shows nothing -- a form of Simpson's Paradox across time.
- **"Precision theater."** Massive over-collection produces statistically significant but practically meaningless effects. A 0.01% lift detected with 99.99% confidence is a waste of everyone's time.
- **Stakeholder fatigue.** The market evolves during the test, and the insight becomes stale before it is actionable.

There is a "sweet spot" for every experiment: enough duration to capture the effect, but not so much that external factors overwhelm it.

### The payday effect

Consumer liquidity fluctuates around the 1st and 15th/16th of the month, causing spikes in purchasing behavior and average order value during pay periods. A test running only 2 weeks might capture only a "rich" or "poor" period. Tests should span at least one full pay cycle to avoid this bias, but this can often be accomplished in less than 30 days by starting at the right time.

## The temporal patterns that make calendar defaults dangerous

### Novelty

Users react to what is new, not what is sustainably better.

Stopping during novelty measures curiosity, not value.

### Learning

Users may initially perform worse while adapting, then improve.

Stopping too early can kill something that would have worked.

### Fatigue

A treatment can look strong at first, then decay with repeated exposure.

Stopping too early can overstate long-run benefit.

### Delayed conversion

When the funnel takes time, early steps are not the full outcome.

Stopping early can optimize for low-value immediacy.

## Better ways to shorten the decision cycle

### Sequential testing

Sequential methods (mSPRT, GSPRT) calculate a likelihood ratio at every data update with dynamic decision boundaries that are wide early and narrow over time. The boundary is mathematically constructed to maintain overall error rate regardless of how many times the team checks results. This can reduce average required sample size by 30-70% compared to fixed-horizon testing, especially when effects are large or when there is no effect (futility stopping frees resources faster).

Best for:

- high-velocity environments
- situations where the cost of delay is meaningful
- tests with clear review rules

### Bayesian stopping rules

Bayesian A/B testing reframes the decision as "What is the expected loss if I am wrong?" rather than "Is this significant?" This risk-based framing directly addresses business questions. The test stops when expected loss drops below the organization's risk tolerance threshold.

Important caution: many "Bayesian" testing tools use flat/uninformative priors by default, which can give false confidence faster than frequentist tests. An uninformative prior assumes huge effects are as likely as small ones, leading to extremely high posterior confidence from noise alone. Teams should validate their Bayesian stopping rules with simulation before relying on them.

### Variance reduction

CUPED (Controlled-experiment Using Pre-Experiment Data) works by subtracting variance explained by pre-experiment behavior: Y_adjusted = Y - θ(X - E[X]), where X is the pre-experiment covariate. This lets teams detect the same effect with fewer observations, directly shortening required duration.

Important limitation: CUPED is ineffective for new-user acquisition experiments (landing page tests for first-time visitors) because there is no pre-experiment behavior to use as a covariate. In those cases, stratified randomization (balancing on categorical covariates like geography or device at design time) offers a weaker but still useful alternative.

Best for:

- high-volume experimentation programs
- organizations with good pre-experiment covariates
- returning-user experiments where behavioral history exists

### Validated proxies and surrogate metrics

Use fast metrics only when the organization has evidence they predict the long-term outcome.

A proper Surrogate Index is not a single proxy metric but a weighted linear combination of short-term proxies predicting the long-term outcome. The Prentice Criterion provides the formal validation standard: a short-term metric must fully mediate the effect of treatment on the long-term outcome. Mere correlation is insufficient; it must capture the causal pathway.

Best for:

- long-lead outcomes
- product areas with strong historical data

Important caution:

A proxy is an acceleration tool, not a free pass. If it drifts, the decision quality drifts with it. Day-of-week effects can often be handled without 30-day brute-force approaches by explicitly modeling day-of-week as a covariate or extrapolating from shorter durations using historical data.

## A practical duration matrix

Think about duration through signal velocity and risk.

### High signal velocity, low risk ("The Optimizer")

Recommended approach:

- short tests with sequential monitoring (mSPRT)
- fast decisions
- simpler stop rules

Examples:

- low-cost UI changes
- copy tests
- obvious usability fixes

### High signal velocity, high risk ("The Pivot")

Recommended approach:

- sequential monitoring with Bayesian bandit allocation
- strong guardrails
- tighter stop criteria

Examples:

- checkout or pricing flow changes
- major monetization changes

### Low signal velocity, low risk ("The Niche")

Recommended approach:

- CUPED + fixed horizon
- modest durations
- clear futility rules

Examples:

- minor optimizations on low-traffic surfaces

### Low signal velocity, high risk ("The Bet")

Recommended approach:

- longer duration
- explicit power planning
- Surrogate Index + cohort analysis
- long-term outcome tracking or validated surrogates

Examples:

- retention interventions
- financial-services flows
- strategic product bets

### The Cost of Delay formula

A concrete way to frame the duration trade-off: Cost of Delay = Estimated Value of Change / Duration. If a feature is worth $1M/year, every week of testing costs approximately $19,000 in unrealized value. When Cost of Delay is exceptionally high (critical bug fix, time-sensitive promotion), the strategy may shift to "ship and monitor" -- moving validation from pre-release experimentation to post-release observability with automated guardrails and instant rollback.

### Financial services specific considerations

Low-velocity financial services experiments often require 12+ weeks based on mandatory power analysis, making underpowered 30-day tests worse than not testing at all. A "slow cook" design can help: expose users for 2 weeks but measure behavior over the next 8 weeks. Compliance and trust risks from prolonged exposure (customers noticing different fee structures, regulatory scrutiny) also impose a "risk tolerance window" -- a maximum duration set by acceptable user exposure rather than statistical certainty. Organizations should have the discipline to declare infeasibility when a proper experiment would require more duration or sample than the business can tolerate.

## A pre-launch duration checklist

Before the experiment starts, answer these questions:

1. What exact outcome are we trying to estimate?
2. When does that outcome usually occur?
3. What minimum effect size is worth acting on?
4. How much sample is needed to detect it?
5. What temporal effects could distort early reads?
6. What is the cost of a false positive?
7. What is the cost of waiting?
8. What is the maximum tolerable exposure window if the treatment is harmful?
9. What early stop or futility rules will we use?
10. What makes this experiment not worth running at all?

If a team cannot answer those questions, "30 days" is probably functioning as a substitute for thinking.

## How to operationalize this across an organization

Strong teams do not solve duration one experiment at a time forever. They institutionalize better defaults.

Useful practices:

- require a written test plan with duration rationale
- build power calculators into the experimentation workflow
- automate experiment-health and guardrail monitoring
- document how long different classes of tests usually need
- review past tests to learn where the organization systematically over-waits or under-waits

Over time, the goal is to replace a universal default with organization-specific defaults based on accumulated data from past experiments (e.g., "most tests that eventually win show significance by day 10 in our context").

## Common pitfalls beyond the 30-day default

- **Running too short without power analysis.** A 7-day test that was never powered to detect the expected effect is worse than a 30-day test, because it produces false negatives that kill good ideas.
- **Ignoring novelty effects.** Looking at a "days since exposure" view and excluding the first few days can separate novelty from sustained value.
- **Not using variance reduction.** Many organizations are unaware that CUPED can cut required duration substantially, leading them to default to calendar-based rules.
- **Optimizing for the wrong metric.** Testing duration anchored to a fast metric (clicks) when the actual decision depends on a slow metric (retention) produces quick but wrong answers.
- **Loss aversion.** People feel the pain of a wrong decision more acutely than the pleasure of a correct one, leading to excessive caution and analysis paralysis. This is a direct application of Kahneman-Tversky loss aversion to experimentation: the fear of a false positive drives teams to run longer than the evidence warrants.

## Emerging approaches that further challenge fixed durations

- **Increased automation.** Platforms are automating sample size calculation, variance reduction application, and stopping decisions, removing the need for manual duration rules.
- **Heterogeneous treatment effects.** Different users respond differently; segment-level analysis can extract value from experiments that look flat in aggregate, potentially ending tests earlier for some segments.
- **Causal inference from observational data.** When experiments are infeasible (too slow, too expensive, too risky), methods like propensity score matching, instrumental variables, and difference-in-differences offer causal estimates without controlled exposure windows.
- **Multi-armed bandits.** Dynamic explore-exploit algorithms continuously adjust traffic allocation, replacing the fixed-duration paradigm with an adaptive one that maximizes cumulative reward while learning.

## A better default rule

If a team needs one sentence to remember, use this:

Run the experiment until the decision metric is observable and the remaining uncertainty is smaller than the cost of waiting.

That may be:

- 48 hours
- 10 days
- 6 weeks
- 90 days

The right answer depends on the decision.

## Bottom line

The 30-day experiment is not a law of nature. It is a convenient habit.

Mature experimentation programs replace it with a better discipline:

- power before comfort
- observability before optimism
- time-based rules only when they reflect real user and business cycles
- faster methods when speed is available
- longer horizons when truth arrives slowly

The goal is not shorter tests or longer tests.

It is better-timed tests.
