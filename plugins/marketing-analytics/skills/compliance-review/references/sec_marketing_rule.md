## Overview

SEC Rule 206(4)-1 under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940 (the "Marketing Rule")
governs advertisements and other marketing communications by SEC-registered investment
advisers. It replaced the prior Advertising Rule and Cash Solicitation Rule with a
single, principles-based framework.

## Definitions

**Advertisement** includes two prongs:

1. **Traditional advertisement** -- Any direct or indirect communication an adviser
   makes to more than one person, or to one or more persons if the communication
   includes hypothetical performance, that offers the adviser's investment advisory
   services regarding securities.

2. **Compensated testimonial/endorsement** -- Any endorsement or testimonial for which
   the adviser provides compensation, directly or indirectly.

## Seven General Prohibitions

An advertisement may not include:

### 1. Untrue Statements of Material Fact
Any statement of material fact that is untrue. This includes factual claims about
performance, assets under management, investment process, or team qualifications
that are inaccurate.

**Scanner keywords:** Specific numerical claims, AUM figures, track record dates,
credential claims.

### 2. Unsubstantiated Material Statements of Fact
Any material statement of fact the adviser does not have a reasonable basis for
believing it can substantiate upon demand by the SEC.

**Scanner keywords:** Claims requiring data support, attribution statements,
capability claims.

### 3. Untrue or Misleading Implications
Any untrue or misleading implication about a material fact relating to the adviser.

**Scanner keywords:** Comparative language, implication of exclusivity, suggestion
of guaranteed outcomes.

### 4. Failure to Disclose Material Facts
Failure to provide fair and full disclosure of a material fact necessary to make the
statement not misleading.

**Scanner keywords:** Partial performance data, omitted fee information, missing
risk factors.

### 5. Cherry-Picked Performance
Any material claim or statement that is reasonably likely to cause an untrue or
misleading implication about a material fact, including through the presentation of
performance in a misleading manner (e.g., cherry-picking favorable time periods).

**Scanner keywords:** Non-standard reporting periods, single-period highlights,
omitted down periods.

### 6. Misleading to Target Audience
Any information that is otherwise materially misleading, considering the target
audience of the advertisement.

**Scanner keywords:** Retail-facing content with institutional-level complexity,
jargon in consumer materials.

### 7. Otherwise Materially Misleading
Catch-all prohibition against any other materially misleading content.

**Scanner keywords:** Superlatives ("best," "top," "number one," "guaranteed,"
"risk-free," "safe," "no risk"), promissory language ("will achieve," "ensures"),
certainty language ("always," "never fails").

## Performance Presentation Requirements

### Gross and Net Performance

- If gross performance is shown, net performance must also be shown.
- Net performance must be presented with at least equal prominence to gross.
- Net must be calculated after deduction of all fees and expenses a client would pay.
- If using a model fee, the model fee must be disclosed and must equal or exceed the
  highest fee charged to the relevant portfolio.

### Time Period Requirements

- Performance must not be presented for select periods unless it also shows results
  for 1-year, 5-year, and 10-year periods (or since inception if shorter).
- All periods must end on a consistent date (most recent practicable).
- Performance for periods of less than one year must not be annualized.

### Benchmark Requirements

- If performance is compared to a benchmark, the benchmark must be appropriate.
- The benchmark must be clearly identified.
- Results must be shown for the same time periods as the adviser's performance.

### Hypothetical Performance

Hypothetical performance (including back-tested, model, and targeted/projected) may
only be shown if the adviser:
- Adopts and implements policies and procedures reasonably designed to ensure the
  hypothetical performance is relevant to the likely financial situation and objectives
  of the intended audience.
- Provides sufficient information for the audience to understand the criteria and
  assumptions used.
- Provides sufficient information to understand the risks and limitations of using
  hypothetical performance.

### Extracted Performance

When showing the performance of a subset of investments from a portfolio, the
advertisement must also show the total portfolio performance.

## Testimonials and Endorsements

### Required Disclosures

For testimonials and endorsements, the adviser must disclose:
- Whether the person giving the testimonial/endorsement is a client or investor.
- Whether the person is compensated (cash or non-cash).
- Material conflicts of interest.

### Written Agreement Requirement

A written agreement is required with any person giving a testimonial or endorsement
if the adviser provides compensation of more than $1,000 (or equivalent) over the
preceding 12 months.

### Disqualification

Certain "bad actors" (persons subject to SEC disqualification events) may not provide
testimonials or endorsements.

## Third-Party Ratings

An advertisement may include a third-party rating only if the adviser:
- Has a reasonable basis for believing the questionnaire or survey is structured to
  produce unbiased results.
- Discloses the date of the rating and the period of time evaluated.
- Discloses the identity of the third party that created and tabulated the rating.
- Discloses whether compensation was provided for obtaining the rating.

## Books and Records

Advisers must retain:
- All advertisements disseminated (directly or indirectly).
- Records substantiating material statements of fact.
- Records of the adviser's annual review of compliance policies related to marketing.
