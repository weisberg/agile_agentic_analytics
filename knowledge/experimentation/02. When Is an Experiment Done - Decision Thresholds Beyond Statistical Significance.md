# When Is an Experiment Done? Decision Thresholds Beyond Statistical Significance

## Executive summary

An experiment is done when the organization has enough trustworthy evidence to choose among four actions:

- ship
- roll back
- continue
- archive as inconclusive

That sounds simple, but it is a fundamental shift away from the most common amateur rule in experimentation: "Stop when p < 0.05."

Mature programs do not treat statistical significance as the same thing as decision readiness. They treat it as one input into a broader decision engine that includes:

- validity of the experiment itself
- size and business relevance of the effect
- downside risk
- guardrail performance
- temporal stability
- opportunity cost of waiting
- long-term effects that may not yet be visible

The central insight is this: an experiment is not done when it becomes mathematically interesting. It is done when the remaining uncertainty is no longer worth the cost of carrying it.

## The problem with the classical stopping rule

The fixed-horizon experiment with a precomputed sample size and a single final p-value remains a useful foundation. But in real organizations it runs into five practical problems.

### 1. Teams peek

Product teams, marketers, and executives rarely wait patiently until the end of a test. They look early and often. In a classical fixed-horizon framework, that behavior corrupts the nominal false-positive rate. A test designed for a 5% type-I error rate can easily drift to 30% or higher if teams keep checking and opportunistically stopping. Research shows that if you stop an A/B test the moment it first crosses 95% significance, there is roughly a 50% chance the result is a false alarm.

### 2. Significance ignores economics

A tiny statistically significant lift may be commercially irrelevant. If a variant improves a metric by 0.2% but introduces engineering complexity, maintenance cost, design debt, or support burden, the true decision may still be "do not ship."

### 3. Non-significance is ambiguous

A non-significant result can mean:

- there is no meaningful effect
- there is an effect, but the test was underpowered
- the wrong metric was chosen
- the effect is heterogeneous and canceled out in aggregate
- the effect is delayed and not yet observable

Treating all non-significant results as "no difference" is intellectually lazy and operationally expensive.

### 4. Short-term metrics can mislead

Some metrics move fast but are poor indicators of long-term value. Add-to-cart, opens, clicks, feature taps, and first-session engagement can all produce strong early readings that later reverse once novelty fades or deeper user behavior emerges.

### 5. The cost of waiting is real

Every extra day of a running test has an economic consequence:

- a winning feature is not fully rolled out
- a harmful feature continues to touch users
- the next experiment remains blocked
- teams delay learning on more valuable hypotheses

The correct question is not "Can we keep waiting?" It is "What are we paying for this additional certainty?"

## From significance testing to decision engines

The best experimentation organizations have moved from a narrow statistical mindset to an operational decision mindset.

The old question:

- Is the result statistically significant?

The better question:

- Is the evidence now strong enough, safe enough, and decision-relevant enough to justify action?

That shift turns experimentation from a reporting function into a governance function. It also changes what "done" means. A finished experiment is not one that produced a number. It is one that produced a defensible decision.

## The layered decision model

The most useful way to think about experiment completion is as a stack of gates. Each gate answers a different question. An experiment can clear the statistical gate and still fail the rollout gate.

### Gate 1: validity

Before asking whether the treatment worked, ask whether the test was trustworthy.

Minimum checks:

- no sample ratio mismatch
- stable and correct exposure logging
- reasonable randomization balance
- no obvious contamination across groups
- no broken instrumentation

If validity fails, the test is not "done." It is invalid. Continuing to discuss effect size in an invalid experiment is like debating a lab result from a contaminated sample.

### Gate 2: safety

Guardrail metrics answer a simple question: even if the primary metric improved, did we do harm somewhere else?

Examples:

- crash rates
- page latency
- support contacts
- unsubscribe rate
- complaint rate
- trust signals
- seller or supplier health in a marketplace

Guardrails are not secondary metrics. They are veto metrics.

### Gate 3: materiality

A result can be statistically persuasive and commercially trivial.

Questions to ask:

- Does the effect clear the threshold of caring?
- Is it large enough to justify rollout cost and complexity?
- Would we still want this result if the true effect were at the lower end of the interval?
- Does the result move a metric that actually matters to the business model?

The minimum detectable effect should not be a purely statistical setting. It is a business lever that encodes how much change is worth acting on.

### Gate 4: risk

Different decisions require different evidence thresholds.

Low-risk examples:

- copy tweaks
- reversible UI changes
- non-core layout changes

High-risk examples:

- pricing or fee changes
- recommendation logic with possible fairness implications
- major checkout or signup redesigns
- policy and eligibility changes
- experiments in regulated environments

When the cost of a false positive is high, the organization should demand more certainty, stronger guardrails, and sometimes longer follow-up windows.

### Gate 5: time

The effect has to be observed over the right temporal horizon.

Questions:

- Has novelty settled?
- Has the learning curve matured?
- Have delayed conversions had time to happen?
- Have we observed at least one full behavioral cycle, such as weekday/weekend or billing cycle?

An experiment can be valid, safe, and promising, but still too early to call because the observed effect is not yet stable.

### Gate 6: opportunity cost

The final question is not about mathematics. It is about portfolio management.

If more time is unlikely to change the decision in a useful way, the test is done. If continuing mostly buys expensive ambiguity, stopping is rational.

## The statistical crisis of the fixed horizon

Traditional A/B testing frameworks assume a predetermined sample size and a single final analysis. In industrial experimentation, this assumption often breaks immediately.

### The peeking problem

Every unplanned interim look increases the chance of a false discovery under a fixed-horizon design. This matters because organizations naturally want to monitor live tests. They want to catch disasters early and capture wins fast. The demand is reasonable. The classical method is not built for it.

### The efficiency problem

Even when a treatment is clearly strong, a fixed-horizon test may require the team to keep running until the planned sample is reached. That means:

- winners are delayed
- losers remain live
- experimentation bandwidth is wasted

The statistical ideal and the operational ideal diverge.

### The communication problem

P-values answer a question most stakeholders are not actually asking. Business leaders want to know:

- How likely is this to be better?
- How much could we lose if we are wrong?
- What happens if we wait?

Those are decision questions. Classical significance testing only partially serves them.

## Sequential testing: the first major upgrade

Sequential testing methods were built for precisely the environment in which modern experimentation teams operate: repeated looks, pressure for faster decisions, and the need to preserve error control.

Properly implemented sequential testing can reduce average experiment duration by 20-30%, and by up to 60% when effect sizes are large. This directly increases throughput and lets teams explore a larger hypothesis surface area per year. Some organizations now treat experimentation velocity -- experiments run per engineer per year -- as a first-class organizational metric.

### Group sequential testing

Group sequential approaches predefine a limited number of interim looks and distribute the total error budget across them using alpha-spending functions.

Two common alpha-spending approaches:

- O'Brien-Fleming boundaries are very conservative early (spending almost no alpha on initial looks) and save the budget for later analyses. Best for confirmatory experiments where early stopping carries high risk.
- Pocock boundaries spend alpha more evenly across looks, making early stopping easier but requiring a higher total sample size if the test runs to completion.

Strengths:

- preserves rigor
- relatively intuitive for teams used to z-tests and confidence intervals
- powerful when sample size can be estimated well

Weaknesses:

- less flexible than continuous monitoring
- depends on a reasonably predictable data accumulation pattern

This approach works especially well when data arrives in batches, such as daily reporting environments.

### Always-valid inference

Always-valid methods support continuous monitoring while preserving valid inference. The two primary approaches are:

**Mixture Sequential Probability Ratio Test (mSPRT).** The mSPRT evaluates the likelihood ratio of observed data under a composite alternative hypothesis (a distribution over possible effect sizes) versus the null. Because the test statistic behaves as a martingale under the null, it produces "always-valid p-values" that are correct at any sample size, not just at a predetermined endpoint. In early stages the always-valid p-value is substantially more conservative than a standard t-test p-value, penalizing small samples and preventing premature stopping from noise. As data accumulates, the two converge. This means teams can check results at any time: if the always-valid p-value drops below 0.05 after 1,000 visitors, the result is valid even if the original power calculation called for 10,000.

**Generalized Sequential Probability Ratio Test (GSPRT).** The GSPRT uses the supremum (maximum) of the likelihood function rather than integrating over a mixing distribution, avoiding the need to specify a prior distribution over effect sizes. Uber adopted GSPRT as part of their Speed, Quality, and Risk (SQR) framework, which automates graduated feature rollout (1% → 5% → 20% → 50% → 100%). At each ramp stage the system evaluates whether it is safe to proceed, automatically halting rollout if metrics degrade or accelerating if success metrics show strong positive signal.

Strengths:

- highly flexible
- compatible with streaming data
- no need to pre-specify a small number of looks

Weaknesses:

- conceptually more abstract
- less familiar to teams trained on classical A/B testing
- requires careful tuning and internal education

A practical caution: some vendors implement a "corrected-alpha approach" that does not fully solve the peeking problem and can still inflate false positive rates. Teams should verify that their platform uses genuine sequential methods with valid stopping boundaries.

### Futility boundaries

One of the most underused ideas in experimentation is stopping not because a winner emerged, but because continuing is unlikely to pay off.

A futility rule asks:

- Given the current trajectory, is this test unlikely to produce a decision worth acting on in a reasonable time?

This is especially valuable in portfolio-heavy environments where backlog pressure is high. Every experiment that continues without a credible path to useful clarity is effectively taxing the entire program.

## Bayesian decision rules: the second major upgrade

Bayesian reasoning is not just a statistical alternative. It is a change in the language of experimentation.

Instead of framing the result as:

- If there were no effect, how surprising would these data be?

it frames the result as:

- Given the data, how likely is this variant to be better, and how costly would it be to be wrong?

That is much closer to how operating teams actually make decisions.

### Probability of being better

One of the most intuitive Bayesian outputs is the probability that treatment outperforms control. This is easier for stakeholders to reason about than a p-value and creates clearer risk conversations.

### Credible intervals

Credible intervals let teams discuss plausible ranges of lift in direct terms, rather than abstract frequentist coverage properties. This is especially useful when pairing statistical evidence with rollout cost or expected payoff.

### Expected loss

Expected loss is where Bayesian experimentation becomes especially valuable for decision-making.

It answers:

- If we choose this variant and it is actually worse, what is the expected cost of that error?

This allows organizations to translate abstract uncertainty into business exposure. It is particularly useful when the main question is not "Is this definitely better?" but "Is the downside small enough that we should move?"

### Threshold of caring

A mature Bayesian program does not ask whether the treatment is greater than zero. It asks whether it exceeds a meaningful practical threshold.

That threshold may be:

- a target revenue lift
- a cost-saving requirement
- a minimum activation increase
- a maximum tolerated loss

This moves the organization from statistical significance to decision relevance.

### Calibrating epsilon

The expected loss threshold (epsilon) should not be an arbitrary number. Leading implementations calibrate it relative to the baseline metric value -- for example, 0.1% of mean visitor value for strict tests, or up to 2% for more exploratory tests. The key is that epsilon encodes organizational risk tolerance in concrete business units, not abstract statistical ones.

### Priors and discipline

Bayesian methods are powerful, but they are not magical. Priors can sharpen analysis or distort it. Optional stopping bias exists in Bayesian frameworks just as in frequentist ones. Mature teams:

- use weakly informative priors when uncertainty is high
- consider empirical priors based on historical experiments (shrinking noisy estimates toward the distribution mean)
- validate stopping rules with simulation under both null and alternative hypotheses
- avoid turning Bayesian monitoring into undisciplined optional stopping theater
- recognize that informative priors from analyzing large portfolios of past experiments can act as a natural false discovery rate control, shrinking small noisy results toward zero

## The metric hierarchy: not every metric gets a vote

One reason organizations struggle to decide when experiments are done is that too many metrics are treated as equally important.

They are not.

### The overall evaluation criterion

Every meaningful experiment should have a primary decision metric tied to long-term business value. This may be a single metric or a carefully defined composite, but it must be explicit.

Examples:

- completed purchases
- funded accounts
- retained subscribers
- contribution profit
- a composite retention and engagement objective

If the experiment lacks an authoritative outcome metric, stopping decisions will become political.

### Success metrics

These are the metrics expected to improve if the treatment works. They should align directly with the decision the test is meant to inform. The appropriate statistical test is a superiority test, adjusted for multiple comparisons (Bonferroni, Hochberg, or similar) when tracking multiple success metrics to prevent cherry-picking a single winning metric.

### Guardrail metrics

These are the metrics that should not degrade in a tolerable rollout. They protect the organization from shipping narrow wins that damage the broader system.

The appropriate statistical test for guardrails is a non-inferiority test against a predefined Non-Inferiority Margin (NIM) -- for example, "latency cannot increase by more than 10ms" or "unsubscribe rate cannot increase by more than 1%." Guardrails hold veto power: even if every success metric shows strong lift, a guardrail violation blocks the ship decision.

Importantly, guardrail metrics should not receive standard false-positive corrections (Bonferroni). Instead, they should be adjusted for false negatives to ensure sufficient statistical power to detect real degradations. The risk with guardrails is missing a problem, not falsely flagging one.

Spotify's Confidence platform codifies this into a conjunctive logic gate: ship if and only if (1) Treatment is superior on at least one success metric, (2) Treatment is non-inferior on all guardrail metrics, and (3) no deterioration metric is triggered.

### Deterioration metrics

Some mature systems separately track metrics where even modest harm should trigger a rapid review or automatic rollback. These are circuit breakers: if a critical metric (server CPU load, payment failures, crash rate) shows statistically significant degradation of any magnitude, the experiment is automatically aborted to protect users. This is distinct from a post-hoc "do not ship" decision; it is an emergency stop during the run.

Examples:

- payment failure rate
- content abuse rate
- trust complaints
- support escalations

### Proxy metrics

Proxy metrics deserve a place in the hierarchy, but below the true decision metric.

They are most valuable when:

- they move faster than the final outcome
- they have been historically validated against that outcome
- they help shorten learning cycles without replacing actual measurement entirely

Poor programs confuse sensitivity with truth. Strong programs use proxies as accelerators, not substitutes.

## Risk-adjusted thresholds: one rule is never enough

There is no universal confidence threshold that makes sense for every test.

### Low-risk changes

If the change is cheap to reverse, low in blast radius, and tightly guarded, it may be rational to ship with lower certainty or after a shorter window.

### High-risk changes

If the change affects pricing, trust, fairness, eligibility, or core user experience, the organization may need:

- stronger statistical evidence
- more stable temporal patterns
- broader guardrail coverage
- longer follow-up windows

### Dynamic thresholds

The strongest organizations implicitly or explicitly vary their decision boundaries based on:

- experiment risk class
- strategic importance
- expected cost of delay
- expected cost of error

This is more mature than pretending the same 95% rule should govern everything from a headline test to a payments policy.

Some organizations derive time-dependent decision boundaries through simulation: extremely conservative thresholds early in an experiment that gradually relax as data accumulates. This reflects the reality that a strong signal after two days deserves more skepticism than the same signal after two weeks.

### ROI-based threshold selection

A quantitative approach to threshold selection treats the significance level as an economic variable. By modeling the expected ROI of shipping at different confidence levels -- balancing the probability of a true positive against the cost of false positives and the cost of delay -- teams can identify the threshold that maximizes expected business value. In some analyses, the optimal threshold is far from 95%: lower for cheap, reversible changes; higher for expensive, irreversible ones. This makes thresholds a portfolio-level optimization, not a universal constant.

### Portfolio-level false discovery rate

When an organization runs many experiments in parallel, controlling the false discovery rate (FDR) across the portfolio becomes important. Procedures like Benjamini-Hochberg control the expected proportion of false discoveries rather than the per-test error rate. This is especially relevant at scale, where running hundreds of tests per quarter means that even a 5% per-test error rate produces dozens of false wins per year.

## Decision latency versus cost of error

The deeper trade-off beneath every stopping rule is the trade-off between two costs.

### Cost of error

What happens if we ship the wrong thing?

Possible consequences:

- revenue loss
- churn
- trust damage
- operational instability
- fairness or compliance issues

### Cost of delay

What happens if we wait too long?

Possible consequences:

- delayed value capture
- slower roadmap learning
- backlog congestion
- strategic drag in competitive markets

The best stopping decisions explicitly balance these two forces. If cost of delay is enormous and cost of error is moderate, earlier action becomes rational. If cost of error is catastrophic, patience is rational.

The optimal stopping time is where the marginal cost of waiting another day exceeds the marginal reduction in expected cost of error. If a feature is worth $1M per year, every week of testing costs approximately $19,000 in unrealized value -- making the cost of delay concrete rather than abstract.

### Ship to Learn

For low-risk, reversible changes, some organizations collapse the "test then ship" phase into a "ship and monitor" phase. Features are deployed behind feature flags with real-time guardrail monitoring. If guardrails hold, the feature stays. If they break, the flag is turned off immediately. This is not a license to skip experimentation. It is a recognition that some information can only be gained in production, and that the decision engine's safety mechanisms (automated guardrails, instant rollback) make the approach rational when cost of error is bounded.

### Phased rollout as sequential evidence

A related pattern is phased rollout: deploying to 10% of users with moderate confidence, then expanding to 50% as a form of real-world confirmation before full deployment. Each ramp stage provides additional evidence about the treatment's effect under increasingly representative conditions. This is especially useful when the organization's confidence needs to increase alongside the blast radius of the change.

## Long-term validity: the short-term win problem

Many experiments look different in the short term than they do over longer horizons.

### Novelty and primacy

Users often react to newness before they react to value. A treatment can spike engagement because it is unfamiliar, or dip because users are still learning it.

### Delayed outcomes

Some outcomes simply take longer to appear.

Examples:

- subscription retention
- account funding
- repeat purchase
- complaint decay or escalation

### Holdouts as calibration tools

One of the best ways to judge whether short-term signals are reliable is to maintain long-term holdouts: a small persistent percentage of users (1-5%) excluded from all new features for a prolonged period. This group measures the cumulative impact of the experimentation program and reveals the "Sum of Parts" fallacy -- the common situation where individual experiment uplifts (2% + 2% + 2% ...) sum to far more than the actual realized growth (perhaps 3%). The holdout reveals this non-additivity, grounding business impact forecasts in reality rather than optimistic arithmetic.

Holdout data also calibrates the decision engine itself. By comparing the short-term proxy metrics of experimental groups against the long-term retention and revenue of the holdout group, data scientists can verify which proxies actually predict long-term value. When a proxy stops correlating with the true outcome, the decision engine is updated.

### Meta-analysis of past experiments

The highest-maturity organizations study their own experimentation history to answer questions like:

- Which early metrics actually predict long-term value?
- How often do early winners fade?
- How often do flat starts become later wins?
- Which experiment classes are most vulnerable to novelty?
- Which test categories show time-varying effects, and which do not?

Netflix, for example, analyzed historical experiments across temporal dimensions and found that most signup flow experiments did not show strong time-varying effects, giving confidence to run shorter experiments in that domain. This kind of meta-analysis transforms stopping policy from ideology into empirical operating knowledge and can justify shorter durations where the data supports it.

## Special cases that require more sophisticated designs

### Network effects and interference

Standard user-level randomized tests rely on the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA): one user's treatment does not materially alter another user's outcome. This assumption breaks in:

- rideshare and delivery systems (a treatment user booking a driver removes that driver from the control user's supply pool)
- marketplaces
- ranking or recommendation systems
- social products

In these contexts, user-level stopping logic can be misleading because the measured effect may be artificially inflated by resource competition between groups.

**Switchback testing.** Instead of randomizing users, the platform randomizes time windows within a geographic region. For example, in a specific city, the new dispatch algorithm runs from 8:00-8:20 AM (treatment), then the old algorithm runs from 8:20-8:40 AM (control), then switches back. The experimental unit is the region-time block, not the user. Washout periods between blocks are discarded to prevent carryover. The challenge is that sample size drops dramatically (from millions of users to perhaps 1,000 time blocks), inflating variance. DoorDash found that naive t-tests on switchback data yield false positive rates up to 62% due to within-block correlation. They use multilevel modeling with cluster-robust standard errors to correctly estimate variance, achieving strong statistical power while keeping false positive rates controlled and enabling decisions 30% faster than naive methods.

**Variance reduction in switchback designs.** To recover statistical power lost to the switchback design, techniques like CUPAC (Control Using Predictions as Covariates) use machine learning models to predict expected outcomes for each time block based on contextual features (weather, time of day, historical demand). Testing the residual between observed and predicted outcomes reduces unexplained variance, effectively "buying back" the power lost to the small sample size.

**Modeling interference bias.** Some organizations explicitly quantify spillover rather than ignoring it. If a treatment shows a 5% lift, but modeling estimates that 2% of that came from cannibalizing the control group's resources, the adjusted "true" lift is 3%. This prevents shipping features that look locally beneficial in an A/B test but fail to deliver global value at 100% rollout.

Other recommended responses:

- geo-based designs
- cluster randomization
- ecosystem-level guardrails

### Marketplaces

A change can benefit one side of the marketplace while harming the other. Shipping decisions must consider:

- buyer metrics
- seller metrics
- matching efficiency
- long-run liquidity

### Regulated and financial contexts

These environments require especially careful definitions of "done" because a strong short-term metric can mask downstream risk or compliance exposure. In such cases:

- long-term observation windows may matter more
- fairness and customer-protection guardrails deserve veto status
- decision thresholds should be stricter

## Organizational operating models for better stopping decisions

A sophisticated statistical framework will still fail in a weak operating culture.

### Pre-commitment

Every experiment should begin with a documented plan covering:

- primary metric
- guardrails
- minimum duration or information threshold
- maximum tolerable downside
- allowed review cadence
- stop, ship, rollback, and archive criteria

A practical multi-criteria stopping rule might require: (a) minimum 14 days runtime, (b) minimum 100+ conversions per variant (preferably 300+), (c) statistical significance on the primary metric, (d) the observed difference persisted for the last 3 consecutive days, (e) no secondary or guardrail metric raises concerns, with a pre-set maximum duration (such as 28 days) as a hard cap. The minimum conversions safeguard is important because an experiment can reach statistical significance with very few conversions, producing a technically significant but practically unreliable result.

This converts a live debate into a governed decision process.

### Human judgment still matters

Even the most advanced experimentation platform cannot automate every decision. A useful stopping framework still depends on human review of:

- metric coherence
- product context
- engineering nuance
- strategic importance

But human judgment should be structured, not improvisational.

### The experiment review memo

A strong review memo should answer:

1. What decision was this test meant to inform?
2. Did the experiment pass validity checks?
3. Which metric has final authority?
4. What is the plausible effect range, not just the point estimate?
5. Which guardrails matter most and how did they move?
6. What is the expected loss if we choose incorrectly?
7. What is the cost of delaying another week?
8. Which long-term effects remain unresolved?
9. Is the result coherent with prior evidence?
10. What is the recommended action and why?

## Case-style lessons from leading experimentation cultures

Different mature companies have solved the stopping problem in different ways, but several common lessons repeat.

### Lesson from large-scale streaming environments

Organizations operating with massive traffic and daily batched data often prefer structured sequential methods because they allow fast yet disciplined decisions. They especially value the ability to stop early when a result is dramatically better or worse than expected.

### Lesson from complex marketplaces

Platforms with long journeys and two-sided dynamics often become skeptical of strong early results. They invest more in simulation, dynamic decision thresholds, and stability checks because the first strong signal is often not the final truth.

### Lesson from experimentation platforms at scale

Companies that built deep experimentation cultures learned that the statistical method alone is not enough. They had to build:

- trustworthy logging
- clear OECs
- strong guardrail enforcement
- shared experimentation literacy
- mechanisms to resist HiPPO-driven overrides

The method and the culture rise together or fail together.

### Cultural heuristics that support good stopping decisions

**Twyman's Law** -- "any figure that looks interesting or different is usually wrong" -- serves as a useful institutional principle. It fosters healthy skepticism toward surprisingly strong results and encourages validation before celebration.

**The Rule of Thirds** -- roughly one-third of experiments show positive effects, one-third negative, and one-third neutral -- provides a calibration anchor. If an organization's win rate dramatically exceeds one-third, it is likely running tests that are too safe or interpreting results too loosely. If it falls well below, the hypothesis generation or execution pipeline may need work.

## A practical decision framework

The following framework is intentionally more detailed than a simple "ship or not" rubric because real organizations need more than one outcome.

### Ship now

Ship when:

- the test is valid
- no critical guardrail is worsening
- the decision metric exceeds the practical threshold
- temporal behavior looks stable enough
- cost of delay now exceeds value of more certainty

### Roll back now

Roll back when:

- a critical guardrail shows material harm
- the decision metric shows meaningful downside
- the treatment creates unacceptable operational or trust risk
- continuing would mostly expose more users to known harm

### Continue

Continue when:

- the test is healthy
- the plausible effect range still spans materially different decisions
- the treatment may matter but the current precision is inadequate
- novelty, learning, or delayed outcomes have not yet resolved

### Stop as inconclusive

Stop when:

- the experiment has already consumed the information budget that makes sense
- further runtime is unlikely to change the decision enough to justify the opportunity cost
- the organization has learned that the effect, if any, is too small to matter

An inconclusive test is not a failed test. It is a completed investment in uncertainty reduction.

## Infrastructure requirements for modern stopping decisions

The advanced decision criteria described above -- sequential testing, Bayesian rules, automated guardrails -- require a data platform that can support them.

To realize the value of continuous monitoring and sequential testing, the data pipeline must be real-time or near-real-time. If experiment statistics are only updated in daily batch runs, the value of "continuous monitoring" is largely lost. Leading organizations invest in streaming architectures to update experiment statistics continuously, enabling the system to trigger an early stop or guardrail alert within minutes of a threshold breach rather than 24 hours later.

Some platforms now synthesize multiple inputs (significance, MDE attainment, guardrail health) into automated decision outputs with color-coded recommendations: "Ship Now," "Roll Back Now," "Ready for Review," or "Continue." These automated frameworks do not replace human judgment but compress the cognitive load of the most routine decisions, freeing review bandwidth for the genuinely difficult calls.

## What "done" should mean going forward

A modern definition of experiment completion should combine:

- validity
- safety
- materiality
- calibrated risk tolerance
- temporal appropriateness
- portfolio opportunity cost

In other words, an experiment is done when the next unit of information is less valuable than the action the organization can now take.

## Bottom line

The immature question is:

- Did this test hit significance?

The mature questions are:

- Is the experiment trustworthy?
- Is the effect large enough to matter?
- Is the risk acceptable?
- Has the effect stabilized enough to believe?
- What are we paying to wait longer?

When teams adopt that framework, "done" stops being a statistical cliff and becomes what it should have been all along: a disciplined business decision grounded in evidence.
