# **21c. From Holdouts to Experimentation \- The First-Year Maturity Curve**

# From Holdouts to a Culture of Experimentation: Evolving Marketing Analytics

## Introduction

Many organizations start their analytics journey with basic **holdout-based measurement** before maturing into a formal experimentation program. In a holdout test (also called a **control group test**), a portion of the audience is deliberately excluded from a marketing campaign to serve as a baseline, while the rest receives the usual treatment. By comparing outcomes between the two groups, teams can quantify the **incremental impact** of their marketing efforts[\[1\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=Holdout%20testing%20is%20the%20process,would%20otherwise%20not%20be%20realized). For example, an email marketing team might send a campaign to 90% of customers while “holding out” the other 10% (who receive no email) to see how much lift in engagement or sales the campaign truly generates[\[1\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=Holdout%20testing%20is%20the%20process,would%20otherwise%20not%20be%20realized). This simple A/B setup (send vs. no-send) helps answer the critical question: *“What would happen if we did nothing?”* and ensures that any observed uptick in conversions is due to the campaign itself. In an era of complex multi-channel marketing – where ad platforms might claim credit for conversions that would have happened anyway – holdout testing is considered the **gold standard** for isolating true causal impact[\[2\]](https://www.measured.com/faq/holdout-test/#:~:text=The%20reality%20is%20that%20every,and%20how%20much%20is%20noise). It directly answers the counterfactual question: *“How many sales would we lose if we didn’t do this marketing?”* This kind of incrementality insight is increasingly vital as marketing budgets tighten and accountability is paramount.

Starting with holdout tests is a sensible first step. It provides a reality check on marketing assumptions and can reveal whether marketing communications are driving genuine uplift or merely cannibalizing sales that would have occurred anyway[\[3\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=The%20main%20reason%20for%20running,the%20profits%20of%20your%20business). In fact, the primary reason to run a holdout experiment is often to ensure you’re not *“giving away your profit margin when it’s not needed,”* as one email marketing expert notes[\[4\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=The%20worst,margin%20when%20it%E2%80%99s%20not%20needed). For a company that regularly sends discounts or offers, a holdout can show if those promotions are truly incremental or simply **cannibalizing natural sales** that would have happened without the incentive[\[3\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=The%20main%20reason%20for%20running,the%20profits%20of%20your%20business). The worst-case scenario is an eye-opener: if the customers who received no marketing end up buying at the same rate as those who did, it means the marketing had no impact – or worse, that you’ve been eroding profit by giving discounts unnecessarily[\[4\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=The%20worst,margin%20when%20it%E2%80%99s%20not%20needed). Uncovering such truths early is crucial. It prevents complacency and highlights where the status quo marketing may be wasting resources.

However, **holdout-based measurement is just the beginning**. Relying on occasional holdouts or one-off tests gives only snapshot insights. As organizations grow more analytically ambitious, they strive to build a **formal experimentation program** – a repeatable, scalable process where many ideas (not just “send vs. not send”) are tested continuously to guide decisions. Evolving to this stage involves much more than technology; it requires cultural change, new team processes, and careful sequencing of steps. An authoritative study from Microsoft emphasizes that reaching a mature “experiment at scale” capability is an iterative journey of both technical and cultural evolution[\[5\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWalk%E2%80%9D%20milestones%2C%20to%20the%20%E2%80%9CRun%E2%80%9D,by%20using%20a%20Fly%20wheel). In practice, there is no overnight transformation – companies progress through stages of experimentation maturity (“crawl, walk, run, fly” in one model)[\[6\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=At%20the%20time%20when%20we,and%20in%20what%20way%20determines), each with its own challenges and learnings.

In fact, some pioneers in financial services embraced this approach decades ago. **Capital One**, for example, famously built its credit card business through an “information-based strategy” of relentless testing. When Capital One solicits new cardholders by direct mail, it can run *thousands of experiments* on different credit offers and messaging[\[7\]](https://cebma.org/assets/Uploads/Anderson-2011.pdf#:~:text=drive%20decision%20mak%02ing,But%20other%20companies%E2%80%99%20business%20models). By systematically testing interest rates, fees, incentives, and target segments, Capital One was able to discover winning combinations that competitors missed. This test-and-learn ethos was revolutionary in banking at the time, and it underpins the idea that robust experimentation can unlock enormous value even in a traditionally conservative industry.

In the following sections, we explore how an organization – especially in marketing analytics for financial services – can evolve from basic holdout tests to a robust experimentation culture. We will look at the **typical first experiments** that “immature” teams run, how a single early win can spark broader buy-in, and the **common pitfalls** that often stall progress. The focus is on practical sequencing: the realistic steps and missteps companies experience on the journey, rather than an idealized end-state. Marketing experimentation examples (particularly from email marketing) will illustrate these concepts, but the lessons are broadly applicable across channels and industries.

## Stage 1: Starting with Holdout-Based Measurement

In the earliest stage of maturity, organizations rely on simple holdout tests to measure impact. A holdout experiment is straightforward by design: one group gets the marketing treatment, and a control group is held back and gets nothing[\[1\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=Holdout%20testing%20is%20the%20process,would%20otherwise%20not%20be%20realized). By reviewing differences in outcomes – such as conversion rates or revenue – the team can determine if the campaign truly drove an “incremental increase” in engagement or sales that **would not have happened otherwise**[\[1\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=Holdout%20testing%20is%20the%20process,would%20otherwise%20not%20be%20realized). This approach provides a baseline measurement of marketing effectiveness.

Take the example of an email campaign promoting a new financial product. Rather than emailing the entire customer base as usual, an analyst might suggest holding out a random 10% of eligible customers. If the emailed group shows, say, a 5% signup rate and the holdout group shows a 3% signup rate, the **incremental lift** from the email is roughly 2 percentage points. That lift is the true value created by the campaign – the portion of signups that *wouldn’t* have occurred without the email. Such insight is invaluable for justifying marketing investments, especially in a conservative, results-driven environment like financial services.

Holdout tests are often the *first* experiments an immature analytics organization runs because they address fundamental questions of ROI. Marketers frequently assume their efforts work, but the data can surprise. A holdout can expose “cannibalization” of natural sales[\[3\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=The%20main%20reason%20for%20running,the%20profits%20of%20your%20business) – for instance, you may discover that customers who didn’t receive a promotional email ended up purchasing at nearly the same rate as those who did. In the worst-case scenario, the no-contact group might buy *more*, revealing that heavy discount emails were actually eroding profit margin with no benefit[\[4\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=The%20worst,margin%20when%20it%E2%80%99s%20not%20needed). Uncovering such a finding early on can prompt a major re-think of strategy (e.g., perhaps resources should shift to channels that *do* show lift, or messaging should be adjusted to add value beyond what customers would do anyway). Whether the holdout result is positive or negative, the organization learns something essential: either “this marketing works and yields \+X% lift” or “this marketing as-is isn’t doing much, so we can cut it or try a different approach.”

Importantly, running a basic holdout requires relatively low technical infrastructure. It can often be done with existing email tools or by manually splitting a list. The **organizational hurdle** is more cultural: convincing stakeholders to hold back a portion of customers (and thus potentially forgo some immediate sales) for the sake of learning. This can be a tough sell initially – no one likes the idea of *not* marketing to customers. Here, having leadership support is key. Forward-thinking managers recognize that a small short-term sacrifice (not messaging a fraction of the audience) can yield valuable evidence to guide much larger decisions. In a financial services context, this might mean persuading executives that it’s worth *not* sending a certain upsell offer to 5% of customers this quarter in order to accurately measure the uplift – the insight gained could save or earn millions by scaling effective campaigns and killing ineffective ones.

Early holdout experiments tend to focus on **high-level questions**:

* *“Does this channel or campaign work at all?”* – e.g., emailing vs. not emailing, or sending direct mail vs. no mail, to measure baseline impact.

* *“How much of our sales are truly incremental?”* – e.g., what percent of credit card sign-ups or loan applications happen only because of our marketing, versus what would happen organically.

* *“Are we reaching a point of diminishing returns?”* – e.g., if we reduced contact frequency or stopped targeting a certain segment, would sales drop or remain the same (indicating oversaturation or waste)?

These foundational questions set the stage for more refined experiments later. At Stage 1, success is measured not just by uplift found, but by the organization’s realization of the **value of testing itself**. Even a null or negative result (e.g., discovering a campaign had no incremental effect) is incredibly useful – it can trigger reallocation of budget or a change in tactics. Thus, an early mantra is *“let the data speak, even if it’s counterintuitive.”* In fact, one best practice for initial experiments is to deliberately test something that people are unsure or divided about, because a **counterintuitive result** can grab attention and prompt a mindset shift. Researchers at Microsoft observed that an A/B test which “contradicts stakeholders’ expectations” often provides the most bang for the buck in convincing people of experimentation’s value[\[8\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=power%20of%20A%2FB%20testing%20and,do%20a%20quick%20survey%20of). If everyone internally assumes a certain marketing tactic is vital, and a holdout test shows no impact, that surprise can become a powerful story that challenges conventional wisdom and opens eyes to the need for more testing. Conversely, if the test validates the tactic, it provides hard numbers that justify continued investment. Either way, the organization gains knowledge it wouldn’t have had without experimenting.

Before moving on, it’s worth noting that holdouts do come with limitations. They tell you **if** something works, but not necessarily *how to improve it*. Once an organization validates that a channel (say, email) has positive incremental impact, they naturally start asking more granular questions: *Which subject line works better? What content drives more engagement? Who should we target or not target?* Answering these requires moving beyond yes/no holdouts into multi-variant experimentation. That transition is where many teams struggle – it requires formulating hypotheses, designing different treatments, and sometimes more advanced analysis. But it’s also where the real optimization begins, which brings us to the next stage of evolution: running the first set of structured A/B tests in an ongoing program.

## First Experiments in Immature Organizations

When a company is new to experimentation, the first 3–5 tests they run are usually simple, tactical, and focused on quick wins. These initial experiments serve as proofs of concept and learning opportunities. Based on common patterns in marketing teams, typical first experiments include:

1. **Holdout Campaign to Measure Incremental Lift:** As discussed, a broad holdout test is often experiment \#1. For example, an email marketing team might withhold a promotional email from a random subset of customers to quantify the campaign’s true impact[\[1\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=Holdout%20testing%20is%20the%20process,would%20otherwise%20not%20be%20realized)[\[3\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=The%20main%20reason%20for%20running,the%20profits%20of%20your%20business). This kind of test addresses the fundamental question, “Does our marketing drive additional conversions or revenue, and *by how much*?” A real-world illustration comes from a retailer that tested its catalog mailings. By splitting customers into a mailed group and a no-mail holdout group, they discovered the catalogs drove only a 14% incremental lift in revenue – far less than the 40% contribution claimed by the vendor’s attribution models[\[9\]](https://www.measured.com/faq/holdout-test/#:~:text=After%20the%20campaign%2C%20Measured%20analyzed,the%20majority%20of%20incremental%20revenue). This surprising result (14% vs. 40%) gave the team hard evidence to reduce wasted spend and target only the most responsive customers going forward[\[9\]](https://www.measured.com/faq/holdout-test/#:~:text=After%20the%20campaign%2C%20Measured%20analyzed,the%20majority%20of%20incremental%20revenue). In a similar vein, a bank might hold out a segment from an email upsell campaign to see if those customers upgrade their accounts at a lower rate; if they don’t, it implies the campaign’s ROI is questionable. Overall, this first category of experiment is about **proving baseline value** – it often provides the business case (or wake-up call) that spurs further investment in experimentation.

2. **A/B Test of Subject Lines or Messaging:** Once a team is comfortable with basic control vs. treatment measurement, they often experiment with **creative elements**, especially subject lines in emails. Subject lines are easy to vary and can significantly influence open rates, making them a common early A/B test. For instance, one hypothesis could be that including the customer’s first name in the subject will increase open rates. A Capital One marketing playbook gives a similar example: *“Adding the recipient’s first name in the subject line will increase the click rate.”* This is a classic first A/B test because it’s simple to execute and measure (open rate or click rate being the metric) and ties directly to a marketing outcome[\[10\]](https://www.capitalone.com/tech/software-engineering/using-experimentation-for-sound-decision-making/#:~:text=Let%E2%80%99s%20begin%20by%20setting%20up,a%20link%20to%20the%20site)[\[11\]](https://www.capitalone.com/tech/software-engineering/using-experimentation-for-sound-decision-making/#:~:text=1,they%20have%20short%20attention%20spans). Early subject-line tests often yield quick lessons. If a personalized subject outperforms, it reinforces the value of relevancy and personalization; if it doesn’t, that’s equally informative – perhaps your audience values a clear offer or a sense of urgency more than seeing their name. Other messaging tests might include trying different email copy approaches (e.g., a formal tone vs. an informal one) or different calls-to-action. The goal is to snag an easy win, like a higher click-through rate or conversion rate with one version, thus demonstrating the tangible benefit of testing even minor creative tweaks.

3. **Content or Offer Variation:** Another typical early experiment is trying two different content or offer approaches to see which resonates more. In email marketing, this could mean testing **Offer A vs. Offer B** within the email. For example, a finance company might test an email offering “0% APR for 6 months” vs. one offering “$100 cash bonus” to see which promotion drives more account sign-ups. Similarly, they might experiment with email design/content – for instance, a version with a big image banner and rich graphics vs. a plainer text-focused email – to see which drives more clicks or conversions. These tests usually involve one substantial difference at a time (to keep it simple): e.g., a different headline, a different hero image, or a different incentive. The learning can directly inform future campaigns: if one offer outperforms, marketing can double down on that in subsequent campaigns. Often, organizations find that certain messaging angles or benefits (e.g., emphasizing “low fees” vs. “bonus rewards”) perform better, which challenges assumptions and provides direction for creative strategy.

4. **Send Time or Frequency Experiment:** Many fledgling programs test operational aspects like **when** or **how often** to send communications. A send-time test might involve sending one batch of emails in the morning vs. another batch in the evening to see which gets better engagement (opens or clicks). A frequency test is particularly insightful if there’s internal debate such as, “Are we emailing our customers too often? Or not enough?” For instance, the marketing team might split their audience: one half receives the usual weekly newsletter or four emails a month, while the other half receives only two emails a month. They would then monitor metrics such as open rates, click-throughs, conversion, and importantly **unsubscribe rates** and spam complaints. These experiments can reveal a “sweet spot” for contact frequency. In one case, researchers ran a contest to guess the optimal email frequency for an e-commerce company, and the actual test involved groups receiving anywhere from 1 email per month up to 15 emails per month[\[12\]](https://marketingexperiments.com/email-marketing/dec-2-09-email-clinic-html#:~:text=Segment%201%3A%201X%20PER%20MONTH,Segment%207%3A%2015X%20PER%20MONTH)[\[13\]](https://marketingexperiments.com/email-marketing/dec-2-09-email-clinic-html#:~:text=Image%3A%20Email%20Sends%20GraphResults%3A%20Testing,at%20that%20point%20of%20irritation). The results showed that beyond a certain point, additional emails led to diminishing returns in revenue and a sharp increase in unsubscribes – essentially, more email wasn’t always better[\[13\]](https://marketingexperiments.com/email-marketing/dec-2-09-email-clinic-html#:~:text=Image%3A%20Email%20Sends%20GraphResults%3A%20Testing,at%20that%20point%20of%20irritation). Early frequency tests help organizations find a balance between staying engaged with customers and overwhelming them. This is particularly important in financial services where customer trust is crucial; bombarding customers with too many messages can erode trust or prompt opt-outs, defeating marketing efforts in the long run. A simple test of “less vs. more” can settle arguments with data and set an optimal contact policy.

5. **Personalization vs. One-Size-Fits-All:** As a slightly more advanced early experiment, some teams explore segmentation or personalization strategies. For example, an insurance company’s marketing team might test a generic email vs. one tailored to the customer’s profile (say, customizing imagery or product recommendations based on the customer’s age or life stage). The hypothesis could be that a tailored message will improve engagement and conversion among the targeted segment. Initial segmentation experiments are usually coarse (perhaps just 2 segments – e.g., younger vs. older customers – to keep it manageable) and look for big differences. The outcomes inform whether investing in personalization yields enough lift to pursue further. Often, organizations discover that certain segments respond very differently to marketing – for instance, younger customers might prefer a more casual tone and digital-first approach, whereas older customers might respond to more formal language or detailed information. This insight can justify more segmentation in future campaigns. Early personalization tests also teach teams how to operationalize targeting (e.g., using dynamic content insertion, which is a new skill for many marketing orgs). If the personalized variant significantly outperforms, it strengthens the case for investing in customer data and content variation capabilities. If it doesn’t, that itself is a useful finding – maybe personalization isn’t worth the complexity in that context, or maybe the team needs to try a different personalization angle.

These first experiments are deliberately **simple to execute** and **focused in scope**. As advised by experimentation experts, it's wise to avoid complexity in initial tests – no multi-factor designs, no complicated cross-channel coordination[\[14\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=,split%2050%2F50%20to%20maximize%20sensitivity). Keeping tests small and bite-sized reduces the chance of errors and makes results easier to interpret[\[14\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=,split%2050%2F50%20to%20maximize%20sensitivity). Early on, the organization likely lacks a sophisticated A/B testing platform or dedicated experimentation team, so tests must fit into existing workflows. A good rule of thumb is to choose tests that can be run with the tools and data you already have, rather than requiring new systems. For example, an email platform that can randomize splits and track basic metrics (opens, clicks, conversions) is enough to run the above tests without needing data scientists or custom analytics pipelines.

Another hallmark of first experiments is that they tie to **visible metrics** that matter to the business. Open and click rates, conversion counts, or incremental sales are immediately understandable to both marketers and executives. A key tip from practitioners is to ensure you can “easily connect the impact to your metrics” – you don’t want to be building complex data pipelines for a simple test[\[15\]](https://www.firstprinciples.ventures/insights/the-experimentation-flywheel-growing-ab-testing-momentum-at-scale#:~:text=Measurement%3A). Early experiments should yield results that are straightforward to communicate, like “Version A got 500 clicks and Version B got 600 clicks, a 20% lift.” This clarity helps in the next step of evolution: getting broader buy-in.

**Illustrative Scenario:** To see how these early experiments can unfold, imagine a marketing team at a financial services firm beginning its experimentation journey. Initially, the team sends every promotional email to all customers with no measurement of incremental impact. At the urging of a new analytics lead, they run a simple holdout test on an upcoming credit card offer email – 90% of customers get the email, 10% are held back as a control. The result: the emailed group has significantly higher application rates, confirming the campaign’s value and delivering measurable revenue lift. Buoyed by this success, the team shares the finding with their CMO, who is impressed to see **hard numbers** on marketing impact for the first time.

Encouraged to continue testing, the team then turns to optimization. They perform an A/B test on the email’s subject line (a personalized subject vs. a generic one). The personalized version wins with a higher open rate and modestly higher click-through, reinforcing the idea that small tweaks can improve outcomes. Next, they experiment with email frequency: splitting the audience to receive either the usual four emails per month or a lighter two-per-month schedule. This test yields a surprise – the lighter schedule produces almost the same total conversions, with far fewer unsubscribes, indicating the team had been over-emailing. Armed with this insight, they reduce contact frequency, improving customer sentiment without hurting sales.

Within the first few months, this hypothetical team has used 3–4 experiments to challenge assumptions and quantify marketing effects. They learned which campaigns truly drive incremental business, which messaging resonates better, and how to avoid saturating their audience. Equally important, they’ve begun to change minds internally. Leaders now see concrete evidence (e.g. “the holdout test showed a 5% lift, worth $500K in revenue”) and are more willing to support further testing. The initial holdout became the **success story** that the analytics lead cites in meetings to advocate for an expanded program. The stage is set to move from these ad-hoc trials to a more formal, continuous experimentation approach.

## Leveraging Early Wins to Build Support

One successful experiment can become the **inflection point** for an organization’s experimentation journey. It’s common to see teams reuse a single compelling result – essentially an internal case study – to justify expanding the experimentation program. When an early test provides a clear win or a striking insight, savvy teams will broadcast that story far and wide to gain allies and executive sponsorship.

For instance, imagine the marketing team’s first holdout test revealed that an email campaign drove a significant incremental increase in account sign-ups (or conversely, that it drove none and saved the company from wasting effort). That result is gold. The team can present it to leadership with a narrative like: “We held out 10% of customers from our Q1 email campaign and found a **6% lift in purchases** among those who got the emails versus those who did not. That 6% represents an additional \\$500,000 in revenue[\[16\]](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/email-marketing-whats-a-hold-out-group-and-why-you-should-use-them/#:~:text=Once%20you%20know%20your%20natural,your%20programs%20regularly%20is%20imperative). If we hadn’t tested, we wouldn’t know this – we might have underestimated the campaign’s value or, conversely, we might be spending on campaigns that don’t actually move the needle. Now imagine if we could test more of our campaigns; we could optimize every dollar.” By translating the experiment into business impact, the team makes a compelling argument for doing more experimentation.

Sharing early successes is not just for executives; it’s equally important to get *colleagues* and other departments on board. A best practice is to **socialize results widely** – essentially, to market your marketing experiments internally. Teams at Microsoft, for example, hold internal “Best Experiment” talks where they showcase interesting A/B test results across the company to inspire others[\[17\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=3,interests%20in%20the%20scientific%20method). In a smaller organization, this might take the form of an email newsletter or a brownbag meeting where the experiment owner walks through what they did and what was learned. The goal is to spark reactions like, *“That’s fascinating – we should be testing our campaigns too\!”* According to experts, when one team’s results are “shared broadly, this can lead to interest in A/B testing by new teams and new A/B testing scenarios”[\[18\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=3,make%20an%20A%2FB%20testing%20program). It creates a positive ripple effect, where teams that were not experimenting see the concrete benefits and want to try it for themselves. To support this spread of interest, it’s helpful to create an environment where new teams feel they will be supported (in terms of tools or expertise) and that their initial experience with A/B testing will be a success[\[19\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=new%20teams%20and%20new%20A%2FB,make%20an%20A%2FB%20testing%20program).

**Reusing a single holdout experiment as a rallying cry** often involves framing it in terms that resonate with business objectives. For example, if the holdout proved a certain marketing spend yields high ROI, the team can frame it as “This experiment **proved the value** of our email channel – it delivered \\$X in incremental revenue, which is a 10x return on the cost. We should increase investment there.” Conversely, if the experiment found little impact, they can say “We identified an inefficiency – a campaign we thought was driving sales actually wasn’t, so we can save \\$Y by not doing that anymore or redeploy that budget to something else.” Both angles – proving winners and identifying losers – are powerful for rallying support. The key is to emphasize that **decisions informed by experiments are better decisions**. This shifts the discussion from opinion-based debates to evidence-based action.

It’s worth noting that not every early experiment will be a smashing success. Sometimes the result is neutral or the “win” is simply that a bad idea was *prevented*. Teams can leverage those too. An experiment that stops a negative outcome (for instance, preventing the rollout of a feature that would have hurt conversion) is a success of the testing program, even if it’s not a market success for the idea. As the Microsoft experimentation group points out, an A/B test that correctly flags a feature that *regressed* key metrics can be as valuable as an A/B test that showed an improvement[\[20\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=program%20forward%2C%20while%20failure%20may,when%20selecting%20initial%20A%2FB%20tests). So when championing experimentation, include those narratives: “Thanks to our test, we *didn’t* launch a change that would have caused a 5% drop in online applications – we saved the company from a costly mistake.” This reinforces that experiments serve as a safety net and learning tool, not just a way to chase uplifts.

In practical terms, leveraging an early win often leads to calls for more resources or at least more permission to experiment. It might convince management to green-light an “experimentation initiative” or to fund an A/B testing platform, or to assign dedicated analysts to support testing. One pattern is that after seeing initial value, leadership asks for **more decisions to be supported by experiments**. They might say, “This worked for email – can we also test our direct mail campaigns? Or our website? Let’s extend this approach.” This is when the demand for experimentation starts to outpace the team’s manual capacity, which is a good problem to have – it means the organization is ready to move into a more structured program.

However, a word of caution: while reusing a single success story is effective, organizations should avoid the trap of thinking one experiment’s results solve everything. The goal is to use the story as a *springboard*, not a crutch. For example, a team shouldn’t assume that because one holdout test was positive, they now know everything about that channel and don’t need to test further. Nor should they cling to one surprising result and generalize it to all contexts without additional evidence. The purpose of championing that first experiment is to open the door for a **culture of continuous testing**, not to declare the work done. So alongside the celebration of the win, smart teams also communicate the need for ongoing validation: “This was one campaign; we have dozens more and customer behavior changes over time – we should build the capability to test and learn continuously, not just once.”

In summary, early wins need evangelists. It takes effort to turn a single data point into organizational momentum. But when done well – through persuasive communication, tying results to business KPIs, and involving key stakeholders in the interpretation – a lone experiment can ignite a flame of experimentation within the company. The next step is channeling that momentum into a formal program, ensuring that testing becomes an integrated, repeatable part of how the organization operates rather than a one-off project.

## Building an Experimentation Program: A Step-by-Step Evolution

With initial experiments under their belt and growing interest from stakeholders, organizations face the challenge of scaling experimentation from a one-team effort to a cross-company program. This evolution should be handled in practical stages. Instead of leaping directly to an “ideal” end state, companies progress iteratively, addressing people, process, and technology along the way. We can think of this in roughly four phases (though in practice these can overlap):

### Phase 1 – Gaining Buy-In & Forming a Core Team (Crawl)

The first phase is all about **establishing buy-in and assembling the basics**. Leadership support at this juncture is crucial – without executive champions, an experimentation program can fizzle out quickly. Thus, as soon as those initial tests demonstrate value, forward momentum depends on securing explicit executive buy-in[\[21\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=Phase%201%20,6%20months). This could mean a senior leader publicly endorsing experimentation as a strategy, allocating budget to it, or simply encouraging teams to test ideas rather than relying solely on intuition. Simultaneously, a **core experimentation team** or task force should be formed[\[21\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=Phase%201%20,6%20months). In early stages, this is often a small, scrappy group – maybe one or two analysts or data scientists plus a couple of enthusiastic marketers or product managers. Their role is to drive the next round of experiments and coordinate initial efforts.

During Phase 1, the organization might still rely on manual processes to run tests, and that’s okay. In fact, experts advise to “run a few experiments with manual processes if needed” before worrying about fancy tools or automation[\[21\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=Phase%201%20,6%20months). The emphasis is on demonstrating value quickly and building a track record of wins. The core team can identify 1–2 pilot projects (perhaps with different departments or channels) as **first customers** of the budding program[\[21\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=Phase%201%20,6%20months). For Vanguard’s context in financial services, this might involve one pilot in the email marketing team (e.g. testing an email campaign vs. holdout, as discussed) and another in, say, the web team (perhaps A/B testing a change to the online account setup flow). Picking friendly teams who are open to experimentation increases the odds of early success.

Early on, the core team should also start laying down some guiding principles and doing some education. This might include simple **experiment design guidelines** (for example, how to properly randomize users into groups, how long to run tests, what metrics to focus on) and basic statistical literacy for stakeholders. Many successful programs cite the importance of early **education** – explaining what statistical significance means, why tests need sufficient sample size, etc., so that stakeholders trust the process. Documentation can be lightweight at this stage (maybe a one-page “Experimentation 101” primer), but establishing a common language and framework is part of setting the foundation.

An important early deliverable in Phase 1 is a rough roadmap or vision for the program. While keeping expectations realistic, the core team can sketch out what they aim to achieve in the next 6–12 months. For example: “In the next 6 months, run at least 5 experiments across two channels, build a basic internal dashboard of results, and train 10 more staff on experimentation basics.” Having such goals helps maintain leadership confidence that the effort is moving forward deliberately. It also gives the team something to measure itself against.

### Phase 2 – Initial Expansion & Culture Shift (Walk)

Once the groundwork is laid, Phase 2 focuses on **expanding the experimentation practice** to more teams and establishing some supporting infrastructure. A key objective here is achieving an **initial culture shift** – getting people to think “let’s test it” more routinely instead of assuming or debating. One effective strategy is to go after “quick-win, low-effort experiments” and do *more of them*, even if they are small[\[22\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=,Create%20reusable%20experiment%20templates). Quantity has a quality of its own at this stage; running many simple tests builds familiarity and yields a steady stream of insights to keep stakeholders engaged. As one guide put it, focus on pushing the *flywheel* by doing the basics well and often: *“performing simple, high-quality experiments often”* is the winning recipe to keep the momentum increasing[\[23\]](https://www.firstprinciples.ventures/insights/the-experimentation-flywheel-growing-ab-testing-momentum-at-scale#:~:text=,keep%20spinning%20faster%20and%20faster%E2%80%9D).

During this expansion, the core team often acts as an internal coach or support hub. They help other teams design and analyze experiments (since those teams are new to it) and ensure quality control. For example, they might create **reusable experiment templates** or checklists so that any marketing or product manager can follow a standard process to set up a test[\[22\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=,Create%20reusable%20experiment%20templates). Common templates could include an email A/B test template, a landing page test template, etc., with predefined steps and metrics. This standardization is critical to avoid chaos as more people start experimenting. It’s around this time that investing in a minimal **A/B testing platform or tool** becomes useful – perhaps the company purchases a license for an off-the-shelf experimentation tool or builds a very basic internal system. The priority is enabling easy test setup and measurement (cohorting users, measuring outcomes) so that running an experiment is not overly burdensome. In evaluating tooling, teams often face a **buy vs. build** decision[\[24\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=Phase%202%20,6%20months). Using a reputable third-party platform (such as Optimizely, Adobe Target, or others) can accelerate the program with less engineering effort, while building a custom platform offers more flexibility and integration with internal systems if the company has the resources. Many firms initially choose a vendor solution to get up and running quickly, then consider building their own as experimentation scales. Whichever route, the goal is to have a reliable system in place to roll out experiments and collect results consistently.

Another hallmark of Phase 2 is **celebrating any and all experiments** – not just the big wins. The organization should visibly recognize teams that run tests, to reinforce the desired behavior. For instance, one recommendation is to “publicly celebrate any completed experiments”[\[22\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=,Create%20reusable%20experiment%20templates). This could be as simple as a shout-out in a company meeting (“Sales tested a new email script last week – great job embracing experimentation\!”) or an internal blog where experiment results are shared. These rituals help embed experimentation into the company’s identity. Employees start to see testing as a normal part of how work gets done, rather than a special initiative. In this phase, leadership can play a big role by asking about experiments in project reviews (“Have we tested that assumption?”) and by praising teams that take an evidence-based approach – sending the signal that it’s safe and even encouraged to experiment.

On the process side, Phase 2 is when initial governance might form. Perhaps an **Experimentation Council** or regular meeting is created to review proposed tests and share results. The purpose isn’t heavy bureaucracy, but rather coordination and knowledge sharing. This ensures that experiments align with business priorities (so teams focus on tests that matter) and that teams aren’t duplicating efforts or inadvertently running conflicting tests. It also creates a forum for discussing test design and analysis challenges, further building internal skills.

At this stage, **metrics and data quality** come into focus as well. The more experiments run, the more critical it is to have trustworthy data and consistent metrics definitions. The team might introduce a system for defining **standard KPIs** (e.g., everyone agrees what “conversion” means or what counts as an “active user”)[\[25\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=,Create%20reusable%20experiment%20templates)[\[26\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=%2A%20Evolve%20high,insights%20to%20generate%20experiment%20hypotheses). They might also start implementing basic data validation for experiments, such as checking for **Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM)** when splitting test groups (to ensure a 50/50 split is actually 50/50 and there isn’t a tracking bug)[\[27\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=fundamental%20components%20for%20running%20a,ups%20and%20new). Running an occasional A/A test (where both variants are identical) to validate the experimentation system end-to-end is a common best practice at this juncture[\[28\]](https://www.convert.com/blog/a-b-testing/ab-testing-mistakes/#:~:text=,if%20the%20tool%20works)[\[29\]](https://www.convert.com/blog/a-b-testing/ab-testing-mistakes/#:~:text=Why%3F) – if an A/A yields a significant difference, that indicates a problem in randomization or measurement that needs fixing. By Phase 2’s end, the organization should have a few processes in place to ensure experiments are being set up correctly and data is reliable.

Culturally, Phase 2 is about turning the initial enthusiasm into a sustainable interest. This is where the idea of an **“experimentation flywheel”** (popularized by Microsoft and others) really takes shape[\[30\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=What%20is%20a%20Flywheel%3F)[\[31\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=Introducing%20the%20A%2FB%20testing%20Flywheel). As described earlier, the cycle goes: run more tests → demonstrate value → increase interest → justify investment → lower the friction to run tests → which leads to even more tests[\[18\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=3,make%20an%20A%2FB%20testing%20program)[\[32\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=trustworthy%20and%20easier%20to%20execute,it%20worthwhile%20for%20more%20teams). In Phase 2, the company is actively spinning this flywheel. For example, as more teams show interest (perhaps a social media team wants to test ads, or the retail branch marketing team wants to test direct mail offers), leadership allocates more resources – maybe hiring another data analyst or increasing the budget for an A/B testing tool. Those resources in turn reduce the “human cost” of running tests (making it easier and faster)[\[32\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=trustworthy%20and%20easier%20to%20execute,it%20worthwhile%20for%20more%20teams)[\[33\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=infrastructure%20and%20data%20quality%20create,to%20try%20running%20A%2FB%20tests), which encourages even more teams to hop on board. It’s a virtuous cycle of growth: value → investment → capability → more value.

As experimentation scales in Phase 3, it’s wise to establish a **network of experimentation champions** in the organization. Many companies designate support at three levels: an executive sponsor (to champion the program and secure resources), strategic liaisons or program managers (to coordinate experiments across teams and integrate them into business strategy), and ground-level experts embedded in teams to help design and run tests[\[34\]](https://www.firstprinciples.ventures/insights/the-experimentation-flywheel-growing-ab-testing-momentum-at-scale#:~:text=Executive%20Sponsorship%3A)[\[35\]](https://www.firstprinciples.ventures/insights/the-experimentation-flywheel-growing-ab-testing-momentum-at-scale#:~:text=Ground%20Support%3A). This layered approach ensures top-down buy-in and bottom-up expertise. For instance, Microsoft and others found that moving experienced experimenters into new teams or leadership roles can rapidly spread the testing culture and accelerate adoption[\[36\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=1,stages%20of%20A%2FB%20testing%20growth)[\[37\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=another%20team%20which%20runs%20%E2%80%9Csimilar,Such). Having multiple champions also prevents the program from stalling if one person leaves; the culture of experimentation becomes self-reinforcing across departments.

### Phase 3 – Scaling Up & Standardization (Run)

By Phase 3, the organization has multiple experiments running in parallel across different areas, and the focus shifts to **scaling efficiently and maintaining quality**. Here, the program usually formalizes further – possibly establishing a **Center of Excellence (CoE)** for experimentation or a full-fledged experimentation team if it hasn’t already. The practices that were ad hoc in earlier phases become institutionalized. For instance, the company might develop a centralized **experimentation platform** (or fully roll out a third-party platform across all products and channels) to handle randomization, exposure logging, and automated analysis of experiments[\[38\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=4,g). The platform ensures consistency (everyone uses the same definitions and methods) and frees teams from reinventing the wheel each time. Microsoft’s Experimentation Platform (ExP) is an example of an at-scale system with robust features, but even a mid-size company at Phase 3 will have some unified tooling or integrations in place for experimentation.

One key aspect of scaling is focusing on **data quality and measurement rigor**. Phase 3 organizations invest heavily in metrics and analytics infrastructure. They define overarching success metrics often called **Overall Evaluation Criteria (OEC)** – essentially the North-Star metrics that experiments are judged by (e.g., for an e-commerce site it might be overall revenue per user, for a bank it might be new accounts opened or customer lifetime value)[\[39\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=2,of%20OEC%20and%20Data%20Quality)[\[40\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=to%20shift%20to%20connecting%20test,and%20Diagnostic%20metrics%20should%20be). They also establish **guardrail metrics** to monitor unintended consequences (for example, tracking if an experiment that boosts conversion also causes an increase in customer support calls or unsubscribe rates)[\[41\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=3,We%E2%80%99ve%20extensively)[\[42\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=valuable%20learnings%20with%20executives%2C%20etc,for%20manual%20intervention%20allows%20for). The aim is to have a comprehensive view of experiment impact: not just “did metric X go up?” but also “did we hurt anything else, and does this likely translate to long-term value?” Developing a richer set of metrics – often classified as primary metrics, secondary metrics, and guardrails – helps Phase 3 organizations avoid local optimizations that harm the bigger picture[\[39\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=2,of%20OEC%20and%20Data%20Quality)[\[43\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=the%20changes%20being%20tested,focus%20areas%20within%20the%20product). For instance, a marketing experiment might show increased click-throughs (primary metric up), but careful analysis might reveal it also increased unsubscribe rates (guardrail metric down); with that insight, the team might decide not to roll out the “winning” variant broadly because it has a hidden cost.

During Phase 3, **knowledge sharing and training** become more systematic. The company likely sets up formal training programs or documentation so that new employees can learn the experimentation framework. It’s common to have an internal knowledge base or archive of past experiment results – an “experimentation library” – where anyone can look up what was tried before and what the outcome was[\[44\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=,Embed%20experiment%20experts%20into%20teams)[\[45\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=,review%20process). Booking.com, for example, is famous for its internal system where every experiment is logged and searchable, ensuring the organization’s learning accumulates and is not lost. Continuous education is also stressed: companies might run internal workshops, bootcamps, or lunch-and-learns on designing good experiments, avoiding pitfalls, and interpreting results. One veteran of Booking.com noted, *“At some point, I was training people every week... because every week, we were hiring new developers and designers. That was the biggest effort – to keep the culture intact and help it grow and flourish across the organisation.”*[\[46\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=Jonas%20emphasises%20the%20importance%20of,com%E2%80%99s%20experimentation%20culture). This underlines that as you scale, maintaining a strong culture requires persistent onboarding of newcomers into the experimentation mindset. The CoE (or central team) often takes on this role of being evangelists and trainers, as well as advisors on complex experiments.

Another development in Phase 3 is dealing with more **complex experiment designs** and tackling **edge cases**. Whereas earlier phases stuck to simple A/B tests (one change at a time), now the company might venture into multi-variant tests, multi-armed bandit approaches, or sequential testing methods for more efficiency. They might also have to manage many experiments running concurrently. This raises questions about interactions – *“If we run 5 experiments on the website at once, will they interfere with each other?”* Initially, this can cause concern, but by Phase 3 many organizations have enough data and experience to realize that **simultaneous tests usually don’t conflict much** if set up properly[\[47\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=One%20of%20the%20biggest%20misconceptions,more%20common%20than%20they%20are)[\[48\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=1,handful%20of%20interactions%20were%20detected). As ABsmartly’s CEO noted, “people are usually so afraid of running many experiments simultaneously, but interactions almost never happen. It’s not a problem to run dozens — even hundreds — of experiments at the same time, as long as you avoid testing on the same element”[\[49\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=Jonas%20advises%20teams%20to%20take,a%20pragmatic%20approach). So Phase 3 is when companies develop guidelines for parallel testing (e.g., maybe each team gets a slice of traffic or certain segments, or use experimental design techniques to handle overlaps). Advanced monitoring can be put in place to detect any rare interactions (some platforms provide automatic interaction checks[\[48\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=1,handful%20of%20interactions%20were%20detected)[\[50\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=3,part%20of%20a%20multivariate%20test)), but in general the mindset shifts from caution to **high-velocity testing**. This dramatically increases the organization’s learning rate.

By Phase 3, experimentation is also tied more directly into **strategic decision-making**. Test results are not just for tactical tweaks, but feed into product roadmaps, marketing strategy, and resource allocation. For example, if experiments consistently show a particular product feature doesn’t improve user engagement, the company might decide to sunset that feature and invest elsewhere. If marketing experiments reveal certain customer segments respond poorly to a tactic, strategy might shift to focus on segments that do respond or to develop new approaches for the non-responders. Many Phase 3 companies adopt a practice of requiring an “experiment or data” backing for major decisions – e.g., before a big new initiative is rolled out, they’ll run a pilot experiment to validate the idea. The decision-making cadence starts to revolve around experimental evidence.

### Phase 4 – Operational Excellence & Experimentation Culture (Fly)

In the final phase, experimentation is deeply ingrained in the organization’s DNA. Companies at this level (think Amazon, Netflix, or top-tier financial firms that have embraced analytics) run experiments “everywhere and on *everything*” in pursuit of continuous improvement[\[51\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=Phase%204%20,year%203)[\[52\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=,experiment%20results%20to%20prove%20causality). Marketing, product, customer experience, operations – all can benefit from controlled experiments, and the organization has the capability to design and execute tests across these domains. In Phase 4, the program achieves a state of **operational excellence**, where experimentation is no longer something special – it’s business as usual.

Operational excellence means the processes and systems for experimentation are highly automated, efficient, and trusted. Setting up an experiment might be as easy as filling out an online form or toggling a feature flag. The experimentation platform by now likely has sophisticated features: automatic ramp-ups, real-time analytics dashboards, alerts for statistically significant impacts or metric guardrail violations, etc.[\[53\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=5,support%20costs%20and%20increase%20the)[\[54\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=testing%20at%20scale,the%20trustworthiness%20of%20decisions%20making). Many companies in Phase 4 even integrate experimentation into their deployment or campaign workflows – for example, any new email template or any new feature code might go out as a test by default. The notion of a “launch” changes to a notion of a “test-first rollout.” Some organizations call this being in “perpetual beta,” meaning everything new is tested on a subset before scaling.

At the cultural level, Phase 4 companies foster an environment where **data trumps hierarchy**. Decisions without experimental evidence are the exception, and even then usually temporary until a test can be run. Leadership not only expects to see test results backing proposals, but *demands* it for major initiatives. Importantly, they also empower teams to run experiments proactively – there is a sense of trust and psychological safety that allows for experimentation. Failures are viewed as learning, not something to hide. This is critical: if people fear punishment for a failed test, they’ll avoid testing bold ideas. Phase 4 cultures celebrate the learning even when an idea fails. Amazon famously has principles around being “right a lot” by being willing to test and be wrong. Capital One’s founder Rich Fairbank once noted that their many tests meant many ideas failed, but those failures were simply steps toward finding the winners – that ethos is what experimentation culture is about.

One hallmark of reaching “Fly” stage is that the organization can’t imagine operating without experimentation. It has become **integral to the decision-making process**. The company has confidence in its experimentation system and trusts the data it produces, so decisions large and small are funneled through that lens. For example, in a Phase 4 financial services firm, if marketing wants to change the pricing on a loan product or add a new fee, they will automatically plan a randomized trial (perhaps testing it in one region vs. another) to gauge impact before rollout. If the UX team wants to redesign the mobile app, they might do so gradually via A/B tests on portions of traffic rather than a “big bang” release. Experimentation is embedded into the product development and marketing release cycle, not a separate process or afterthought[\[55\]](https://www.firstprinciples.ventures/insights/the-experimentation-flywheel-growing-ab-testing-momentum-at-scale#:~:text=Embed%20experimentation%20to%20existing%20product,development%20processes)[\[56\]](https://www.firstprinciples.ventures/insights/the-experimentation-flywheel-growing-ab-testing-momentum-at-scale#:~:text=across%20the%20organisation). One executive described it like breathing: the teams ideate → test → learn → ideate, in a continuous loop.

The organization by now also measures what it does in terms of experimentation program success. They might track metrics like “experiments per month,” “percentage of decisions validated by experiments,” or the aggregate uplift delivered by experiments in a year. This becomes part of leadership dashboards. It’s not experimentation for experimentation’s sake, though – it’s done to tie back to business value. For instance, Phase 4 organizations can often say “In the past year, our experiments collectively drove a 5% increase in revenue” (because they measure the incremental gains)[\[9\]](https://www.measured.com/faq/holdout-test/#:~:text=After%20the%20campaign%2C%20Measured%20analyzed,the%20majority%20of%20incremental%20revenue)[\[57\]](https://www.measured.com/faq/holdout-test/#:~:text=that%20the%20catalog%20was%20delivering,the%20majority%20of%20incremental%20revenue). They may even incorporate experiment results into long-term business cases and forecasts.

Companies at this stage even run **“reverse” or negative experiments**, deliberately removing or withholding a feature for a subset of users to confirm its importance[\[51\]](http://www.howtoexperiment.com/#:~:text=Phase%204%20,year%203). This guards against the accumulation of legacy features that everyone assumes are useful but have never been properly validated. In other words, a mature experimentation culture not only tests new ideas but also continuously tests its past implementations to ensure they still deliver value. Vanguard and similar firms could, for instance, turn off a long-standing email alert for a small group of customers as a negative experiment, checking whether its absence affects engagement. If it doesn’t, that might indicate the feature was not as valuable as presumed (or that customer needs have changed). Such practices exemplify an organization that leaves no stone unturned – everything is subject to empirical evaluation. For example, some companies assign a tiny percentage of users to a long-term **global holdout group**, excluding them from all new features or marketing treatments, to objectively measure the cumulative impact of all experiments over time[\[58\]](https://www.geteppo.com/blog/holdouts-measuring-experiment-impact-accurately#:~:text=Not%20all%20experimentation%20programs%20have,against%20the%20additional%20overhead%20incurred)[\[59\]](https://www.geteppo.com/blog/holdouts-measuring-experiment-impact-accurately#:~:text=The%20first%20hard%20requirement%20is,holdouts.%20Unless%20you). This kind of across-the-board holdout offers a final layer of validation that the sum of individual test wins is actually driving the expected business lift. Such techniques underscore how far an organization can go in pursuit of trustworthy insights once experimentation is deeply ingrained.

By Phase 4, the organization likely also ties experimentation to **long-term customer value and objectives**. Short-term experiment metrics are great, but the company will have mechanisms (like longitudinal tracking or successive holdouts) to ensure that what wins in the short term also pans out in the long term. If a winning variant ever backfires later (e.g., causes higher churn in six months), the culture is to catch that through follow-up analysis or long-term holdouts and correct course[\[60\]](https://www.geteppo.com/blog/holdouts-measuring-experiment-impact-accurately#:~:text=The%20first%20use%20case%20for,impact%20of%20a%20team%E2%80%99s%20work)[\[61\]](https://www.geteppo.com/blog/holdouts-measuring-experiment-impact-accurately#:~:text=A%20second%20use%20case%20for,octane%20pace%20of%20regular%20experiments). Continuous refinement is the norm.

It’s important to realize that very few organizations reach this ultimate stage in full, and that’s okay. The aim of describing it is aspirational – to show what is possible when practical sequencing pays off. The journey from holdouts to a culture of experimentation can take several years. Microsoft’s experimentation leaders noted that it requires iterative investment and there’s “no easy path” – you have to go through the turns of the flywheel repeatedly to scale up[\[5\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWalk%E2%80%9D%20milestones%2C%20to%20the%20%E2%80%9CRun%E2%80%9D,by%20using%20a%20Fly%20wheel). Each phase builds on the previous, and parts of the company may be in different phases at a given time. For instance, the digital marketing team could be in Phase 4 while another department like branch operations is just entering Phase 2; part of the program’s job is to elevate all areas over time.

## Common Early Mistakes and Pitfalls

As organizations embark on this journey, they inevitably encounter pitfalls. Recognizing common early mistakes can help teams avoid or mitigate them. Here are some frequent errors in the early stages of building an experimentation program, and why they can stall progress:

* **Choosing the Wrong First Experiment:** The outcome of initial tests can *make or break* momentum[\[62\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=1,test%20in%20which%20improvements%20were). One mistake is picking a first experiment that is overly trivial (so even if it “wins,” it doesn’t impress anyone) or, conversely, too ambitious and complex (and then it fails or yields inconclusive data). For example, testing a minor webpage color change or a tiny wording tweak as your very first test might not produce a noticeable result or meaningful insight – it won’t excite stakeholders. On the other hand, attempting a complicated multivariate test that spans multiple teams as your first effort can lead to delays, execution problems, or confusion in analysis. The Microsoft experimentation team advises selecting initial A/B tests that have *high potential impact*, are *simple to execute*, and *easy to measure*[\[63\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=,website%20and%20mobile%20app%20or)[\[64\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=rule%20of%20thumb%2C%20we%20recommend,may%20introduce%20data%20quality%20issues). Skipping those criteria is a pitfall. If the first experiment doesn’t produce a clear, credible insight, executives might sour on the whole idea of testing. **Avoid this by choosing an impactful, manageable test as your opener** – something related to a key business metric, but contained enough that you can execute it with confidence. A good first test might be in a single channel or on a single feature, with one control and one treatment, to maximize clarity[\[14\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=,split%2050%2F50%20to%20maximize%20sensitivity).

* **Overcomplicating Experimentation Processes:** In early phases, some teams inadvertently make experimentation cumbersome. This includes overly manual setups for every test, over-engineering the analysis (using complex statistics when not needed), or requiring excessive approvals for each experiment. Over-engineering slows everything down[\[65\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=productivity%2C%20or%20processes%20but%20sometimes,slow%2C%20cumbersome%2C%20and%20ultimately%20less)[\[66\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=setups%20for%20every%20experiment%2C%20like,ultimately%20less%20appealing%20to%20teams). For instance, a team might insist on designing multi-factor experiments with dozens of combinations before they’ve even run a simple A/B, or they might require a lengthy review process where every test plan goes through multiple committee approvals. These things can stall the program. Complexity also **discourages adoption** – if running experiments is too complicated, teams will avoid the process[\[67\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=Complexity%20also%20discourages%20adoption,creative%20solutions%20to%20potential%20problems)[\[68\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=workflows%2C%20allowing%20teams%20to%20set,monitor%20tests%20with%20minimal%20friction). As ABsmartly’s CEO put it, *“If experimentation is not easy, if it is not part of the culture, people will try to bypass it completely. It should be super simple to set up a test.”*[\[69\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=Jonas%20Alves%2C%20CEO%20of%20ABsmartly%2C,importance%20of%20making%20experimentation%20effortless) The remedy is to streamline: **simplify workflows and automate where possible**. In Phase 2, for example, the program might introduce templates or simple tools so that setting up a standard A/B test takes minutes, not weeks. Avoid burdensome steps that don’t add value. Especially in the beginning, aim for a “minimum viable process” – just enough structure to ensure tests are sound, but not so much that it creates friction.

* **Lack of Statistical Rigor and Power:** Another early mistake is not paying attention to experiment design fundamentals like sample size and statistical significance. Teams new to testing might stop an experiment too early as soon as they see a trend they like (the classic **“peeking”** problem), or run tests on too few users and then declare winners that are actually just noise. This leads to false positives and reversals that can erode trust in experimentation. One study found that many A/B testing users make critical mistakes, with error rates as high as 30% due to things like peeking and misanalysis[\[70\]](https://www.capitalone.com/tech/software-engineering/using-experimentation-for-sound-decision-making/#:~:text=generated%20from%20well,need%20is%20an%20Experimentation%20Playbook). Additionally, underpowered tests (too small a sample or too short a duration to detect a realistic effect) are very common and yield inconclusive results, which frustrates stakeholders who expected clear answers. An experimentation program can stall if several tests in a row come back “inconclusive” because they weren’t designed with enough power. The fix is to educate teams on basics: use sample size calculators, run tests for the full duration determined (don’t cut it short at the first sign of significance), and employ proper statistical methods. For example, running an A/A test (identical variants) at the outset can help validate that your testing tool isn’t biased – you should see no significant difference if things are working right[\[28\]](https://www.convert.com/blog/a-b-testing/ab-testing-mistakes/#:~:text=,if%20the%20tool%20works)[\[29\]](https://www.convert.com/blog/a-b-testing/ab-testing-mistakes/#:~:text=Why%3F). If an early test result seems too good to be true, double-check for a **Sample Ratio Mismatch** (did each variant actually get equal exposure?)[\[71\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=Data%20integrity%20is%20crucial%20for,to%20underreporting%20or%20technical%20issues) – this is a common technical issue that can invalidate results. In short, establish a habit of rigor: plan tests with adequate power[\[72\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=bling%20experimentation), avoid peeking (or use methods to adjust for it if you must look)[\[73\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=5,data%20to%20mitigate%20premature%20conclusions)[\[74\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=Statsig%20provide%20inflated%20confidence%20intervals,data%20to%20mitigate%20premature%20conclusions), and ensure randomization is working. It’s better to have a few trustworthy results than many dubious ones.

* **Ignoring Data Quality and Integrity:** Early experimenters might not realize how crucial clean data is for drawing valid conclusions. Common issues include instrumentation bugs (events or conversions not tracked uniformly for test vs. control) or sample ratio mismatches (due to a bug, your 50/50 split ends up 60/40)[\[75\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=1,to%20detect%20sample%20ratio%20mismatches)[\[76\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=Tyler%20pointed%20out%20a%20common,to%20underreporting%20or%20technical%20issues). If decisions are made on faulty data, the program’s credibility takes a hit. For example, if a dashboard shows Variant B is winning but later you discover half of Variant A’s events weren’t recorded due to an analytics bug, the team (and executives) will lose confidence in the process. To avoid this, implement data checks in every experiment. **Verify the sample split** is as expected (many platforms provide an SRM check – use it, or do a chi-square test manually)[\[75\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=1,to%20detect%20sample%20ratio%20mismatches). Monitor metrics for oddities during the test – for instance, if both variants’ conversion rates drop to zero on a certain day, that signals a tracking outage. It’s wise to run a smoke test or dry run of the experiment setup (like a week of A/A test) to ensure the instrumentation and assignment logic are solid[\[27\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=fundamental%20components%20for%20running%20a,ups%20and%20new). Essentially, the experimentation team should act as data quality guardians. By catching and fixing data issues early, you prevent a scenario where stakeholders start saying “We can’t trust these experiment results.”

* **Not Having a Clear Hypothesis or Success Criteria:** Running an experiment without a well-defined hypothesis is like shooting arrows in the dark – even if you hit something, you won’t know what it means. A common pitfall is launching a test with a vague idea like “let’s see what happens,” with no specific hypothesis or success metric. This can lead to ambiguous interpretations or “p-hacking” (finding some random metric that moved and calling the test a win). Before running a test, teams should articulate: *what change are we testing, what do we* *predict* *will happen, and what metric will determine success?* Without this clarity, it’s easy for confirmation bias to creep in (people see what they want in the data) or for the team to argue about results after the fact. A good hypothesis is **Constrained, Actionable, Testable, and Specific (CATS)**, as one Capital One playbook put it[\[77\]](https://www.capitalone.com/tech/software-engineering/using-experimentation-for-sound-decision-making/#:~:text=follows%20CATS%20). For example: “Adding a personalized greeting in the email will increase click-through rate by at least 1% because it makes the message feel more relevant.” That hypothesis names the change, the expected direction and magnitude, the metric (CTR), and the reasoning. If the test results come in, everyone can clearly see if the hypothesis held or not. The mistake is skipping this step. The cure is to require a brief hypothesis write-up for each test – it forces thought upfront and aligns the team on what constitutes a win or loss.

* **Using Vanity Metrics Instead of Business Metrics:** Early experiment designs can go astray if the team chooses metrics that don’t correlate with real business value. It’s easy to measure things like email open rates or website clicks, but a higher click rate is meaningless if those clicks don’t convert into sales or retention. Focusing on the wrong metric can give a false sense of success and lead decision-makers to question the program’s impact. For example, a test might show a 20% lift in email opens – a “win” on paper – but if revenue per customer doesn’t budge, executives may rightfully ask, “So what?” Ensure you pick **meaningful KPIs** for experiments (ideally tied to customer value or profit) and not just vanity metrics[\[78\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=3,driven%20KPIs). One guideline is to link test metrics back to primary business outcomes: clicks should lead to conversions, lifts in engagement should translate to retention or revenue, etc. By aligning experiments with metrics that matter, you prevent the program from getting stuck optimizing superficial numbers while true performance stalls[\[79\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=formulation%20and%20continuous%20learning%20from,experimentation).

* **Overlooking Segment Differences in Results:** Sometimes an experiment’s average outcome hides important differences among customer segments. A test might appear to have no effect overall, but that could mask a positive impact for one group and a negative impact for another. If teams don’t examine results by relevant segments or customer attributes, they risk drawing the wrong conclusion. For example, in one marketing experiment a publisher tested offering deep discounts versus shallow discounts in catalogs. The aggregate sales difference between the test and control was negligible, suggesting the discounts didn’t matter. However, further analysis revealed that **long-time customers who had recently paid full price were alienated by the deep discounts** – those customers reduced their future spending by 18% after seeing newer buyers get huge markdowns[\[80\]](https://cebma.org/assets/Uploads/Anderson-2011.pdf#:~:text=initial%20results%20may%20be%20deceiving,priced%20item)[\[81\]](https://cebma.org/assets/Uploads/Anderson-2011.pdf#:~:text=Further%20analysis%20revealed%20a%20disturbing,catalog%20with%20shallow%20discounts%20spent). The lesson is to dig into the data: look at subsets (new vs. loyal customers, high-value vs. low-value, etc.) to ensure a “win” isn’t hurting a key segment or that a “no result” isn’t actually a mix of plus and minus effects that cancel out. Especially in financial services, different client segments can respond very differently to offers, so segmentation analysis is crucial in early experiments.

* **Siloed Efforts and Lack of Knowledge Sharing:** In the “walk” phase (Phase 2), a common misstep is different teams running experiments in isolation without sharing methods or findings. This can lead to duplicate work (wasting time testing something another team already tried) and inconsistent methods (one team might be calculating a metric differently than another). Siloes also slow cultural adoption – if one team discovers something great but nobody else hears about it, the impact is localized. A fragmented culture leads to an inconsistent approach where some teams embrace experimentation and others ignore it[\[82\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=A%20poor%20experimentation%20culture%20tends,an%20organisation%E2%80%99s%20experimentation%20best%20practices)[\[83\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=hires%20compounds%20the%20problem%2C%20which,an%20organisation%E2%80%99s%20experimentation%20best%20practices). Over time, lack of knowledge sharing means mistakes are repeated and wins aren’t leveraged company-wide. The solution is to **centralize knowledge and encourage cross-pollination**. Set up an internal forum or channel where experiment results are posted. Consider a periodic meeting or newsletter to highlight interesting experiments across the business. The ABsmartly blog emphasizes training and constant knowledge sharing – at Booking.com, they had new employees training every week on experimentation and used dashboards so everyone could see the impact of tests[\[46\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=Jonas%20emphasises%20the%20importance%20of,com%E2%80%99s%20experimentation%20culture)[\[84\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=,why%20and%20how%20of%20experimentation). By Phase 3, many companies implement an experimentation repository or dashboard accessible to all[\[84\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=,why%20and%20how%20of%20experimentation)[\[85\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=across%20the%20organisation,impact%20business%20metrics%20over%20time). This ensures that when one team learns something (e.g., a certain email subject works best), other teams can apply that learning instead of starting from scratch. It also helps build a sense of community – teams feel like they’re all contributing to a collective learning effort, rather than competing silos.

* **Fear and Conservatism – Not Testing Boldly Enough:** Paradoxically, another mistake is being too cautious with experimentation. This includes not testing at all when one should (due to fear of risk), or testing only very safe, small variations that don’t challenge any assumptions. For example, a team might avoid holding out any customers from a campaign because they fear missing out on possible sales – thus they never truly measure the campaign’s necessity. Or they run only trivial UI tests (like button colors) and declare victory on tiny uplifts, while avoiding deeper questions like “Do customers even want this product feature?” or “Is our targeting strategy right?” Playing it too safe can cause the program to stall out on low-impact optimizations, which might make experimentation seem not worth the effort. One root cause of this conservatism is often **cultural resistance or fear of failure**. If the company’s culture punishes failure or if managers are unwilling to risk a negative result (like losing some sales from a holdout), they will shy away from experiments that have uncertain outcomes – unfortunately, those are often the ones most worth doing. To combat this, leadership should explicitly promote a “test and learn” mentality, where *not testing* is seen as riskier than testing. Highlight that an experiment that shows an idea *didn’t* work is still valuable because it prevented wasted investment. Encourage teams to test bold, strategic questions (in a controlled way) rather than only small cosmetic changes. Also, educate that running experiments properly (e.g., on a subset) *limits risk* – you’re never going to implode the business with a 10% holdout, but you stand to gain valuable knowledge. In summary, **don’t let fear paralyze the program**. Early on, pick at least one or two tests that really matter, not just low-stakes ones, to show that the methodology can guide strategic decisions. This will earn much more respect internally than a dozen trivial tests.

* **Over-Reliance on One Team or Person:** In some companies, the experimentation initiative starts with one superstar analyst or a single team spearheading it. That’s great to get going, but a mistake is not building redundancy and breadth as the program grows. If only one person knows how to run tests and interpret results, you have a single point of failure. What if they leave the company or get pulled to another project? The momentum could grind to a halt. Similarly, if experimentation is siloed in, say, the digital analytics team and no one else is involved, it can become a bottleneck – other teams will say “we have to wait for analytics to run that test for us,” and if analytics is swamped, fewer tests happen. To avoid this, **democratize the experimentation skills**. Train multiple people (across different teams) in the tools and methods. Encourage a model where each team might have a “testing champion” so the load isn’t on one group. ABsmartly notes that relying too heavily on a single team to create and run experiments can create bottlenecks and make experimentation slow and cumbersome[\[66\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=setups%20for%20every%20experiment%2C%20like,ultimately%20less%20appealing%20to%20teams)[\[86\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=them%20long%20delays%20before%20they,ultimately%20less%20appealing%20to%20teams). The best way to scale is to distribute capability. This is why Phase 2 and 3 involve education and embedding experts in teams – to propagate the expertise. Also, establishing good documentation and self-service tools means teams don’t have to go through one person for every test. By Phase 3, the organization should aim for experimentation to be self-serve for most straightforward tests, with the central team only consulting on the more complex or risky ones. That way, the departure of any one individual won’t derail the overall program.

* **Miscommunicating Results or Overhyping Findings:** How results are communicated is critical. A mistake is to present experiment data without proper context or to overclaim what the results mean. For example, announcing “Test B increased conversions by 50%\!” without clarifying that it was from 2% to 3% (a relative 50% which might sound more impressive than the absolute lift) can mislead stakeholders. Or not explaining statistical confidence might lead others to think a random fluctuation is a real win. This can set unrealistic expectations and lead to disappointment later (“Last time you said 50% improvement, but our sales barely moved\!”). Conversely, being too timid or jargon-heavy in communicating can also lose your audience. The key is to **communicate insights accurately and clearly**. Include uncertainty where appropriate (“we’re 95% confident this increase is real, margin of error ±1%”) and relate the result to business implications (“this could translate to \\$X additional revenue per quarter”). Another common miscommunication is to declare a test a “win” based on a metric that isn’t the true goal (see vanity metrics above). That can cause misalignment – maybe the CEO cares about revenue, but the experiment team celebrates an increase in clicks that didn’t lead to revenue. Align on what success means *before* communicating it out. Furthermore, be careful not to attribute causality beyond what was tested. If you tested subject lines, don’t overgeneralize the result to “email marketing is solved.” Miscommunication, especially early on, can hurt credibility. Executives and others are watching these initial experiments closely. If one is oversold and then doesn’t hold up (perhaps it regresses in a follow-up test or the effect fades), skepticism will grow. So it’s better to be honest and nuanced: e.g., “Version B beat Version A by 5%. That’s a modest lift and we’ll verify it with another test or over a longer period. Still, it’s directionally encouraging and we’ll implement it for now.” Such transparency actually builds trust – you’re seen as rigorous and not jumping to conclusions. In short, **speak the language of the business** (connect to KPIs, dollars, customers) and maintain integrity in reporting. That way, as the program grows, stakeholders know they can rely on the experiment team for truthful, useful insights.

Avoiding these pitfalls greatly increases the likelihood that the experimentation initiative will gain traction and mature. Of course, mistakes will still happen – they are part of the learning curve. The key is to treat the building of an experimentation program *itself* as an iterative experiment: try approaches, measure what’s working (e.g., number of teams adopting testing, speed of test cycles, quality of insights), learn from mistakes, and adapt the strategy. In doing so, you’ll navigate around these common roadblocks and keep the organization’s confidence in the journey.

## Conclusion

The journey from basic holdout-based measurement to a full-fledged experimentation program is a transformative one for any organization. It’s a journey that blends data-driven technique with organizational change management. We began with a simple act – holding out a control group to measure incremental impact – and we end with a vision of a culture where experimentation informs decisions at every level. Each step in between is critical. By starting small with the right first experiments, demonstrating value through early wins, and consciously scaling processes and culture, companies create a **flywheel of experimentation** where each success breeds further investment and interest[\[18\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=3,make%20an%20A%2FB%20testing%20program)[\[87\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=4,conditions%20for%20streamlining%20the%20A%2FB).

For a financial services firm like Vanguard, the implications are profound. Marketing analytics can move from an era of gut-driven campaigns and retroactive ROI reports to a proactive mode where campaigns are test-driven and optimized *in-market*. Instead of debating opinions, teams set up experiments to get answers. Over time, this rigor spreads beyond marketing – influencing product development, customer experience, and even strategy. It’s the difference between guessing what customers want versus continually **learning from them** through controlled trials.

To conclude, here are a few practical tips gleaned from this journey:

* Secure leadership **buy-in** early by delivering a clear win or insight from an initial pilot test[\[62\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=1,test%20in%20which%20improvements%20were).

* Start with **simple, high-impact experiments** (e.g. one control vs one treatment on an important feature) to ensure quick, believable results[\[14\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=,split%2050%2F50%20to%20maximize%20sensitivity).

* **Reduce friction** in running tests – make it easy to set up experiments and analyze data – so teams are willing to do it (if testing is hard, people will skip it)[\[67\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=Complexity%20also%20discourages%20adoption,creative%20solutions%20to%20potential%20problems)[\[33\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=infrastructure%20and%20data%20quality%20create,to%20try%20running%20A%2FB%20tests).

* **Share learnings** widely and celebrate both wins and informative failures. This builds a supportive culture and spreads best practices across teams[\[18\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=3,make%20an%20A%2FB%20testing%20program)[\[84\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=,why%20and%20how%20of%20experimentation).

* Invest in **data quality and metrics**. Trustworthy, relevant metrics (and checks like A/A tests or SRM) are crucial to maintain credibility and make correct decisions[\[71\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=Data%20integrity%20is%20crucial%20for,to%20underreporting%20or%20technical%20issues)[\[79\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=formulation%20and%20continuous%20learning%20from,experimentation).

In financial services, where customer behavior and market conditions can change quickly, developing a strong experimentation muscle now will position the organization to respond swiftly and intelligently to future challenges.

Ultimately, evolving into an experimentation-driven organization is a **competitive advantage**. It means the company can adapt faster, because it’s constantly probing the market for what works and why. It also means the company can avoid costly missteps by vetting ideas on a small scale before full rollout. In the fast-changing landscape of marketing (and finance), that agility and discipline in decision-making is what separates the leaders from the laggards. As we’ve detailed, the journey from basic holdout tests to a mature experimentation program is not trivial – but it is absolutely achievable with a practical, step-by-step approach. By learning from common early mistakes, leveraging early victories to gain buy-in, and steadily investing in people and tools, any organization can progressively cultivate a robust culture of experimentation. And once that culture takes hold, the organization is on a self-sustaining path of continuous improvement – a true **test-and-learn enterprise** that can thrive on data-driven insights in every aspect of its marketing and beyond.

The journey is challenging, but the message from pioneers is clear: **start small, learn fast, and never stop experimenting**.

**Practical Tips to Evolve an Experimentation Program:**

* Secure leadership **buy-in** early by delivering a clear win or insight from an initial pilot test[\[62\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=1,test%20in%20which%20improvements%20were).

* Start with **simple, high-impact experiments** (e.g. one control vs one treatment on an important feature) to ensure quick, believable results[\[14\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=,split%2050%2F50%20to%20maximize%20sensitivity).

* **Reduce friction** in running tests – make it easy to set up experiments and analyze data – so teams are willing to do it (if testing is hard, people will skip it)[\[67\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=Complexity%20also%20discourages%20adoption,creative%20solutions%20to%20potential%20problems)[\[33\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=infrastructure%20and%20data%20quality%20create,to%20try%20running%20A%2FB%20tests).

* **Share learnings** widely and celebrate both wins and informative failures. This builds a supportive culture and spreads best practices across teams[\[18\]](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/articles/it-takes-a-flywheel-to-fly-kickstarting-and-keeping-the-a-b-testing-momentum/#:~:text=3,make%20an%20A%2FB%20testing%20program)[\[84\]](https://absmartly.com/blog/the-top-3-experimentation-mistakes-businesses-make#:~:text=,why%20and%20how%20of%20experimentation).

* Invest in **data quality and metrics**. Trustworthy, relevant metrics (and checks like A/A tests or SRM) are crucial to maintain credibility and make correct decisions[\[71\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=Data%20integrity%20is%20crucial%20for,to%20underreporting%20or%20technical%20issues)[\[79\]](https://www.statsig.com/blog/top-8-common-experimentation-mistakes-how-to-fix#:~:text=formulation%20and%20continuous%20learning%20from,experimentation).

In financial services, where customer behavior and market conditions can change quickly, developing a strong experimentation muscle now will position the organization to respond swiftly and intelligently to future challenges.

The journey is challenging, but the message from pioneers is clear: **start small, learn fast, and never stop experimenting**.
