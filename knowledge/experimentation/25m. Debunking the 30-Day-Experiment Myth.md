## Executive Summary

The 30-day experiment duration has become a deeply entrenched convention in the world of online controlled experiments. This report provides a comprehensive critical analysis of this norm, examining its historical origins, evaluating when it is justified versus unnecessary, and presenting advanced methodologies for shortening decision cycles. We introduce a practical decision framework based on signal velocity and risk that empowers organizations to move beyond the one-size-fits-all approach. The analysis demonstrates that while the 30-day duration may have made sense in an era of limited data and computing power, modern statistical methods and business imperatives demand a more nuanced and agile approach to experiment duration.

## Introduction

In the fast-paced world of digital product development and marketing, online controlled experiments, or A/B tests, have become the gold standard for data-driven decision-making. From minor UI tweaks to major feature rollouts, organizations across industries rely on experimentation to validate hypotheses, mitigate risk, and optimize for user engagement and business growth. The rise of experimentation as a core organizational capability has been one of the defining trends of the digital age. Companies like Amazon, Google, Facebook, and Netflix have built their competitive advantage in part on their ability to run thousands of experiments per year, continuously testing and iterating on every aspect of their products.

Yet, for all the sophistication that goes into designing and analyzing these experiments, a surprisingly simplistic and often unexamined convention has taken hold: the 30-day experiment duration. Walk into most product organizations today, and you will find that experiments are routinely scheduled to run for exactly 30 days, regardless of the traffic volume, the expected effect size, or the business context. This report provides a critical analysis of this 30-day norm, exploring its historical and organizational origins, evaluating its appropriateness in various contexts, and presenting a modern framework for determining experiment duration based on signal velocity and risk.

While the 30-day duration can be a reasonable default in some scenarios, it is by no means a universal optimum. In many cases, it is a suboptimal choice that can lead to significant opportunity costs, either by delaying the rollout of beneficial changes or by prolonging the exposure of users to detrimental ones. The opportunity cost of a delayed decision can be substantial. For a winning feature, every day of delay is a day of lost revenue, engagement, and customer value. Consider a high-traffic e-commerce site that runs an experiment on a new checkout flow. If the new flow increases conversion rate by 2%, and the site processes $1 million in revenue per day, then every day of delay in rolling out the winning feature costs the company $20,000. Over a month, that adds up to $600,000 in lost revenue. For a losing feature, every day of continued exposure can lead to user frustration, churn, and brand damage. In a competitive landscape where speed is a key differentiator, the ability to make faster, more agile decisions is a critical capability.

This report will demonstrate that a more nuanced and data-informed approach to experiment duration is not only possible but essential for any organization that seeks to maximize its experimentation velocity and maintain a competitive edge. We will delve into advanced statistical methodologies that can dramatically shorten decision cycles, including sequential testing, variance reduction techniques like CUPED (Controlled-experiment Using Pre-Experiment Data), and the use of leading indicators and surrogate metrics. Finally, we will synthesize these concepts into a practical decision framework that empowers experimenters to choose the right duration for the right reasons, moving beyond the one-size-fits-all 30-day dogma.

## The Historical and Organizational Origins of the 30-Day Norm

The prevalence of the 30-day experiment duration is not a coincidence, nor is it the result of a rigorous statistical calculation. Rather, it is a product of historical inertia, organizational convenience, and a deeply ingrained human desire for rhythm and predictability. To understand why so many organizations default to a 30-day cycle, we must look beyond the realm of statistics and into the history of business practices and the psychology of corporate culture.

### The Rhythm of the Business: Monthly and Quarterly Cadences

The modern business world operates on a calendar-based rhythm. Financial reporting, sales quotas, marketing campaigns, and strategic planning are all typically organized around monthly and quarterly cycles. This cadence has its roots in pre-digital-era accounting and management practices, where the logistical challenges of data collection and analysis necessitated a periodic, batched approach. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, when most business transactions were recorded by hand in ledgers, it was simply impractical to produce financial statements more frequently than once a month. The monthly report became the standard unit of business communication, providing a regular snapshot of performance and a forum for decision-making.

The concept of the fiscal year, which can be divided into quarters and months, has been a cornerstone of business for centuries. This regular, predictable cycle allows for consistent tracking of financial health, comparison of performance over time, and alignment of goals and incentives across the organization. It is a system that brings order and predictability to the often-chaotic world of commerce. The quarterly earnings report, in particular, has become a defining feature of public company life, driving short-term decision-making and creating a powerful incentive to show consistent, predictable growth.

This monthly rhythm has a powerful influence on how organizations think about time and progress. When it comes to experimentation, aligning with the existing monthly reporting cycle offers a number of perceived benefits. First, there is **synchronization**: running experiments for a full month ensures that the results are available in time for monthly business reviews. This simplifies the process of communicating findings and securing buy-in from stakeholders. It allows for a clean narrative: "This month, we ran these experiments, and here are the results." Second, there is **predictability**: a fixed 30-day duration makes it easier to plan and resource experimentation programs. Teams know when experiments will start and end, allowing for better coordination and allocation of engineering and data science resources. This predictability is comforting in a world of uncertainty. Third, there is **simplicity**: a one-size-fits-all approach is easy to communicate and enforce. It eliminates the need for complex calculations and debates about the appropriate duration for each individual experiment. It becomes a simple rule of thumb that everyone can follow, regardless of their statistical expertise.

However, this convenience comes at a cost. The monthly business cycle is an arbitrary construct that has little to do with the underlying dynamics of user behavior or the statistical requirements of a given experiment. By forcing experiments to conform to this pre-ordained schedule, organizations often sacrifice speed and agility for the sake of administrative tidiness. The rhythm of the business, in this case, can become a cage that limits the potential of experimentation. The tail is wagging the dog: the needs of the reporting calendar are driving the design of the experiments, rather than the other way around.

### The Quest for a Representative Sample: Capturing Weekly Cycles

Another major driver of longer experiment durations is the desire to capture a full range of user behavior. It is a well-established fact that user activity on most websites and apps varies significantly depending on the day of the week. For example, a B2B software product might see peak usage during weekdays, with a significant drop-off on weekends when employees are not at work. Conversely, a consumer-facing e-commerce site might experience a surge in traffic over the weekend, when people have more leisure time to browse and shop. Similarly, a food delivery app might see different patterns on weekdays (lunch and dinner orders from office workers) versus weekends (brunch and family dinner orders).

To obtain a representative sample, it is crucial to run an experiment for at least one full week, and ideally for multiple weeks, to average out these weekly fluctuations. This is a valid and important consideration. Running an experiment for only a few days can lead to skewed results if, for example, a promotional email is sent out on a Tuesday, driving an unusual spike in traffic from a particular user segment. Or if the experiment happens to start on a holiday weekend, when traffic patterns are atypical. By extending the experiment duration to two, three, or four weeks, experimenters aim to smooth out these variations and get a more accurate picture of the true treatment effect.

The 30-day duration, which conveniently encompasses four full weekly cycles plus a few extra days, is often seen as a safe and conservative choice that provides a robust buffer against the vagaries of weekly seasonality. It is a simple way to ensure that the experiment has captured a full range of user behavior, from the Monday morning rush to the Saturday night shopping spree. However, as we will see later, there are more sophisticated ways to handle seasonality that do not require such a lengthy and inflexible duration. For example, one could use statistical models to explicitly account for day-of-week effects, treating day-of-week as a covariate in the analysis. Or one could use a shorter duration and then extrapolate the results to a full week, using historical data to estimate the day-of-week effects. The 30-day duration is a brute-force solution to a problem that can often be solved with more finesse.

### The Psychology of Risk Aversion and the Fear of False Positives

Organizational culture plays a significant role in shaping experimentation practices. In many companies, there is a strong aversion to making wrong decisions, which translates into a deep-seated fear of false positives (Type I errors). A false positive occurs when an experiment shows a statistically significant effect that does not actually exist, leading the organization to roll out a change that has no real benefit or may even be harmful. This fear is not entirely irrational. The cost of a false positive can be high, both in terms of wasted engineering resources (spent building and maintaining a feature that doesn't actually work) and potential damage to the user experience (if the change actually makes things worse).

As a result, many organizations have adopted a very conservative stance towards experimentation, prioritizing the avoidance of false positives above all else. This often manifests as a preference for long experiment durations, as a larger sample size is seen as a way to increase confidence in the results and reduce the risk of being fooled by randomness. The thinking goes: if we run the experiment for a full 30 days, we can be really sure that the effect is real.

The practice of "peeking" at experiment results before they have reached their predetermined sample size is a major contributor to an inflated false positive rate in the traditional, frequentist A/B testing framework. Every time an experimenter checks the results, they are essentially performing another statistical test, and the more tests they perform, the higher the chance of finding a spurious significant result. This is a well-known problem in statistics, sometimes called the "multiple comparisons problem" or the "optional stopping problem." To combat this, many organizations have adopted a strict policy of not looking at the results until the experiment has run its full course. A fixed, long duration like 30 days serves as a simple and effective commitment device to prevent peeking and maintain statistical rigor.

This approach, while well-intentioned, is often overly conservative. It treats all experiments as equally risky and fails to account for the fact that some changes are likely to have much larger and more obvious effects than others. Furthermore, it ignores the opportunity cost of not stopping an experiment early when a clear winner or loser has emerged. The concept of "loss aversion," first identified by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in their groundbreaking work on behavioral economics, is highly relevant here. People tend to feel the pain of a loss more acutely than the pleasure of an equivalent gain. In the context of experimentation, this can lead to an excessive focus on avoiding false positives (the "loss" of making a wrong decision) at the expense of maximizing the potential for true positives (the "gain" of finding a winning feature). This bias towards caution can lead to a culture of analysis paralysis, where the fear of making a mistake prevents the organization from taking calculated risks and moving quickly.

As we will discuss in a later section, modern statistical methods like sequential testing provide a way to peek at results safely, without inflating the false positive rate, thus rendering the strict, long-duration approach obsolete.

### The Influence of Agile Development Cycles

The rise of agile software development methodologies has also, paradoxically, contributed to the entrenchment of fixed-duration experiments. Many agile teams operate on two-week sprint cycles, during which they plan, build, and ship new features. The agile manifesto, published in 2001, emphasized iterative development, continuous feedback, and rapid response to change. Sprints are a way of breaking down large projects into manageable chunks and creating a regular rhythm of delivery and reflection.

It is a common practice to launch an experiment at the beginning of a sprint and let it run for two full sprints (i.e., four weeks, or roughly 30 days) before making a decision. This aligns the experimentation timeline with the development cadence, but it is an arbitrary alignment that, once again, prioritizes organizational convenience over statistical efficiency. The logic is: we build a feature in one sprint, we test it in the next two sprints, and then we decide what to do with it in the fourth sprint. This creates a neat, predictable cadence, but it is a cadence that is driven by the needs of the development team, not by the needs of the experiment itself.

The agile philosophy is all about rapid iteration and continuous feedback. In theory, experimentation should be a natural fit for this approach. However, the rigid, fixed-duration experiment can actually be at odds with the agile mindset. Instead of getting feedback as quickly as possible, the team is forced to wait for an arbitrary deadline. A more truly agile approach would be to use a more flexible and adaptive approach to experiment duration, where the length of the experiment is determined by the data, not by the sprint calendar. This would allow teams to move even faster, stopping experiments as soon as they have enough information to make a decision, rather than waiting for the sprint to end.

In summary, the 30-day experiment duration is a cultural artifact, a product of historical business practices, a defense mechanism against statistical pitfalls, and a convenient, if suboptimal, solution to the challenges of planning and coordination. It is a dogma that has been adopted for reasons of simplicity and safety, but it is a dogma that is ripe for re-examination in the light of modern statistical theory and the ever-increasing demand for speed and agility in the digital marketplace.

## When Long Durations Are Justified vs. Unnecessary

While the 30-day norm is often a suboptimal default, there are certainly situations where a long experiment duration is not only justified but necessary. Conversely, there are many more situations where such a long duration is a wasteful and unnecessary impediment to rapid learning and iteration. The key is to understand the specific characteristics of the experiment and the business context in which it is being run. In this section, we will explore both sides of this equation, providing concrete examples and guidelines for when to use long durations and when to avoid them.

### When Long Durations Are Justified

**1. Long Purchase Cycles and Delayed Conversions**

For businesses with long and considered purchase cycles, such as high-end e-commerce, real estate, automotive, B2B software, or financial services, it is essential to run experiments long enough to capture the full customer journey. A user might be exposed to a new feature in the first week of an experiment but not make a purchase until several weeks later. This is especially true for high-value, high-consideration purchases, where customers typically spend a significant amount of time researching, comparing options, and deliberating before making a final decision.

A short experiment would fail to attribute this conversion to the treatment, leading to an inaccurate and likely underestimated treatment effect. In such cases, running an experiment for 30, 60, or even 90 days may be necessary to get a true reading of the impact on the bottom line. For example, a company selling luxury watches might find that its customers take an average of 45 days from their first visit to the website to making a purchase. An experiment that runs for only 14 days would miss a large proportion of these conversions and would likely conclude that the new feature had no effect, even if it was actually a major driver of sales. Similarly, a B2B software company selling an enterprise product with a six-month sales cycle would need to run experiments for many months to see the full impact on closed deals.

**2. Novelty and Learning Effects**

When a new feature or a significant redesign is introduced, users' initial reactions may not be indicative of their long-term behavior. This is known as the novelty effect (if the initial reaction is positive) or the learning effect (if there is an initial dip in engagement as users adapt to the new interface). For example, a flashy new design might initially attract a lot of clicks, but this effect may wear off as the novelty fades and users return to their habitual patterns. Conversely, a major change to the user interface might initially confuse and frustrate users, leading to a drop in engagement, but over time, as they learn the new layout and understand its benefits, their engagement might rebound and even surpass the previous baseline.

To distinguish these temporary effects from the true, long-term impact of the change, it is necessary to run the experiment long enough for user behavior to stabilize. This can often take several weeks, making a 30-day duration a reasonable choice. The key is to look at the "days since exposure" view of the experiment results, and to see if the treatment effect is converging to a stable value, or if it is still changing significantly over time. For example, when Facebook introduced its algorithmic news feed (replacing the chronological EdgeRank feed), there was an initial backlash from users who were used to the old system and felt that they were losing control over what they saw. However, over time, users adapted to the new system and engagement levels eventually surpassed their previous levels. A short experiment would have incorrectly concluded that the algorithmic feed was a failure, when in fact it was a major long-term success.

**3. Low Traffic and Small Effect Sizes**

The required sample size for an experiment is a function of several factors: the baseline conversion rate, the minimum detectable effect (MDE), the desired statistical power (typically 80%), and the significance level (typically 5%). The formula for sample size calculation shows that the required sample size increases dramatically as the effect size decreases. For websites with low traffic or for experiments where the goal is to detect a very small effect size, it can take a long time to accumulate the necessary number of users and conversions to reach a statistically significant result.

In these scenarios, a 30-day duration may be the only way to achieve the required statistical power. For a small startup with only a few thousand visitors a month, it might take a full 30 days to have enough data to confidently detect a 5% lift in conversion rate. In this case, the long duration is a necessary evil. However, it is always worth questioning whether it is worth spending a month to detect a tiny effect, or whether the team's time would be better spent on ideas with a potentially larger impact. There is an opportunity cost to running experiments, and it may be better to focus on the big bets rather than the small optimizations.

**4. Seasonal Effects and Special Events**

Some businesses are highly seasonal, with significant variations in traffic and user behavior depending on the time of year. For example, a retail website might see a huge spike in traffic during the holiday shopping season (November and December), while a tax preparation software company might see peak usage in the spring (March and April). If an experiment is run during a period of unusual activity, it may be necessary to extend the duration to ensure that the results are representative of normal conditions.

Similarly, if there are special events or external factors that might influence user behavior during the experiment period (such as a major marketing campaign, a competitor's product launch, or a news event), it may be wise to run the experiment for a longer period to average out these effects. The goal is to isolate the true effect of the treatment from the noise of external factors.

### When Long Durations Are Unnecessary

**1. High Traffic and Large Effect Sizes**

For high-traffic websites and apps, it is often possible to reach statistical significance in a matter of days, or even hours, especially when the treatment has a large effect. In these situations, continuing to run the experiment for the full 30 days is a waste of time and resources. Every day that a winning feature is not rolled out to all users is a day of lost value. Conversely, every day that a losing feature is exposed to users is a day of potential harm to the user experience and the business.

For a company like Amazon or Netflix, with hundreds of millions of active users, an experiment with a 1% impact on a key metric can be detected with high confidence in a very short amount of time, due to their massive user base. Running such an experiment for 30 days would be a colossal waste of opportunity. The company would be leaving money on the table (if the feature is a winner) or damaging the user experience (if the feature is a loser) for no good reason.

**2. The Goal is to Detect a "Bug" or a Major Outage**

Sometimes, the purpose of an experiment is not to optimize a metric but to ensure that a new feature does not have a catastrophic negative impact. In these cases, a very short experiment may be sufficient. If a new feature is causing a significant drop in a key guardrail metric, such as page load time, error rate, or crash rate, this will often be apparent very quickly. There is no need to wait 30 days to confirm that something is broken. A simple "canary test" where the new feature is exposed to a small percentage of users (say, 1% or 5%) for a few hours can be enough to catch major problems before they affect the entire user base. This is a common practice in software engineering, where new code is gradually rolled out to production, with careful monitoring at each stage to catch any issues early.

**3. The Business Context Demands Speed**

In a highly competitive market, speed of execution can be a critical advantage. If a competitor has just launched a new feature, a company may need to respond quickly with its own version. In this context, waiting 30 days to get a perfect reading on the impact of the new feature may be a luxury that the company cannot afford. It may be better to accept a higher level of uncertainty and make a decision based on a shorter experiment, especially if the potential upside is large and the downside risk is low.

In the world of social media, for example, trends can come and go in a matter of days. A company that takes 30 days to test a new feature related to a current trend will have missed the boat entirely. The trend will have passed, and the feature will be irrelevant. In these cases, it is better to move fast and iterate quickly, even if it means making some decisions with less-than-perfect data.

**4. Iterative Testing and Rapid Prototyping**

In the early stages of product development, when a team is still exploring the problem space and trying out different solutions, it may be more valuable to run a large number of short experiments rather than a small number of long experiments. The goal in this phase is to learn quickly and to fail fast, not to achieve statistical perfection. A series of one-week experiments can provide valuable directional insights and help the team converge on a promising solution, even if each individual experiment is not powered to detect a small effect size. This is the philosophy of "lean startup" and "rapid prototyping": build, measure, learn, and iterate as quickly as possible.

## Shortening the Decision Cycle: Advanced Methodologies

The 30-day dogma is largely a product of the limitations of the traditional, frequentist approach to A/B testing. However, in recent years, a number of advanced statistical methodologies have emerged that allow for more flexible and efficient experimentation. These methods make it possible to shorten the decision cycle without sacrificing statistical rigor. In this section, we will explore three of the most powerful of these methodologies: sequential testing, variance reduction, and the use of leading indicators and surrogate metrics.

### Sequential Testing: Peeking with Confidence

As we discussed earlier, one of the main reasons for the adoption of long, fixed-duration experiments is to prevent the practice of "peeking" at the results, which inflates the false positive rate. Sequential testing provides a direct solution to this problem. It is a statistical method that is specifically designed to be monitored continuously, allowing experimenters to stop the experiment as soon as a statistically significant result is reached, without invalidating the results.

Sequential testing has its roots in the work of Abraham Wald during World War II. Wald, a brilliant statistician working for the U.S. military, developed the sequential probability ratio test (SPRT) as a way to make decisions about the quality of munitions with as few tests as possible. The goal was to determine whether a batch of ammunition met quality standards without having to test every single round, which would be prohibitively expensive and time-consuming. Wald showed that by using a sequential approach, where the decision to accept or reject the batch was made after each test, it was possible to reach a conclusion much faster than with a fixed-sample-size approach. The same principles can be applied to online experiments, where the goal is to make a decision about a new feature with as few users as possible.

There are several different approaches to sequential testing, but they all share the same basic principle: the threshold for statistical significance is adjusted over time to account for the fact that the data is being checked repeatedly. Early in the experiment, a very large effect is needed to declare a winner. As more data is collected, the threshold becomes less conservative. This allows for experiments with large effects to be stopped very quickly, while still providing the necessary statistical rigor for experiments with smaller effects.

One of the most popular and powerful approaches to sequential testing is the use of "always valid p-values" and confidence intervals. These are statistical constructs that, as their name suggests, are valid at any point in time, regardless of how many times the data has been peeked at. This gives experimenters the freedom to monitor their experiments in real-time and make decisions as soon as the evidence is strong enough. The key insight is that by using a different statistical framework (based on martingales and optional stopping theorems), it is possible to construct p-values and confidence intervals that do not suffer from the multiple comparisons problem.

The benefits of sequential testing are enormous. It can dramatically reduce the average duration of experiments, allowing for a much higher experimentation velocity. Studies have shown that sequential testing can reduce the average experiment duration by 30-50% compared to fixed-horizon testing, without any loss of statistical rigor. This enables a more agile and responsive approach to product development, where ideas can be tested and iterated on much more quickly. And it reduces the opportunity cost of running experiments, by ensuring that winning features are rolled out as soon as possible and losing features are shut down before they can do too much damage.

However, sequential testing is not a silver bullet. It is more complex to implement than traditional, fixed-horizon testing, and it requires a sophisticated experimentation platform. It is also important to note that while sequential testing allows for early stopping, it does not guarantee it. If the effect size is small, a sequential test may still need to run for a long time to reach a conclusion. Nevertheless, for any organization that is serious about experimentation, sequential testing is an essential tool for accelerating the pace of learning and innovation.

### Variance Reduction: Sharpening the Signal

Another way to shorten the duration of an experiment is to reduce the variance of the metric being measured. The more "noisy" a metric is, the larger the sample size needed to detect a given effect. By reducing the noise, we can increase the statistical power of the experiment and reach significance with a smaller sample size, and therefore in a shorter amount of time.

One of the most effective techniques for variance reduction is **Controlled-experiment Using Pre-Experiment Data (CUPED)**. The basic idea behind CUPED is to use data about users from before the experiment to predict their behavior during the experiment. By subtracting out the predictable component of user behavior, we can isolate the true treatment effect and reduce the overall variance of the metric.

For example, if we are running an experiment to see if a new feature increases user engagement, we can use each user's pre-experiment engagement level as a control variate. Users who were highly engaged before the experiment are likely to be highly engaged during the experiment, regardless of whether they are in the treatment or control group. By accounting for this pre-existing difference, we can get a much clearer signal of the true impact of the new feature. The CUPED estimator adjusts each user's observed outcome by subtracting a prediction based on their pre-experiment behavior, and then calculates the treatment effect on these adjusted outcomes.

CUPED has been shown to be extremely effective in practice, with some companies reporting variance reductions of over 50%. This can translate into a dramatic reduction in the required sample size and, therefore, the required duration of the experiment. For example, if CUPED reduces the variance by 50%, then the required sample size is cut in half, and the experiment can be run in half the time. It is a powerful tool that should be in the arsenal of every experimenter. The technique was first published by researchers at Microsoft in 2013, and it has since been adopted by many of the leading tech companies.

Another variance reduction technique is **stratification**. This involves dividing the user population into a number of subgroups, or strata, based on some pre-experiment characteristic, such as country, device type, or user tenure. The treatment effect is then estimated separately for each stratum, and the overall effect is calculated as a weighted average of the stratum-level effects. By controlling for the variation between strata, we can reduce the overall variance of the treatment effect estimate. For example, if we know that mobile users and desktop users have very different engagement levels, we can stratify by device type and estimate the treatment effect separately for each group. This will give us a more precise estimate of the overall treatment effect.

Both CUPED and stratification are powerful techniques for reducing variance and shortening experiment duration. However, they both require access to pre-experiment data and a certain level of statistical sophistication to implement correctly. They are not a substitute for good experiment design, but they can be a valuable supplement.

### Leading Indicators and Surrogate Metrics: Predicting the Future

Often, the metrics that we care about the most are slow-moving and difficult to measure in the short term. For example, we might be interested in the impact of a new feature on long-term user retention (e.g., 90-day retention or one-year retention) or customer lifetime value (LTV). Measuring these metrics directly would require running an experiment for many months, or even years, which is clearly impractical. By the time we have the results, the market may have moved on, and the feature may no longer be relevant.

This is where leading indicators and surrogate metrics come in. A **leading indicator** is a short-term metric that is correlated with a long-term outcome. A **surrogate metric** is a predictive model that uses a combination of short-term signals to estimate a long-term outcome. By using these proxies, we can get an early read on the likely long-term impact of a change, without having to wait for the long-term outcome to materialize.

For example, if we are interested in long-term retention, we might use short-term engagement metrics, such as the number of sessions per user in the first week, the number of key actions taken in the first week, or the time spent in the app in the first week, as leading indicators. If a new feature leads to a significant increase in these engagement metrics, it is a good sign that it will also lead to an increase in long-term retention. The logic is that users who are more engaged early on are more likely to stick around in the long run.

A surrogate metric is a more sophisticated version of this idea. It is a machine learning model that is trained on historical data to predict a long-term outcome based on a set of short-term inputs. For example, we could build a model that predicts a user's 90-day retention based on their activity in the first seven days. The model might use features such as the number of sessions, the number of key actions, the time spent in the app, the types of content consumed, and so on. We can then use this model to estimate the long-term retention impact of a new feature based on a much shorter experiment. If the new feature increases the predicted 90-day retention (as estimated by the model) by 5%, we can be reasonably confident that it will also increase the actual 90-day retention by a similar amount.

The use of leading indicators and surrogate metrics is a powerful way to shorten the decision cycle for experiments that are aimed at influencing long-term outcomes. However, it is important to be cautious when using these proxies. The correlation between the short-term metric and the long-term outcome must be strong and stable over time. If the correlation is weak, then the surrogate metric will not be a reliable predictor, and decisions based on it may be wrong. It is also important to periodically validate the surrogate model against the true long-term outcome to ensure that it is still accurate. User behavior can change over time, and a model that was accurate a year ago may no longer be accurate today.

Despite these challenges, the use of surrogate metrics is becoming increasingly popular at data-mature companies like Netflix, Airbnb, and Microsoft, where they are seen as a key enabler of rapid, long-term-focused innovation. These companies have invested heavily in building and validating surrogate models, and they use them routinely to make faster decisions about features that are intended to have long-term impacts.

## A Decision Framework for Experiment Duration

Given the many factors that can influence the optimal duration of an experiment, it is clear that a one-size-fits-all approach is not sufficient. What is needed is a flexible and data-informed decision framework that can help experimenters choose the right duration for the right reasons. This framework should be based on two key dimensions: **signal velocity** and **risk**.

**Signal velocity** refers to how quickly we can expect to get a clear and reliable signal from the experiment. This is a function of several factors:

*   **Traffic volume**: Higher traffic means more data per unit time, which means faster convergence to a statistically significant result.
*   **Baseline conversion rate**: Higher conversion rates mean more events per user, which also means faster convergence.
*   **Expected effect size**: Larger effects are easier to detect and require smaller sample sizes.
*   **Variance of the metric**: Lower variance means less noise and faster convergence.

High signal velocity means that we can expect to reach statistical significance quickly, while low signal velocity means that it will take a longer time.

**Risk** refers to the potential downside of making a wrong decision. This includes both the risk of a false positive (rolling out a bad feature) and the risk of a false negative (failing to roll out a good feature). The level of risk is a function of several factors:

*   **Importance of the metric**: Changes that affect core business metrics (such as revenue or retention) are higher risk than changes that affect secondary metrics (such as clicks or page views).
*   **Cost of the change**: Changes that are expensive to build or maintain are higher risk than changes that are cheap and easy to reverse.
*   **Reversibility**: Changes that are easy to roll back are lower risk than changes that are difficult or impossible to reverse.
*   **Overall business context**: In a highly competitive market, the risk of moving too slowly may be greater than the risk of making a wrong decision.

By plotting these two dimensions on a 2x2 matrix, we can create a simple but powerful framework for thinking about experiment duration:

| | **Low Risk** | **High Risk** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High Signal Velocity** | **Short Duration (e.g., 1-7 days)** | **Sequential Testing** |
| **Low Signal Velocity** | **Fixed Duration (e.g., 14-28 days)** | **Long Duration with Surrogates** |

Let's explore each of these quadrants in more detail:

### Quadrant 1: High Signal Velocity, Low Risk

This is the quadrant of quick wins and low-stakes optimizations. Examples include:

*   Testing a new headline on a high-traffic landing page
*   Changing the color or text of a call-to-action button
*   Tweaking the copy on a product page
*   Adjusting the layout of a search results page

In these cases, the effect is likely to be large and easy to detect (because the traffic is high and the change is prominent), and the downside of getting it wrong is minimal (because the change is easy to reverse and does not affect core business metrics). A short, fixed-duration experiment of 1-7 days is often sufficient. The goal here is to maximize the number of experiments and to iterate quickly. These are the "bread and butter" experiments that make up the bulk of most experimentation programs.

### Quadrant 2: High Signal Velocity, High Risk

This is the quadrant of high-stakes changes with potentially large and immediate effects. Examples include:

*   A major change to the checkout flow or payment process
*   A new pricing model or discount strategy
*   A significant redesign of the homepage or main navigation
*   Removing or adding a key feature

In these cases, it is crucial to get a quick and accurate reading on the impact of the change. Sequential testing is the ideal methodology for this quadrant, as it allows for continuous monitoring and early stopping as soon as a clear winner or loser emerges. The benefit of sequential testing is that it provides both speed and rigor: if the change has a large positive effect, it can be rolled out quickly; if it has a large negative effect, it can be shut down quickly; and if the effect is small or ambiguous, the experiment can continue until there is enough data to make a confident decision.

### Quadrant 3: Low Signal Velocity, Low Risk

This is the quadrant of small, incremental improvements. Examples include:

*   Minor UI tweaks on a low-traffic page
*   Changes to the copy on a rarely-seen error message
*   Optimizations to a feature that is used by a small subset of users
*   Testing a new recommendation algorithm on a small segment

In these cases, it will take a longer time to reach statistical significance (because the traffic is low or the effect is small), but the stakes are low (because the change does not affect core metrics and is easy to reverse). A fixed-duration experiment of 14-28 days is a reasonable choice, as it provides enough time to collect the necessary data without tying up resources for too long. It is important to have a programmatic approach to these experiments, so that they can be run in the background without consuming too much attention. For example, a company could have a dedicated "optimization" track where a continuous stream of these small experiments are run, with minimal oversight.

### Quadrant 4: Low Signal Velocity, High Risk

This is the quadrant of strategic bets and long-term investments. Examples include:

*   A major redesign of the product or user experience
*   A new feature that is intended to drive long-term retention or engagement
*   A change to the business model or monetization strategy
*   Entering a new market or launching a new product line

In these cases, it is essential to get a high-quality reading on the impact of the change, even if it takes a long time. A long-duration experiment of 30 days or more is often necessary. This is also the quadrant where the use of surrogate metrics is most valuable, as they can provide an early indication of the likely long-term impact of the change. For example, if we are testing a new onboarding flow that is intended to improve long-term retention, we might use a surrogate metric that predicts 90-day retention based on first-week engagement. This allows us to get a read on the likely long-term impact after just one or two weeks, rather than waiting three months for the actual retention data.

This framework is not intended to be a rigid set of rules, but rather a guide to help experimenters think more critically about the appropriate duration for their experiments. The specific duration chosen will always depend on the unique characteristics of the experiment and the business context. However, by thinking in terms of signal velocity and risk, experimenters can move beyond the 30-day dogma and make more informed and strategic decisions about how to invest their experimentation resources.

## The Role of Bayesian Statistics

So far, our discussion has been largely focused on the frequentist approach to statistics, which is the dominant paradigm in A/B testing. However, the Bayesian approach offers a fundamentally different way of thinking about probability and inference, and it has important implications for experiment duration.

In the frequentist world, probability is defined as the long-run frequency of an event. A p-value is the probability of observing a result as extreme as the one you saw, assuming that the null hypothesis is true. This is a somewhat convoluted and counter-intuitive way of thinking, and it is the reason why practices like peeking are so problematic. The p-value is only valid if the sample size is fixed in advance, and if you peek at the data and decide to stop early, you are violating this assumption and inflating the false positive rate.

In the Bayesian world, probability is defined as a degree of belief. A Bayesian analysis starts with a "prior" belief about the probability of something (for example, the probability that the treatment is better than the control), and then it updates that belief based on the data to produce a "posterior" belief. The result of a Bayesian A/B test is not a p-value, but a probability distribution for the difference between the treatment and the control. This distribution tells you the probability that the treatment is better than the control, and by how much.

This has a profound implication for experiment duration: in a Bayesian framework, there is no need to pre-specify a sample size or a duration. You can monitor the experiment continuously and stop whenever you are confident enough in the result. The concept of "peeking" is not a problem for Bayesians, because the posterior distribution is always a valid representation of your current state of knowledge. Every time you look at the data, you are simply updating your beliefs based on the new information.

This does not mean that you can just stop whenever you see a result that you like. That would be a form of confirmation bias, and it would lead to poor decisions. Rather, it means that you can define a stopping rule based on your desired level of confidence. For example, you could decide to stop the experiment as soon as there is a 95% probability that the treatment is better than the control. Or you could define a more sophisticated stopping rule based on the expected loss of making a wrong decision. The expected loss is the average cost of making a wrong decision, weighted by the probability of each outcome. By minimizing the expected loss, you can make optimal decisions under uncertainty.

The Bayesian approach is more flexible and intuitive than the frequentist approach, and it is a natural fit for the agile and iterative nature of product development. However, it is also more computationally intensive and requires a different way of thinking. For these reasons, it has been slower to be adopted in the industry. However, as more and more companies build sophisticated experimentation platforms, we are likely to see a gradual shift towards a more Bayesian way of thinking about A/B testing. Some companies, such as Booking.com and Optimizely, have already made this shift and are using Bayesian methods as their primary approach to experimentation.

## Building a Culture of Experimentation Velocity

Moving beyond the 30-day dogma is not just a matter of adopting new statistical methods. It also requires a fundamental shift in organizational culture. A culture of experimentation velocity is one that values speed, agility, and continuous learning over rigid processes and risk aversion. Building such a culture is a long-term effort that requires commitment from all levels of the organization.

### Leadership and Psychological Safety

Leadership plays a critical role in fostering a culture of experimentation velocity. Leaders must champion the importance of speed and agility, and they must create a psychologically safe environment where it is okay to fail. If experimenters are punished for inconclusive results or for making the "wrong" decision, they will quickly become risk-averse and will revert to the safety of long, conservative experiment durations. Leaders must celebrate learning, not just winning, and they must empower their teams to take calculated risks.

This requires a shift in mindset from "avoiding mistakes" to "maximizing learning." In a high-velocity experimentation culture, a "failed" experiment (one that does not produce a positive result) is not seen as a waste of time, but as a valuable source of information. It tells you what doesn't work, which is just as important as knowing what does work. As Thomas Edison famously said, "I have not failed. I've just found 10,000 ways that won't work."

### Education and Empowerment

To make informed decisions about experiment duration, experimenters need to have a solid understanding of the statistical principles involved. This does not mean that every product manager needs to be a PhD statistician, but it does mean that they need to have a basic literacy in concepts like statistical power, significance, effect size, and the trade-offs between speed and certainty. Organizations should invest in training and education to ensure that their teams have the skills they need to design and analyze experiments effectively.

They should also provide their teams with the tools and platforms they need to run experiments efficiently and to make use of advanced methodologies like sequential testing and CUPED. A good experimentation platform should abstract away much of the statistical complexity and provide clear, actionable guidance to experimenters. It should make it easy to set up experiments, monitor results in real-time, and make decisions based on the data.

### From Dogma to Dialogue

Ultimately, the goal is to move from a world where experiment duration is determined by dogma to a world where it is determined by dialogue. For each experiment, there should be a conversation between the product manager, the data scientist, and the engineer about the appropriate duration. This conversation should be guided by the decision framework outlined above, and it should take into account the specific goals, risks, and constraints of the experiment.

By making the choice of experiment duration a conscious and collaborative one, organizations can ensure that they are making the most of their experimentation resources and maximizing their pace of learning and innovation. This dialogue should happen at the beginning of the experiment, when the experiment is being designed, not at the end, when the results are being analyzed. By thinking carefully about the duration up front, teams can avoid the trap of running experiments for too long (and wasting time) or too short (and getting inconclusive results).

## Case Studies: Learning from Industry Leaders

To illustrate the principles discussed in this report, let's examine how some of the world's leading technology companies approach experiment duration. These case studies provide concrete examples of how organizations have moved beyond the 30-day dogma and adopted more sophisticated approaches to experimentation.

### Netflix: The Power of Sequential Testing

Netflix is widely regarded as one of the most sophisticated experimenters in the world. The company runs thousands of experiments per year, testing everything from the recommendation algorithm to the artwork for each title to the layout of the user interface. Netflix has been a pioneer in the use of sequential testing, which allows them to make decisions much faster than traditional fixed-horizon testing.

In a 2016 blog post, Netflix data scientists described their approach to sequential testing, which they call "always valid inference." The key insight is that by using a different statistical framework based on confidence sequences, they can monitor experiments continuously and stop them as soon as there is sufficient evidence to make a decision. This has allowed Netflix to dramatically reduce the average duration of their experiments, from weeks to days in many cases.

One of the challenges that Netflix faces is that many of their key metrics are long-term in nature. For example, they care deeply about long-term retention and customer lifetime value, which can take months or even years to fully materialize. To address this, Netflix has invested heavily in developing surrogate metrics that can predict long-term outcomes based on short-term signals. For example, they have built models that can predict a user's likelihood of churning in the next 90 days based on their viewing behavior in the first week after a change. This allows them to get an early read on the long-term impact of a feature without having to wait three months for the actual churn data.

### Booking.com: A Culture of Rapid Experimentation

Booking.com is another company that is famous for its experimentation culture. The company runs over 25,000 experiments per year, which works out to more than 68 experiments per day. This incredible velocity is made possible by a combination of factors: a strong commitment to experimentation at all levels of the organization, a sophisticated experimentation platform, and a willingness to make decisions quickly based on imperfect data.

Booking.com has adopted a pragmatic approach to experiment duration. For most experiments, they use a simple rule of thumb: run the experiment until you have enough data to make a decision, but no longer than necessary. They use a combination of fixed-horizon testing (for low-risk experiments) and sequential testing (for high-risk experiments). They also make extensive use of variance reduction techniques like CUPED to shorten experiment durations.

One of the key cultural factors that enables Booking.com's high experimentation velocity is a willingness to accept uncertainty. The company recognizes that not every decision needs to be made with 95% confidence. For low-risk experiments, they are willing to make decisions based on directional evidence, even if the results are not statistically significant. This allows them to move much faster than companies that insist on statistical perfection for every decision.

### Amazon: The One-Click Revolution

Amazon is perhaps the company that best embodies the principle of "bias for action" when it comes to experimentation. Jeff Bezos has famously said that "most decisions should probably be made with somewhere around 70% of the information you wish you had. If you wait for 90%, in most cases, you're probably being slow." This philosophy is reflected in Amazon's approach to experimentation, which emphasizes speed and iteration over perfection.

One of Amazon's most famous experiments was the test of the "one-click" checkout feature in the late 1990s. At the time, the standard checkout process required users to go through multiple pages, entering their shipping address, billing address, and payment information. Amazon hypothesized that a simplified, one-click checkout would increase conversion rates, especially for repeat customers.

The experiment was run for a relatively short period (less than two weeks), and the results were dramatic: the one-click feature increased conversion rates by a significant margin. Amazon quickly rolled out the feature to all users, and it became one of the company's most valuable innovations. The feature was so successful that Amazon patented it, and it became a key differentiator for the company for many years.

This example illustrates the power of moving quickly when you have a high-conviction idea and a high signal velocity. Amazon didn't wait 30 days to see if the effect would hold up. They saw a clear signal in less than two weeks, and they acted on it immediately. This bias for action has been a key driver of Amazon's success.

### Microsoft: Experimentation at Scale

Microsoft runs one of the largest experimentation programs in the world, with experiments running across all of its products, from Windows to Office to Xbox to Bing. The company has developed a sophisticated experimentation platform called ExP, which is used by thousands of engineers and product managers across the company.

Microsoft has been a leader in developing and promoting best practices for online experimentation. In particular, the company has done pioneering work on variance reduction techniques like CUPED, which was first developed by Microsoft researchers. Microsoft has also been a strong advocate for the use of guardrail metrics, which are metrics that are monitored to ensure that experiments do not have unintended negative consequences.

One of the challenges that Microsoft faces is that different products have very different characteristics in terms of traffic volume, user behavior, and business goals. A change to Bing, which has millions of users per day, can be tested much more quickly than a change to a niche enterprise product with only a few thousand users. Microsoft's approach is to provide a flexible experimentation platform that can accommodate these differences, rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all approach.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Even with the best intentions and the most sophisticated tools, experimentation programs can fall into a number of common pitfalls. In this section, we will discuss some of the most common mistakes and how to avoid them.

### Pitfall 1: Running Experiments for Too Long

This is the pitfall that this entire report has been focused on. Running experiments for too long is wasteful and slows down the pace of learning. The solution is to adopt a more flexible approach to experiment duration, using the decision framework outlined earlier in this report. Use sequential testing for high-risk experiments, and use short fixed durations for low-risk experiments with high signal velocity.

### Pitfall 2: Running Experiments for Too Short

While running experiments for too long is a common problem, running them for too short can be equally problematic. If you stop an experiment before you have enough data to make a confident decision, you risk making the wrong call. This is especially true for experiments with low signal velocity or high variance.

The solution is to always do a power analysis before launching an experiment. A power analysis tells you how many users you need to detect a given effect size with a given level of confidence. If the power analysis tells you that you need 100,000 users to detect a 1% lift with 80% power, and you only have 10,000 users per week, then you know that you need to run the experiment for at least 10 weeks. There is no way around this fundamental statistical constraint.

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring Novelty Effects

As we discussed earlier, novelty effects can be a major confound in experiments, especially for significant changes to the user interface. If you ignore novelty effects and make a decision based on the initial spike in engagement, you may be disappointed when the effect fades away over time.

The solution is to always look at the "days since exposure" view of your experiment results, and to be cautious about declaring victory too early. If you see a large initial effect that is declining over time, it is likely a novelty effect, and you should wait for the effect to stabilize before making a decision. Alternatively, you can explicitly exclude the first few days of data from your analysis, to remove the novelty effect.

### Pitfall 4: Not Using Variance Reduction

Variance reduction techniques like CUPED can dramatically shorten experiment durations, yet many organizations do not use them. This is often because they are not aware of these techniques, or because they do not have the technical expertise to implement them.

The solution is to invest in education and training for your data science team, and to build variance reduction into your experimentation platform. Once CUPED is implemented, it should be applied automatically to all experiments, so that experimenters can benefit from it without having to think about it.

### Pitfall 5: Optimizing for the Wrong Metric

Sometimes, organizations run experiments for the right duration and with the right methodology, but they are optimizing for the wrong metric. For example, they might optimize for clicks when they should be optimizing for revenue, or they might optimize for short-term engagement when they should be optimizing for long-term retention.

The solution is to always start with a clear understanding of the business goal, and to choose metrics that are aligned with that goal. Use leading indicators and surrogate metrics when necessary, but always validate them against the true long-term outcomes.

## The Future of Experimentation

As we look to the future, it is clear that experimentation will continue to play an increasingly important role in how organizations make decisions. The companies that can experiment faster and more effectively will have a significant competitive advantage. In this final section, we will explore some of the trends and technologies that are shaping the future of experimentation.

### Trend 1: Increased Automation

One of the major trends in experimentation is increased automation. As experimentation platforms become more sophisticated, they are able to automate more and more of the process, from experiment design to analysis to decision-making. For example, some platforms can now automatically calculate the optimal sample size for an experiment, automatically apply variance reduction techniques, and automatically stop experiments when a clear winner or loser emerges.

This automation has the potential to dramatically increase experimentation velocity, by reducing the amount of manual work required to run an experiment. It also has the potential to democratize experimentation, by making it accessible to people who do not have deep statistical expertise.

### Trend 2: Personalization and Heterogeneous Treatment Effects

Another major trend is the move towards personalization and the analysis of heterogeneous treatment effects. Traditional A/B testing assumes that the treatment effect is the same for all users. However, in reality, different users often respond differently to the same change. For example, a new feature might be very appealing to young users but off-putting to older users.

By analyzing heterogeneous treatment effects, organizations can identify which segments of users benefit most from a change, and they can personalize the experience accordingly. This requires more sophisticated statistical methods and more data, but it has the potential to unlock much greater value from experimentation.

### Trend 3: Causal Inference and Observational Studies

While randomized controlled experiments are the gold standard for causal inference, they are not always feasible or ethical. In some cases, it may be necessary to rely on observational data and causal inference techniques to estimate the impact of a change.

Recent advances in causal inference, such as propensity score matching, instrumental variables, and difference-in-differences, have made it possible to draw causal conclusions from observational data with a reasonable degree of confidence. These techniques are becoming increasingly important as organizations seek to understand the impact of changes that cannot be easily randomized.

### Trend 4: Multi-Armed Bandits and Reinforcement Learning

Traditional A/B testing is a form of "explore-exploit" trade-off: you explore different options (by running an experiment) and then exploit the best option (by rolling it out to all users). However, there are more sophisticated algorithms, such as multi-armed bandits and reinforcement learning, that can dynamically balance exploration and exploitation in real-time.

These algorithms can be especially useful in situations where the optimal decision changes over time, or where there are many different options to choose from. For example, a news website might use a multi-armed bandit algorithm to dynamically choose which headline to show to each user, based on real-time feedback about which headlines are getting the most clicks.

## Practical Recommendations

Based on the analysis in this report, we offer the following practical recommendations for organizations that want to move beyond the 30-day dogma and adopt a more sophisticated approach to experiment duration:

1. **Abandon the one-size-fits-all approach.** Recognize that different experiments have different requirements, and that the optimal duration depends on signal velocity and risk.

2. **Invest in sequential testing.** Sequential testing is one of the most powerful tools for shortening experiment durations. Make sure your experimentation platform supports it, and train your team on how to use it.

3. **Use variance reduction techniques.** Implement CUPED and other variance reduction techniques in your experimentation platform. These techniques can cut experiment durations in half or more.

4. **Develop surrogate metrics for long-term outcomes.** If you care about long-term metrics like retention or lifetime value, invest in building and validating surrogate metrics that can predict these outcomes based on short-term signals.

5. **Adopt a Bayesian mindset.** Consider using Bayesian methods, which are more flexible and intuitive than frequentist methods, and which allow for continuous monitoring without the peeking problem.

6. **Build a culture of experimentation velocity.** Foster a culture that values speed, agility, and continuous learning. Celebrate learning, not just winning, and create a psychologically safe environment where it is okay to fail.

7. **Educate your team.** Make sure that everyone involved in experimentation has a basic understanding of statistical principles and best practices. Provide training and resources to help them make informed decisions.

8. **Use the decision framework.** For each experiment, have a conversation about the appropriate duration, guided by the signal velocity and risk framework presented in this report.

9. **Monitor and iterate.** Continuously monitor the performance of your experimentation program, and be willing to iterate and improve. What works today may not work tomorrow, and you need to be willing to adapt.

10. **Challenge the dogma.** Don't be afraid to question conventional wisdom and to try new approaches. The 30-day dogma is just one example of a convention that has outlived its usefulness. There are likely many others.

## Conclusion

The 30-day experiment duration, while a convenient and widely adopted convention, is a relic of a bygone era. It is a one-size-fits-all solution that is ill-suited to the dynamic and fast-paced world of digital experimentation. By blindly adhering to this dogma, organizations risk slowing down their innovation cycles, missing out on valuable opportunities, and making suboptimal decisions.

This report has shown that a more nuanced and data-informed approach to experiment duration is not only possible but essential. By understanding the historical and organizational origins of the 30-day norm, we can begin to question its validity and explore more sophisticated alternatives. The 30-day duration is a product of monthly business cycles, the desire to capture weekly seasonality, the fear of false positives, and the convenience of aligning with agile development sprints. While these are all valid considerations, they should not be allowed to dictate the duration of every experiment.

By leveraging advanced statistical methodologies like sequential testing, variance reduction (CUPED), and surrogate metrics, we can dramatically shorten the decision cycle without sacrificing statistical rigor. Sequential testing allows us to peek at results safely and stop experiments early when a clear winner or loser emerges. Variance reduction techniques like CUPED can cut the required sample size in half or more, allowing us to reach conclusions in half the time. And surrogate metrics allow us to get an early read on long-term outcomes, without having to wait months or years for the actual data.

And by adopting a decision framework based on signal velocity and risk, we can choose the right duration for the right reasons, ensuring that our experimentation programs are both efficient and effective. High signal velocity and low risk experiments can be run in just a few days. High signal velocity and high risk experiments should use sequential testing. Low signal velocity and low risk experiments can run for 2-4 weeks. And low signal velocity and high risk experiments may need to run for 30 days or more, ideally with the use of surrogate metrics to get early feedback.

The future of experimentation belongs to those who are willing to challenge the old dogmas and embrace a more agile and intelligent approach. It is time to move beyond the 30-day rule and into a new era of faster, smarter, and more impactful experimentation. The companies that do this will be the ones that learn the fastest, innovate the most, and ultimately win in the marketplace. In a world where competitive advantage is increasingly driven by the ability to learn and adapt quickly, the ability to run experiments efficiently and to make fast, data-driven decisions is a critical capability. The 30-day dogma is a barrier to this capability, and it is time to leave it behind.
